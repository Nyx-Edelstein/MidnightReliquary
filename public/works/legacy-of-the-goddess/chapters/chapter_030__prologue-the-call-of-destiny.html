Ari stood guard outside the Council chamber, a few paces opposite another guardsman at the other side of the hall. It was mind-numbing work. At least with watch detail it was outside. Here, his only view was an open hall that led out to the castle antechamber.

It was a positively regal view, to be sure. A great chandelier hung midway down the hall. Along one side of the high walls were stained glass murals depicting the Holy Mother and the Celestial Triad; Avalia, Caelon, Vaka, and Nenore. Motes of dust flitted about in rays of light, seemingly cast from above by the Goddesses themselves.

The view got a little stale after the first three hours, though. As did his unprofessional compatriot’s attempts at conversation.

Ari’s thoughts drifted back to the expedition. It bothered him that he hadn’t been chosen. He understood why—only a dozen guardsmen were needed, and any veteran would be picked over a fresh recruit. Still, the disappointment lingered. His uncle had made it sound like he’d be going on adventures regularly. That was the main reason he’d agreed to this job in the first place. He couldn’t shake the feeling of being misled.

Time continued to crawl. To pass the hours, Ari amused himself by finding shapes in the sunbeams. If he focused just right, he could almost see the outline of something—someone?—moving through the motes of dust. A young girl, maybe. Her form shimmered like light caught in glass. The patterns shifted with a strange precision, as if she were dancing to some hidden geometry rather than music. He blinked, and the vision scattered back into meaningless sparkles.

A rising cacophony of voices indicated the Council meeting was at a close. Beside him, a door creaked open and Guard Captain Izu stepped through. Ari snapped to arms. The guard opposite him did not. Captain Izu saluted, and Ari returned to rest.

“New guy. How goes it?” Captain Izu asked, coming to stand next to him while he fastened his helmet. His tone was a bit more familiar than Ari was used to.

“Sir,” Ari said. “Well enough.”

Captain Izu shook his head with a chuckle. “We don’t really do formalities here. Except for show, of course—standard fare around nobles and royals. You’ll get used to it.”

Ari gave a slight nod, unsure how to respond. The guard across the hall muttered something under his breath—something about “green boys.” Izu shot him a sharp look, and Ari chose to let it pass.

“Arlinkar, isn’t it?”

“Just Ari, sir.”

“Alright then. Arisir it is.”

Ari blinked. “I meant just… ’Ari.’”

The guard across the hall stifled a laugh.

“Okay then: Ari,” Izu grinned. “Say, what’s this I hear about you beating Nira in a duel?”

Ari had been asked about that several times now. Apparently it was a bigger deal than he’d thought. He still wasn’t sure what had happened exactly, but that was battle for you. Sometimes instinct took over, and it was over before you knew it.

“I just got lucky, that’s all,” Ari said with a shrug.

Izu smiled, crossing his arms. “That so?”

Ari shifted uncomfortably. He could tell Izu had read something into his response—something he hadn’t meant to imply. Magical Luck was common enough in duels, but what he’d experienced with Nira had been… different. He couldn’t quite put his finger on it. Whatever it was, he didn’t want the Captain thinking he was some combat prodigy who’d woven mundane magic into swordplay or developed some masterful secret technique.

“Well, in case you haven’t heard, there’s a tourney coming up on Remembrance Day,” Izu said, clapping him on the shoulder. “You should sign up. It’d be nice to have some actual competition for a change.”

So much for that. Ari frowned. He didn’t like to show off—it went directly against the teachings of the Faith. Even if few held strictly to the teachings these days, he did. Although, there were exceptions for festivities and entertainment. And it would be far more interesting than anything else in his life right now…

Izu cleared his throat.

“I’ll think about it,” Ari said. “Thanks for the invite.”

Izu and the other guard snapped to arms as the doors opened. Ari followed suit. Izu took point beside a third guard exiting the chamber, and together the two of them escorted the Council members down the hall.

Ari and his compatriot waited until the chamber emptied before unfastening their helmets and heading out. They were off-duty now. The man gave him a curt nod and headed down the hall. Ari wasn’t sure where to go—maybe the guardsman’s quarters? Or the library? He still intended to look into the King’s—

“Ari, my boy!” he heard Uncle Raolin call from behind. “Over here!”

Surprised, Ari turned to see his uncle waving at him. He had been speaking with an older nobleman who was just now departing through the smaller side entrance.

Ari felt a spike of frustration, but forced it down. He’d deal with it later. His uncle meant well—he knew that—but now wasn’t the time or place to hash things out. He waved back and headed inside, managing what he hoped was a convincing smile.

Raolin crossed his arms, beaming. “Hey now. What’s this I hear about you besting Nira in a duel? Why didn’t you tell me over dinner?”

Ari suppressed a groan. “I didn’t think it was a big deal. I just got lucky. That’s all.”

“Ah, I see,” his uncle frowned, one hand stroking his beard. “Well,” he said, brightening up. “There’s a tourney coming up on—”

“Remembrance Day, yeah,” Ari interrupted. “Captain Izu told me. I’ll think about it.” Absentmindedly, he glanced back out of the chamber and down the hall.

Ari heard the clack of Raolin’s cane as he strode closer. He was doing a poor job of hiding his frustration, as usual.

“Hey,” Raolin said, leaning forward on his cane. “I’m sorry you didn’t make the expedition. Usually there’s plenty of room for volunteers, but this one’s different—can’t say more than that. I promise it won’t always be like this, though.”

That… okay, that was fair. Ari let out a breath he hadn’t realized he was holding, feeling some of the tension leave him.

Raolin chuckled, patting him on the side. “Hey. What do you say we catch up with the others? Grab some food and check out the entertainment?”

Ari raised an eyebrow. “Entertainment?”

“Ahh!” Raolin’s face lit up and he twirled his cane. “Seff, the Court Minstrel—he’s back in town! He’s an old friend of mine. We used to be part of the same troupe back in my barding days.” He winked. “You’re in for a real treat.”

Uncle Raolin had managed to snag a seat on one of the benches up front. Ari stood just off to the side nearby. A few of the older guests also sat on the benches, but most stood in a loose crowd just behind and to the sides.

The murmurings of the audience died down as stagehands closed the shutters on the windows. Darkness filled the hall.

The room went silent.

A dozen candles flickered to life. Their flames, reflected in silvered glass, revealed a single man seated on a wooden stool beside a golden harp. He had sharp, angular features and stylish white hair.

Seff?

To Caelon’s mind of gold
And Vaka’s might untold
And dear Nenore’s love…

Wrest order from the void

Such a fearsome sight!
Her eyes wreathed in fire

The tower prop collapsed into pieces as fabric flames rose to engulf the debris. The set pieces shifted to reveal a Blightkin savagely hacking at a screaming woman—drawing gasps and muttered curses from the audience. Further down the stage, a hulking Dreadkin ran a soldier through with a spear while two others fled.

The curtain drew closed as Seff returned to center stage, pacing deliberately and pausing to punctuate the story.

“The people were horrified, stunned, and appalled by the slaughter at Farwatch. Despite their desperate prayers and offerings, the Goddesses were nowhere to be found! Many felt betrayed. Why had they allowed this to happen? Had the people been forsaken?”

The audience murmured among themselves. Seff stepped forward, though the actors paid him no mind. “Well?!” he demanded, arms outstretched. “Avalia needs a champion! Is there no one among you with the spirit and valor to stand against the hordes?”

Ari felt a shove from behind and stumbled forward. Before he could recover, the light from the rafters swung to focus on him. His breath caught. He froze, suddenly desperate to disappear. Shielding his eyes with one hand, he squinted into the brightness. The hall went still.

Oh no. This was really happening.

Dear Avalia, Ari silently prayed. Holy Mother. Goddess Above, please—

“Brave knight!” the Avalia-actress called. “Your spirit is second to none. Come forward, and accept my blessing!”

All around him, the crowd cheered. An auspicious and triumphant fanfare sounded out with horns blaring. Streaming tinsel fell from above. The light from the rafters dimmed, and Ari saw that Seff was waving him over and offering a helping hand onto the stage.

This was too much. It was all just too much.

Something inside him snapped. The Goddesses wanted to have their fun with him? Fine. He would oblige.

Ari took a breath, glanced back at the crowd, and winked. He took a running start, dove onto the stage in a roll, and sprang to his feet. The audience roared with wild applause. Sweeping around to face them, he struck what he hoped was a suitably heroic pose.

Seff stepped up beside him, applauding along with the crowd. “Well, folks—looks like we’ve found our champion!”

All eyes turned to Ari, and the realization struck: he had no idea what to do next. He stood stiffly, looking between the actors and the crowd. The applause died down and an awkward silence settled over the hall.

Erm…

Seff spoke up with a stage whisper. “Mr. Hero, I think you’re supposed to accept the blessing of Avalia.” A bout of laughter rose from the crowd.

Right.

Ari approached and dropped to one knee before the Avalia-actress. The light shone from above on the two of them. Somewhere, a harp played out a simple melody.

“O, Courageous Hero!” she called out, her voice ringing across the stage. Ari winced—he hadn’t realized they needed to project quite so loudly for the hall. “Rise, and accept my blessing. Go forth and bring peace to Avendal!”

The room dimmed and the light focused now on Ari alone, intensifying as the horns and harp sounded out another fanfare. The Avalia-actress and the King scrambled away in the darkness.

Seff strode back to center stage, motioning for for Ari to stand. He did so. The light from above faded, and the room lit up.

“And so, the Champion of Avalia sallied forth alongside the stalwart defenders of the Realm, bringing battle to the evil Blin and buying time for the people to flee to Avalia’s land of promise and prosperity!”

At the back of the stage, three actors dressed as knights entered from the left carrying banners and spears. The crowd shouted and stamped their feet with encouragement from Seff. A moment later, four actors dressed as Blightkin entered from the right to boos and jeers.

“Wait! Mr. Hero!” Seff called from behind. Startled, Ari turned back to see him waving a wooden sword.

Another swung and took down his comrade’s attacker, but was himself felled by two more. The last soldier turned to run, leaving Ari to face the three remaining enemies alone. One stabbed at him with an exaggerated thrust. Ari effortlessly batted the attack away, yanked on the spear, and smacked the actor on the back as he fell to the ground with a yelp.

The other two Blin actors were well out of range and backing away. Ari stood his ground, unsure if he was actually supposed to fight. The fallen actors crawled off-stage.

“The knights fought valiantly and drew the attention of the advancing armies, but the brutish hordes prevailed by sheer numbers alone! Desperate, the Champion made a last stand at the Temple of Avalia, buying time for his allies to retreat!”

From the left side, a two-tiered wooden platform with a fanciful temple backdrop was wheeled in. A second charged and roared, stopping just outside of striking distance. Ari stepped in and made a slashing motion. The Blightkin-actor fell away clutching at his neck.

The remaining four actors spread across the lower rung of the platform. The three Blightkin rushed him at once. Backed against a wall, Ari felt a surge of panic—then had to remind himself this wasn’t a real fight. He slashed at one, who fell away screaming. The other two grabbed him. The Dreadkin advanced with a spear. Ari struggled, jerked free, bopped one on the head, and drew the wooden sword across the other’s chest. They fell away. He readied himself for the Dreadkin’s attack, but his final opponent halted, then turned and fled.

“The Champion succeeded in buying time, though many fell that day,” Seff called out, his tone somber. “The hordes retreated and victory was won, but the cost was high… for the Champion of Avalia himself succumbed to his wounds after the battle.”

Ari looked around uncertainly. He was supposed to be wounded, apparently. Not knowing what else to do, he clutched his chest and dropped to one knee, using the sword to prop himself up.

The curtains fell and the fallen actors rose to line up across the stage. The rest of the troupe hurried to join in.

One of the Blightkin actors clapped him on the shoulder, causing him to miss Seff’s next line. “Hey kid—good job! But easy with the sword next time, yeah? That thing hurts!” He rubbed at his head with a wince.

The curtains rose to thunderous applause. The troupe bowed together, and Ari with them. Seff called out each of the cast by name, and they each posed and took a bow in turn.

The audience erupted with cheers. Ari stepped forward and flourished his wooden sword, playing into the excitement. He couldn’t help but smile. What had he been so worried about? That hadn’t been bad at all.

Best get going. Daylight was waning, and she still had a long ride ahead.
