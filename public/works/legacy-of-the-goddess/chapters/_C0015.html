Shesan, people are dying as we speak in raids from roving Blin bands because they lack protection, and more will die as a result of my actions. If you have any actual suggestions as to how I can do more—please! I’m all ears.”

“Zelda,” Shesan said, turning to face her. “I know you don’t want to hear this, but it’s an obvious truth and you need to acknowledge it. Attempting to keep your kingdom intact in the face of Demise’s assault against your realm only keeps you busy and diverts your attention while he continues to plot and scheme.”

Zelda huffed. “So I’m supposed to—what? Just order Wymar to pack up when we’re so close to ending the Blight? Throw my people to the Blin while I set my soldiers to scouring ancient ruins halfway across the world, chasing myths and legends?”

“I don’t know what the right answer is, or even if there is an answer,” Shesan said, sidestepping her objection. “I only know that Ja’bu was not a coincidence. It fits the same pattern I have seen over and over again, since the beginning with the Gerudo. He will pile on the hardships and sow chaos with minimal exertion on his part while he continues, unchallenged. Except, now he is accelerating. Moving faster and making larger plays as he grows more confident in his victory.

“Do you think Ganondorf is going to wait in the shadows for you to clean up the remnants of the Blin and help the Zora retake their homeland before striking again? No. There will be something else sooner or later—and my bet is on sooner. I don’t know what, but it will hit, and it will hit hard. If we continue as we are, there is no hope.”

Zelda told herself she wasn’t going to do this. That she wasn’t going to get drawn into some pointless argument. “Do you have any specific information?” she asked. “Anything more than conjecture?”

“More than conjecture, but nothing specific. Call it a hunch.”

“I can’t act on a hunch, Shesan.”

“Have you had any more dreams lately?” Shesan said, deflecting.

“Nothing new?” Zelda answered with a shrug. “At least none that are obviously prophetic.”

“And did you recognize all of the prophetic ones as ‘obviously prophetic’ before witnessing that which they referred to?”

“…Yes? Mostly?” Zelda said, flustered. “I mean, they were ominous, vivid, and had recurring themes. Even so, I didn’t know what they referred to specifically until I learned of the Blight and later of Ja’bu. I’m not sure how I could have known ahead of time. There seems to be two distinct categories—well, three, if you consider random, normal dreams. Obviously portentous ones, and ones that only show me glimpses of odd things—like the man in the forest. And it’s difficult to differentiate the latter from just normal dreams.”

“So to clarify: no portentous dreams lately?”

“No,” Zelda said.

“But you said ‘nothing new,’” Shesan said. “Does that mean you’ve had repeats of the same dream?”

Zelda hesitated. “Maybe? I think so?”

“And you don’t think that might be important?”

Zelda grunted in frustration. “Yes, I’ve considered this. The only candidate was one that was similar to the circle of Blin in an open field. Same location, except this time it was night, and there was only one of them. He turned and seemed to notice me. When he reached out, I woke up.” She gestured with an open palm. “Do you have any idea what that could mean? Because I don’t.”

“Hmm,” Shesan said, turning her head, seeming to consider the question seriously. She tapped her fingers together behind her back and paced.

Zelda waited with arms crossed.

“I’m not sure,” Shesan said finally. “And just to be clear, let me preface this by saying it is entirely conjecture. Perhaps there is a group of them undergoing some kind of ritual and your fragments are picking up on the residual magic. Or something.”

“Or something,” Zelda said flatly.

Shesan shrugged.

“Is there something you’re not telling me?”

“No,” Shesan said. “Unfortunately. I’m just as in the dark as you are on this one.”

Zelda stared up at her tent, lying on her bedroll, lost in thought and unable to sleep.

All this time she had berated herself for being a poor queen. Every step of the way on this journey she had felt the need to make excuses to herself. To justify why it was necessary for her to come out here while her kingdom and her people suffered in her absence. She had spent so much time before meeting Shesan simply wondering what to do next when she got back. Where was she most needed? How could she best serve her kingdom and make things better for all?

Now, she wasn’t even sure of that. Was Shesan right? Loathe as Zelda was to admit it, she had a point. Their enemies would not play fair, nor wait for them to recuperate. Was Zelda an even worse queen for not committing everything in her power to the ends of getting ahead of Ganondorf’s next move? Of combating Demise?

They didn’t even know what Demise wanted with the Triforce, but it was a safe bet it played a central role in his plans. Would it really bestow him with the power of the Golden Goddesses? Even if not—Ganondorf had at least five fragments of Power already, according to Shesan. Zelda didn’t know what that meant in practical terms, but it was certainly not good for their side. Wars had been fought over single fragments of Power—a single fragment was enough to turn Father into one of the most fearsome warriors of his time. By all accounts, five shards of Wisdom would substantially clarify her dreams, and with that kind of power, they would no longer be left guessing. What would five fragments of Courage do for Link, when one was enough to put an ordinary, though talented soldier on even footing with the Sheikah elite?

There had to be a balance, no? She wouldn’t have any power or a kingdom’s worth of resources if she didn’t maintain said kingdom and protect it from Demise’s attacks. And even if she did commit everything to thwarting him and lost her kingdom in the process, what would be left to fight for?

Zelda had no idea how to proceed. Should she really do as Shesan suggested, and sacrifice everything else in the hopes of getting ahead? Or should she double down on securing her kingdom and hope that they would be able to weather out the storm?

Was there any hope at all against a foe that had defeated the Mother of Creation?
