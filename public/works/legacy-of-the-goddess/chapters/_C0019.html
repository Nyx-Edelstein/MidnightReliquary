Zelda sat at a small table in a makeshift war room. Laylith, Izu, Naibi and Shesan sat with her, while Malg stood off to the side, leaning against a stack of crates. They were the better part of a days’ ride past Last Bastion. Another day and the Sunseeker regiment would part ways, leaving the rest of the caravan to continue on to Castletown. Good timing on Link’s part.

Zelda was ecstatic that he and his party had found as much as they had on the mission to the Obsidian Spire, but one thing in particular about the discoveries bothered her.

Stalfos.

What kind of world did they live in, where fighting the reanimated bodies of the dead was an actual consideration to be dealt with? Zelda never really gave those stories much thought—she just assumed it was part of how things were back when magic was more prevalent in the world. Back when Hylia was still around. Unbelievable as it was, the Lightsworn existed for a reason, after all.

She found herself fidgeting, and clasped her hands on the table.

“In light of Link’s findings, I am… concerned,” she said to those gathered before her. “About the possibility that Demise may be able to raise the dead to fight against us. In my dreams…”

The slow, inexorable march of a thousand skeletal warriors. Eyes burning with unholy fire, shields raised—barring her path, pushing her nearer and nearer the cliff…

She shook her head, pushing the vision aside. “I find it troubling that my dreams have at times featured Stalfos. And not just Stalfos—in several of them I have seen the reanimated dead. My own mother and father’s wailing corpses. Fishermen with rotting flesh and worms crawling from their skulls. Dead bodies washing ashore and rising up…”

At her side, Laylith gently touched her arm. Zelda took a calming breath.

“Do we know what is being done with the bodies of slain Blin?” Zelda asked. “…Slain Hylians?” She shuddered.

Izu cleared his throat from across the table. “Yes, actually. The bodies of the Blin are piled up in ditches and burned, then buried. I never really thought about why that was done—I just assumed it was about efficiency—but it is standard procedure. Part of the old ways, handed down in the codebooks since time immemorial.”

“And Hylians?” Zelda said anxiously.

“Pyres, usually. When we can recover the body.”

“Does burning the remains prevent the skeletons from being reanimated?” Laylith asked.

Everyone looked to Shesan. The woman rolled a hand outward, as if to say “Who knows?”

“That being part of your ancient traditions suggests it does,” Shesan said. “I can’t say—but really, it isn’t the skeletons you have to worry about. Voidspawn themselves are dangerous, and while the specifics of how their influence manifests in the physical plane varies substantially from place to place, Voidspawn in general are capable of nearly anything. You don’t have to look any further than Ja’bu’s corrupting influence on the Zora to see that.”

She leaned in, meeting each of their eyes in turn, her voice drawing to a whisper. “Voidspawn feed off of the darkness and fear in people’s minds, warping and twisting those they sink their claws into, manipulating them to their own devious ends. They are whatever you fear they are. They are capable of whatever you believe them to be. The bodies of the deceased may conveniently provide a physical form that allows them to act directly, but powerful Voidspawn—or simply large numbers of them—are plenty dangerous all on their own.”

A cold chill crawled up Zelda’s spine.

“That’s all well and good,” Izu said. “But how do we fight them?”

Shesan shrugged. “With luck, we won’t have to.”

“Luck,” Laylith said flatly. “Really?”

“Yes,” Shesan said matter-of-factly. “Luck. We can’t prepare for every eventuality, and at some point you have to make the determination of when not to expend resources chasing down improbabilities. Aside from Ja’bu, the only Voidspawn we’ve encountered were sealed away in an ancient Lightsworn structure—and those were likely nothing more than remnants of the Dark One’s influence.”

The Dark One? Zelda was about to raise the question, but Shesan caught her eye.

…Oh, right. Naibi was sitting right next to her. Zelda bit her lip and looked away.

Shesan continued. “The presence of Voidspawn there heavily suggests there may be more sealed away at the other locations in Link’s visions. Make no mistake, it is a threat we need to be wary of—especially if Ganondorf beats us there and finds some way to free them—but if Demise were capable of unleashing Voidspawn on the world all on his own, he’d have done so by now. Probably.”

“Probably?” Zelda said. It was her turn to be skeptical.

Shesan sighed. “Yes, well, I would be lying if I said it didn’t bother me too—your legends and stories establish worrying precedent. What’s fact and what’s fiction is anyone’s guess, but taken as a whole, the evidence is compelling: Voidspawn have in the past been used as agents of the enemy. Regardless, the fact that Demise in these times has had to resort to working through Ganondorf alone, and that Ganondorf’s actions have been constrained to mustering mortal armies and unsealing ancient evils, implies Demise is not capable of acting directly in that manner. For now.”

She held up a hand, preempting Laylith’s objection. “No, I cannot say for certain—he may well just be holding out for a more opportune time, or even working his way towards restoring lost powers. In my estimation that is unlikely at best, and even then it isn’t the most pertinent threat we face. Need I remind you that Ganondorf has at least five fragments of power?”

“Still no word back from the Goron expedition, by the by,” Izu said indolently. “Nor the other teams sent to rendezvous with Vesyrn and Golvara.”

“Do we know what his next move will be?” Malg said from off to the side.

“Awakening more ancient evils is my bet,” Naibi said.

The table fell silent. Zelda felt a mixture of nerves and a sickness in her stomach. She really didn’t like not knowing, when so much was at stake. They knew Demise was after the Triforce, but beyond that?

She furrowed her brow. Come to think of it, why had there been so much activity with the Triforce recently? The fragments had been lost for ages, yet her dreams were now seemingly showing her their locations. The dreams had not done that before the Blight. And where had Shesan gotten her information about the fragments in the Plains of Ruin?

Was the Triforce “waking up” in response to current events? Or perhaps something specific had happened to trigger it?

Her thoughts fell to Father’s fragment, now in Shesan’s possession. She hadn’t told Zelda of its location—likely keeping it hidden intentionally. Zelda agreed in the abstract that fewer people knowing its whereabouts was a good idea. Still, she wasn’t entirely sold on Shesan’s ability to keep it safe. Zelda knew almost nothing about her or her capabilities. Did Vesyrn have it? Did she have it? Naibi? Or was it being held by some hitherto unknown agent?

Shesan met her eyes, and Zelda looked away.

“Is there something I should know about?” Shesan said suspiciously. “Something you’re not telling me?”

Zelda grit her teeth, feeling a pang of anger and frustration. She let it go. “Not really,” she said, as cordially as she could manage. “Just thinking. There’s so much we don’t know about it. The Triforce, I mean.”

Shesan narrowed her eyes. Zelda felt uneasy. Should she press?

Might as well.

“Shesan, are you certain my father’s fragment is safe?”

She scoffed. “Not even remotely.”

“But you said—”

“—If you recall, what I said was ‘it is as safe as it can be.’ I harbor no illusions it will find its way to Ganondorf the moment it comes into contact with him. It’s out of his hands for now. That’s all I can say. My singular hope is that we can buy enough time to find the other fragments before that comes to pass.”

“What if we were to find someone else it would bind more strongly to?” Zelda ventured.

Shesan raised an eyebrow. “As I said, I’m open to the possibility. Do you have someone in mind?”

Zelda felt a sick taste in her mouth, but she had to voice the thought. If there were any chance it could help…

She took in a deep breath, paused, and composed her thoughts. “There is a man on my council—Prime Counselor Taetus. Commonborn, though he is undoubtedly the most ambitious person I know. For better or worse, he worked his way up from the gutters to become one of the wealthiest and most influential men in my kingdom. He’s been elected to his position by his peers for the past twelve years, and since Father’s passing, has been absolutely relentless in hamstringing me at every opportunity in pursuit of his own selfish goals. He makes a big show of being concerned ‘for the people,’ but that doesn’t stop him from lining his own pockets at every opportunity, and all-but spitting in the face of the nobility every time he gets the chance. Don’t get me wrong—he has his uses. He’s second-to-none at his craft, and someone has to do the job. His type is usually easy to deal with, but this man simply doesn’t know his place! I charged him with forestalling the famine in Castletown, and the very next day he’s confiscating all the rupees in town and flogging nobles in the streets just to make a point!”

To her side, Laylith cleared her throat. Zelda blushed and ran her hands over her face. She might have gotten a little carried away in trying to make her point.

“Anyway,” she said, brushing the tirade aside. “If Wisdom binds to something like idealism and Courage binds to something like selflessness, then perhaps Power binds to something like ambition? There has to be a reason the fragments have traditionally been held by kings and conquerors. Naibi, is it fair to say that Ganondorf was a particularly ambitious king?”

“Fair to say, yes,” Naibi said, not missing a beat. “Uniting the tribes was no small feat, yet the sheer determination of the King Among Kings inspired intense loyalty in his followers. With any other man, it would not have been possible.”

“Your Grace,” Malg said, coming to stand at the table. “Do you mean to say you believe Power would bind more strongly to Counselor Taetus than to Ganondorf?”

Zelda had to stifle a laugh. “No, not at all,” she corrected. “I’m only putting forward the possibility that it may be safer in his hands than wherever else it is presently.”

Shesan eyed her. Zelda returned her gaze, crossing her arms. “What? You think I’m wrong?”

“No, I’m not saying you’re wrong,” Shesan replied, “but you are making certain assumptions. We don’t know whether how strongly a fragment resonates with its current owner matters when it decides someone else is more worthy of holding it.”

“I concede that much,” Zelda said smugly, “but even you have to admit it merits testing, no?”

Shesan hesitated. “I… can’t say. If you’re right, then yes, absolutely. But if you’re wrong, then we increase our exposure. It isn’t without risk, and it’s difficult to weigh the balance.”

“That’s an easy one,” Laylith said, coming to Zelda’s defense. “How can we reduce our exposure?”

Shesan stood, wrapping her hands around the back of her head and pacing from side to side. A moment later, she let out a breath and turned sharply. “I don’t know this man, but from your description he does seem like a plausible candidate. How about this—arrange a meeting, and I’ll see for myself. Afterwards, I’ll give you my assessment and we can determine how to proceed from there. Does that work for you?”

Zelda nodded. “Sounds sensible enough.” She picked up her quill and made a note. “—Oh, so that means you’re coming back to Castletown rather than going with Naibi?”

“That seems to be the case, yes,” Shesan said, returning to her seat. “Will that be a problem?”

“No problem at all,” Zelda said. The castle was big enough she wouldn’t have to set eyes on the woman if she didn¹t need to.

Zelda looked over her notes. “Last item of business for now. Naibi, Shesan: do either of you know anything about a man named ‘Ibboso’ or his tribe? Supposedly he was a Gerudo king who died long ago. Link is looking for the location of his burial site.”

Naibi boggled at her.

“Oh—sorry,” Zelda said hastily. “Is this one of the ‘we don’t talk about it’ things?”

“No,” Naibi said, waving a hand. “Nothing of the sort. I find it curious—that is all. The story of Ibboso the Damned is a tale told to wayward children. Nothing more. Why would such a thing interest you, Your Grace?”

“It was part of Link’s vision,” Zelda said, “When he ascended to the top of the spire. In the vision, an echo of the Goddess Hylia charged him with putting Iboso and his tribe to rest. There may be a fragment of Courage involved.”

Naibi whistled. “Such strange times, when we find stories and fables to be true.”

“Can you tell me about these stories?” Zelda asked eagerly, dipping a quill to ink.

“There is not much to this one, sadly,” Naibi said. “Ibboso was said to be an arrogant king, who renounced the Gerudo way and violated the old laws. He offered refuge to the lost sons of other tribes—ꡦꡟꡛꡀꡲꡀꡟꡟꡖꡨ—those who were exiled for the crime of weakness. This angered the gods, and his tribe was cursed to roam the dry sands for eternity, never knowing the peace of death.”

“The crime of weakness?” Zelda said in disbelief. It was painfully obvious to her that “the gods” in this tale referred to Morudo Geira in some capacity, but she wasn’t about to mention that in front of Naibi.

“We no longer follow the old laws,” Naibi said simply. “Please, let us not speak any more of this.”

Zelda nodded solemnly. “My mistake. Naibi, in Link’s vision, there was a mausoleum in the middle of a graveyard filled with shambling corpses. He said he had a vague feeling it was somewhere to the east, but he doesn’t know exactly where it is. You wouldn’t happen to know where to find that, would you?”

“Ehh,” she waved a hand from side to side. “There is a certain pass in the eastern mountains where no one goes. It is said that it was once a place of ritual and worship, but is now haunted. I never put stock in such tales, but if what you say is true, perhaps the dead really do roam there. I cannot say if it is the same place as his vision, but I also do not know where else it might be. There are not many such tales.”

“But do you know where it is?” Zelda asked anxiously. “Can you point it out on a map?”

“No, but I expect some in the caravan might know.”

“Wonderful!” Zelda said, rising to her feet. “Let’s take care of that right away—Link and his team are waiting for the information. Thank you all—that’s all for now.”

Zelda’s Research Notes
On Fi, Old Magic, Chromatic Magic, and Magical Artifacts

Note: The first of these entries are a compilation of disparate notes recorded separately until the 34th of Last Brisk, Year 548 of the Common Era. Subsequent entries appended thereafter.

35th of First Brisk:

There is so much I do not understand about Fi. She’s quite the peculiar creature. I would have thought that being the Emissary of the Goddess Hylia, she would know more than she does. However, Hylia’s death seems to have adversely affected her somehow. I have inquired at length about ways to restore her memories and lost abilities, but I am wholly unable to understand her responses most of the time. So frustrating.

Telepathy and Compel—these abilities in particular seem likely to be useful in the future, though at present I struggle to find any immediate applications.

Unlike Blue magic, her Telepathy is not limited by range. For now she is relegated to acting only in my vicinity, and further, communicating with anyone besides me—anyone who does not bear Hylia’s Mark—is prohibitively expensive. Still—reliable, instantaneous communication at arbitrary distances opens up quite a few possibilities.

Use of Compel on enemies seems straightforward enough, though again, we don’t have enough Divine Power to use it liberally. I would love to test it further and better understand its limits once we’ve stored enough.

Of particular interest is Hylia’s Mark—or more precisely, Mark of Transference. By Fi’s descriptions, these marks are intimately tied with Fi herself. From what little I’ve gleaned from her nigh-inscrutable responses, Hylia’s mark somehow transmutes my own residual magic and life energies into a form usable by her. Indeed, it seems these energies are the very same as her own “Divine Power.” She’s literally made of the stuff!

…So pretty.

Thus far I am dissatisfied with the rate at which we are accumulating Divine Power. In order to build up the reserve to practically useful levels, it will be necessary to mark others besides just me. Fi says we can only have three “active” marks; marks which allow her to act and communicate directly. “Passive” ones will still generate Divine Power—just at a much lesser rate.

Arlinkar is a natural first choice. Fi says that “acting as befits a Champion of Hylia” generates more Divine Power. Is there anyone alive in the kingdom today more heroic than The Hero of Castletown?

My very first thought after Fi described the Mark of Transference in more detail was to see if we could develop a variant that would require less of an initial investment, and which would only have the “transference” property—ignoring or bypassing all other complications and constraints. Unfortunately, she is very confident this will not be possible. She says the process involves altering one’s “spiritual essence” to cause “cascading resonance” with … something, and that barring developing an entirely new approach, it is essentially all-or-nothing.

I suppose we’ll just have to mark Arlinkar, and then figure out where to go from there.


2nd of Last Brisk:

Following my research into legends and arcana, I asked Fi if the example of the Cane of Byrna would be enough for her to develop, recover, or otherwise reverse-engineer similar Old Magic.

(Don’t ask me—I have no idea how she does anything. She doesn’t even have arms!)

As per the stories, the cane has three abilities: (1) Repelling Magic, (2) Invigoration of its wielder, and (3) Protection from all harm.

Fi is very confident that repelling magic is not in any way related to Old Magic, as the chromatic magics do not interact directly with hers. A useful insight, I suppose. I will have to keep it in mind.

She has not ruled out the possibility of the protection effect being replicable, but at present she is unaware of any mechanisms by which it would work. She told me it would likely require somewhere between ten and a thousand times more Divine Power than we currently have—and that much just to give me a definitive answer! Eek. Probably best to put this one aside for now.

Fortunately, she does think the physical reinvigoration effect may be possible to replicate. I didn’t understand much of what she said when I asked, but it’s somehow related to spiritual essences. If she can observe me while I’m rested and while I’m tired, she might be able to work out a way to use Divine Power to isolate and reverse the relevant changes to my essence, thus in effect restoring stamina.

She estimates it will require an additional 700 to 1300 "units" worth of Divine Power for her to complete testing. Certainly more than we can spare right now, but it doesn’t seem all that much in the grand scheme. She also cautions that it may have adverse effects on metabolism, but that seems like a small price to pay for such a valuable ability. I can’t wait to try it!

Additional notes from my session with her earlier today:

She advises me that having an actual, functional example of magical application to work from would considerably reduce the amount of Divine Power she needs to research new abilities or recover old ones. This makes plenty of sense, but I’m not sure where exactly I’m supposed to get Old Magic artifacts. It’s not like we just have a pile of them lying around in the basement…

I asked about using fragments of the Triforce as a basis, but apparently replicating or altering their functions was well beyond even Hylia’s ability. Too bad.

Oh, also the fragments can’t be suffused with Divine Power. That was one of the first things I had her try. Figures, since it doesn’t work with Imbue either.

Fi has cautioned me several times not to be too optimistic about magical research. Again, it all makes sense. Still, I can’t help but feel like a child in a candy-shop. There’s an entirely new type of magic here that no one else knows about! Surely there’s a plethora of novel applications just waiting to be discovered.

I suppose we’ll just have to wait until we’ve stored up enough Divine Power for more research. Fi won’t even answer me right now because I’ve entirely exhausted the budget.
