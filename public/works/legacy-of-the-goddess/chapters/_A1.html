Link couldn’t sleep. Nightmares, as usual. And so, he found himself sitting atop a guard tower at the southeastern edge of the castle grounds, preparing a meal by the firepit.

It was raining, and that helped. Link found comfort in the rain. It wasn’t a particularly heavy rain, but rather the steady drizzle characteristic of the last few weeks of Storms. The soft patter all about, the low rumblings in the distance, and the gentle tapping on the roof above put his mind at ease.

It almost drowned out the sounds of boisterous soldiers playing cards a floor below.

It was not his place to criticize, but he couldn’t help but feel that most of the city guard didn’t take their duty very seriously. He was off-duty himself (and technically not part of the guard besides), but he didn’t have anything else to do in town. Might as well do something marginally useful with his time. If nothing else, he took solace in the routine.

At this hour, amid the first stirrings of dawn, not much was visible save for other guard towers and a few dozen islands of light along the walkways and walls. The lampposts glowed rather than flickered; the steady light of red magic.

Unbidden, his mind flashed to images of bloodthirsty Blin charging on armored boars across an open field at dusk, eyes and steel glowing with that same red tinge.

Link shook himself from the images, trying to focus on his meal instead. The food here in Castletown was much better than the rations back in Talus. Roasted tubers and boiled hrok eggs with oxgoat cheese and sourmilk. He’d take this over hardtack and jerky any day.

He watched along the walls as he enjoyed his meal in solitude. Mentally, he noted pathways to and from the castle, the patrols and their timings, and their positioning relative to other manned guard towers.

Just as he was finishing up he heard a call from below—“High Captain in command! At arms!”—followed by clamoring of feet and metal.

Uncle Raolin?

Link hurried to clean up, tossing his leavings into the fire. He hadn’t thought his uncle would be out so early, or he wouldn’t have come here. A minute or so passed as he waited anxiously, trying to make out the indistinct sounds of conversation between his uncle and the sergeant on duty.

At last the hatch opened and High Captain Raolin made his way up. He was clad in full officer’s regalia; a tight fitting blue coat hemmed in gold and bearing the winged-shield crest of the City Guard. A cane clacked against the stone with every other step, with Raolin slightly favoring his left leg. Link couldn’t help but note he had gained weight in the three years since they had last met.

Link stood and saluted, right hand crossed over his chest.

“At ease, son.” Raolin said with a smirk. “You’re not on duty here, and you are not under my command.”

“Sir.” Linked dropped his salute and stood at ease.

Raolin laughed and shook his head. He took two steps forward and held his arms outstretched. He met Link’s eyes and cocked his head to one side.

Link relented and embraced his uncle. Raolin gave him a good firm squeeze, then with a hearty guffaw, stepped back and placed a hand on his shoulder. “Goddesses, look at you,” he beamed. “All grown up! You get any taller and I’ll be the one looking up to you.”

Link couldn’t help but smile at his uncle’s simple but earnest affection. “I missed you, Uncle. How’s city life treating you?”

“Hah!” Raolin said, genuine mirth in his eyes. “Getting that scratch was the best thing that ever happened to me. Now I get to sit in on Council meetings, have a dozen Lieutenants clambering to lick my boots…” He trailed off, following Link’s gaze. “Can’t say I much enjoy the paperwork, though.”

Link couldn’t find words to respond, so he just stood quietly, nodding. He never really imagined Raolin as the city type. He was glad to hear his uncle was doing well, though.

Raolin frowned. “What are you doing out here, anyway? I know you got in late—you should be sleeping. Something bothering you?” He led the way to the benches nearby and took a seat, taking off his gloves and warming his hands by the fire.

“No,” Link said, unthinking. He winced. “Yes,” he amended quickly. “Er, I don’t know,” he said, attempting to convey his frustration with hand gestures as he took a seat on the bench. “It’s… complicated.” He wasn’t feeling keen on rehashing his thoughts yet again. Lately everyone seemed overly concerned for him; he didn’t want to worry his uncle too.

“I’ve seen it all, son,” Raolin said earnestly. “Try me. Why don’t you start with your last skirmish?”

Well, he knew that was coming. “It was a bad one,” Link began with a sigh. “We tracked a small band that managed to evade patrols through the passes and into the basin. They were headed straight for Hateno, but turned south just before we caught up.” He paused, discomfort rising within. Raolin didn’t say anything, instead motioning for Link to continue. He did.

“I lost five of my men. None of them died in the fighting. We actually routed them despite it all—even had healers on hand. But… their wounds…”

“Bloodrage,” his uncle spat. “Damn those curs to the Void.” Raolin growled under his breath as he rubbed at his leg. “It’s not a pretty way to go, not by a damn sight. I’m sorry you had to see that.”

“It’s not that,” Link said, shaking his head. He wondered how much he should volunteer. “That was… horrible… but I’ve seen it before. And we all know what our duty means.”

Raolin nodded solemnly. He motioned as if to say “go on.”

The world spun, and Link kept speaking despite himself. He felt disconnected, as if watching himself from a distance. “It didn’t have to happen like that. I could’ve saved them,” he said, voice barely above a whisper. “Something was off. It was the smells, I think. It didn’t smell like they had actually camped that night. I didn’t know what it was at the time, but I didn’t like it. My second insisted the intel was good, that we were in a good position. Said I was being paranoid. I didn’t want to argue, so I gave the order. But I was right. Twice their number were waiting for us in the mountains. It was an ambush.”

Raolin crossed his arms, mouth drawing to a line. “I read the report, son. Ambush or no, you made the right call, and likely saved a lot of lives.” He shook his head. “Losing men who died at your orders? It’s rough. But it happens to the best of us. You can’t blame yourself, and I won’t watch you beat yourself up over it. Trust me son, it could’ve been a lot worse.” A flash of concern, worry, and an uneasy smile. “But it wasn’t, and you are to thank for that.”

“No. I mean, I know,” Link said, coming back to himself. Hearing praise always made him uncomfortable, even when it came from his uncle. But it wasn’t about the skirmish. That ate at him, but there was more to it. His uncle wasn’t seeing it. Why didn’t anyone else see it?

“It’s just… that wasn’t the first time. It was just the worst,” Link said. “It’s worrying—they’re too organized. There’s been more and more reports like that lately. You’ve seen them too, haven’t you?” He hesitated. He might as well just say it. “If I didn’t know better, I’d say they’re feeling out our defenses, gathering information. I fear the worst.”

“You think there’s a Warlord,” Raolin said flatly, his expression unreadable. “Son, if they had a Warlord leading them, they wouldn’t bother with anything but a straight assault with overwhelming force. You know that.”

Link gazed back over the grounds. Lieutenant Averil had said roughly the same, that it was nothing new and nothing unexpected. She had actually ordered him to spend a fortnight reading history before bothering her again, much to Link’s embarrassment. Still, it didn’t add up, even if he didn’t have a better explanation. The Blin were too organized. Link understood their perspective; a couple dozen more deaths than usual over the course of a year was hardly cause for alarm. But they weren’t there like he was!

He could feel his uncle watching him intently. He had let the silence go on too long again. A heavy tension hung in the air. Uncle Raolin knew him better than anyone, and Link knew he was only trying to give him space. Still, the weight of Raolin’s attention pressed down on him and smothered his thoughts.

Link turned back to the guard patrols. He pointed off to the side, desperate to change the subject. “See that? There’s a timing gap.”

“A what?” Raolin looked to where Link was pointing.

“A gap,” Link said. “Every five or six minutes, there’s about a twelve second gap in the guard patrols… there, that isn’t covered by any manned towers. At least at this hour.”

Raolin grunted, seeming unconcerned.

“I mean I could probably slip through if I wanted,” Link offered. “Would be nothing for a trained assassin.”

“Arlinkar…” Raolin said, gaping at him in disbelief. “We’re in Castletown!”

Link winced at the use of his full name. He made to respond, but turned away, flustered. There wasn’t anything he could say to salvage it now. Stupid… He knew not to let thoughts like that slip. Talking like that tended to make people get bad ideas about his mental state, and he was already under enough scruitiny. It was difficult, though. He couldn’t help but let his guard down around his uncle.

Raolin stammered, running his hand through his hair. “Link, listen to yourself! Who would do such a thing? We haven’t been at war with the Gerudo for over a thousand years. Why in the name of the Holy Mother Above would we be worried about assassins?”

“The King—”

“The Sheikah are looking into the King,” Raolin said, cutting him off. “And if they suspected anything, I would be the first to know.” A pause. “As it happens, they say it to be a natural illness.” Pause. “Besides, why would someone send assassins after the fact? That would be absurd.”

Something in his uncle’s words bothered him. A natural illness—that was what Link had heard elsewhere. But he had done some reading, and asked around—the oldest and most experienced healers he knew. He hadn’t found any records of illnesses like that, and they hadn’t heard of any either. Come to think of it, would his uncle actually tell him if the Sheikah found something? Would the Sheikah even tell anyone?

“Link.”

Link sat still. He didn’t like that tone. He didn’t like where this was going.

“Link, look at me.”

He did so. Raolin put a hand on his shoulder. His eyes were red. It pained Link to see him like that.

“Son, you are far too young to carry the weight of the world with you. I cannot abide this. They told me, but I didn’t think it was this bad.”

Link started, eyes narrowing. “I’m fine, Uncle. I just…” He stood, stepping away to cover his frustration. “I need some time to clear my head. That’s all. I know I’ve been through a lot, but I can handle it. I’ll be fine.”

Raolin muttered something under his breath that Link didn’t catch. “I won’t mince words,” he said. “You deserve that much. Your assignment as guard detail to the envoy was a pretense. They didn’t think you’d take kindly to being discharged, and didn’t want to chance what you’d do on hearing the news, so they sent you back to me instead.”

“So that’s it, then?” Link snapped, his suspicions confirmed. His heart pounded in his ears. He was not one to question orders, but everything about the envoy mission was wrong. Link thought they were simply sending him away again to “unwind,” but this?! How could they?

Raolin stood and hobbled over to stand shoulder-to-shoulder, leaning heavily on his cane. Link glanced over to him. In that moment, he saw all the years of age written plainly on his Uncle’s face. Try as he might, he found it hard to hold on to his anger.

Raolin took a slow, measured breath. “Son, I know how important this to you. Your heart is in the right place. But you can’t burn yourself up like this. You’ve been on duty for seven years without reprieve! You’re jumping at shadows. No one serves in the field that long, and for good reason. You know that. Most only go a year at a time, and spend twice or more back home between rotations. What you’re doing is insane. Talus has stood for hundreds of years before you, and will continue to stand after you. There are many, many more to shoulder this burden.”

Link had nothing to say to that. His uncle wasn’t wrong, and Link knew he was genuinely worried. Still, it ate at him. He had to be able to do more. He had to be able to… to protect those that could not protect themselves. Like the Lightsworn of old! That was his calling! So what if it meant sacrifice? That was the whole point, wasn’t it? He shouldered the burden so others didn’t have to. How could they ask him to give up on what Hylia herself had ordained?

His uncle stirred next to him, scratching at his chest.

“Ah! Before I forget, I wanted to give you something,” Raolin said, fishing a necklace out from under his coat. He unclasped it and held it in his palm. He looked distant, as if reminiscing on an old memory. “It’s an old family heirloom,” he said. He turned the necklace over in his palm before passing it to Link. “Your grandfather gave it to me, and his father to him. It should be yours now.”

Hesitantly, Link took the necklace and examined it. It was a simple thing: a jagged golden shard on a thin chain. There was something… something he couldn’t place exactly, but something about holding this necklace felt right. “I… thank you, Uncle,” he said, gazing at the shard, completely transfixed.

“They say it brings fortune and good luck to whoever wears it,” Raolin said, tapping his leg with his cane. “I’m not sure what I think about that, but I’m still standing despite everything, so maybe there’s a grain of truth to it.”

Link nodded, clasping the chain behind his neck. He suddenly found that he had lost track of his thoughts. What had he been thinking about just now?

“Hey,” Raolin said with a pat to the back. “Cheer up—there’s good news. I pulled some strings, and got you a position in the Royal Guard.”

Link looked to his uncle, askance.

Raolin held up a hand. “Now before you say no, keep in mind this is a prestigious position. It will open doors for you. They’re highly selective—young men your age would kill for the chance.” Somehow, he managed to look smug. “But given your service history, it was an easy enough sell.”

“The Royal Guard,” Link said testily. “You want me to be a glorified doormat?”

Raolin gave a dramatic sigh, slouching. Link looked away. He didn’t like disappointing his uncle, but he could also tell when he was being manipulated.

“Oh, come now. It’s not as bad as that,” Raolin said. “You’ll have plenty of opportunities to get out. Princess Zelda is rather fond of going on excursions around the countryside—and increasingly often as of late.” He paused, eyes lighting up. “You know, she’s always going on about ‘research’ and ‘experiments’ and how her ideas will revolutionize the world and fix everything. Come to think of it, I think you two would get along splendidly. And who knows?” He jabbed Link’s side playfully, winking and wagging his eyebrows.

“Uncle…” Link started in annoyance, feeling his face flush. He tried to hide it, but not before Raolin saw. Chuckling, Raolin threw his arm around Link’s shoulder and ruffled his hair. Link ducked out of the grapple, breaking free and rolling his eyes as he smoothed his hair. He tried not to smile. This wasn’t the time to get caught up in silly games.

“Promise me you’ll at least think about it, yeah?”

Link nodded before he could catch himself.

“Great!” Raolin bellowed, cracking his cane against the floor. “I’ll let Vian know right away.”

“Wait, I didn’t—”

“I’ll schedule a meeting for you in a few hours,” Raolin called over his shoulder. To Link’s horror, he had already turned to leave and was heading back down the steps. “Oh, and one more thing. You’re to take the day off. That means actually try and relax. No training, no patrolling, and for the love of the Mother: no brooding. And come by for dinner tonight! Those are orders.”

“Uncle!” Link stammered, calling after him. “I… I thought I wasn’t under your command!”

His uncle laughed, turning back just as he disappeared below the hatch. “You are now, son!”

Link stood bewildered as the hatch swung shut with a clack. Eventually his thoughts caught up to him. His shoulders slumped in defeat and he took a long, measured breath. He had a lot to think about, and honestly had no idea what to do from here.

It couldn’t be too bad, he told himself. Uncle Raolin had always looked out for him.
