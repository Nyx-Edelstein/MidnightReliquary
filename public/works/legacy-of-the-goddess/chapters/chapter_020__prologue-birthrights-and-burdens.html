Ari couldn’t sleep. Nightmares, as usual. And so, he found himself sitting atop a guard tower at the southeastern edge of the castle grounds, preparing a meal by the firepit.

It was raining, and that helped. Ari found comfort in the rain. It wasn’t a particularly heavy rain—rather, the steady drizzle characteristic of the last few weeks of Storms. The soft patter all about, the low rumblings in the distance, and the gentle tapping on the roof above put his mind at ease.

It almost drowned out the sounds of boisterous soldiers playing cards a floor below.

It was not his place to criticize, but he couldn’t help but feel that most of the city guard didn’t take their duty very seriously. He was off-duty himself (and technically not part of the guard besides), but he didn’t have anything else to do in town. Might as well do something marginally useful with his time. If nothing else, he took solace in the routine.

At this hour, amid the first stirrings of dawn, not much was visible save for other guard towers and a few dozen islands of light along the walkways and walls. The lampposts glowed rather than flickered; the steady light of Red magic.

Unbidden, his mind flashed to images of bloodthirsty Blin charging on armored boars across an open field at dusk, eyes and steel glowing with that same red tinge.

Ari shook himself from the images, trying to focus on his meal. The food here in Castletown was much better than the rations back in Talus. Roasted tubers and boiled cassal eggs with oxgoat cheese and sourmilk. He’d take this over hardtack and jerky any day.

He watched along the walls as he enjoyed his meal in solitude. Mentally, he noted pathways to and from the castle, the patrols and their timings, and their positioning relative to other manned guard towers.

Just as he was finishing up he heard a call from below—“High Captain in command! At arms!”—followed by clamoring of feet and metal.

Uncle Raolin?

Ari hurried to clean up, tossing his leavings into the fire. He hadn’t thought his uncle would be out so early, or he wouldn’t have come here. A minute or so passed as he waited anxiously, trying to make out the indistinct sounds of conversation between his uncle and the sergeant on duty.

At last the hatch opened and High Captain Raolin made his way up. He was clad in full officer’s regalia; a tight fitting blue coat hemmed in gold and bearing the winged-shield crest of the City Guard. A cane clacked against the stone with every other step, with Raolin slightly favoring his left leg. Ari couldn’t help but note he had gained weight in the three years since they had last met.

Ari stood and saluted, right hand in a fist over his chest.

Raolin smirked.“At ease, son. You’re not on duty here, and you’re not under my command.”

“Sir.” Ari dropped his salute and stood at ease.

Raolin laughed and shook his head. He took two steps forward and held his arms outstretched. He met Ari’s eyes and cocked his head to one side.

Ari relented and stepped into his uncle’s embrace. Raolin pulled him in with a firm squeeze, then stepped back with a hearty guffaw, placing a hand on his shoulder. “Goddesses, look at you,” he beamed. “All grown up! You get any taller and I’ll be the one looking up to you.”

Ari couldn’t help but smile at his uncle’s simple but earnest affection. “I missed you, Uncle. How’s city life treating you?”

“Hah!” Raolin said, genuine mirth in his eyes. “Getting that scratch was the best thing that’s ever happened to me. Now I get to sit in on Council meetings, have a dozen Lieutenants clambering to lick my boots…” He trailed off, following Ari’s gaze. “Can’t say I much enjoy the paperwork, though.”

Ari wasn’t sure how best to respond, so he just stood quietly, nodding. He never really imagined Raolin as the city type. He was glad to hear his uncle was doing well, though.

Raolin frowned. “What are you doing out here, anyway? I know you got in late—you should be sleeping. Something bothering you?” He led the way to the benches nearby and took a seat, taking off his gloves and warming his hands by the fire.

Ari hesitated, searching for the right words as he took a seat on the bench. “It’s… complicated,” he said at last. He wasn’t keen on rehashing his thoughts yet again. Lately everyone seemed overly concerned about him, and he didn’t want to add his uncle to the list.

“I’ve seen it all, son,” Raolin said earnestly. “Try me. Why don’t you start with your last skirmish?”

He knew that was coming. “It was a bad one,” Ari began with a sigh. “We tracked a small band that slipped past patrols through the passes and into the basin. They were headed straight for Jarrensvale, but turned south just before we caught up.” He paused, discomfort rising within. Raolin said nothing, only motioning for Ari to continue. He did.

“I lost five of my men. None of them died in the fighting. We actually routed them despite it all—even had healers on hand. But… their wounds…”

“Bloodrage,” his uncle spat, rubbing at his leg. “Damn those curs to the Void. It’s not a pretty way to go, not by a damn sight. I’m sorry you had to see that.”

“It’s not that,” Ari said, shaking his head. He wondered how much to say. “That was horrible, but I’ve seen it before. We all know what our duty means.”

Raolin nodded solemnly. He motioned as if to say “go on.”

Ari felt the words spilling out before he could stop them, distant and detached, as if watching himself from afar. “It didn’t have to happen like that. I could’ve saved them,” he said, voice barely above a whisper. “Something was off. The smells were wrong—it didn’t smell like they’d actually camped that night. I didn’t know what it meant at the time, but I didn’t like it. My second insisted the intel was good, that we were in a strong position. Said I was being paranoid. I didn’t want to argue, so I gave the order.” He paused, the weight of it settling over him. “But I was right. Twice their number were waiting for us in the mountains. It was an ambush.”

Raolin crossed his arms, mouth drawing to a line. “I read the report, son. Ambush or no, you made the right call, and likely saved a lot of lives.” He shook his head. “Losing men who died at your orders? It’s rough. But it happens to the best of us. You can’t blame yourself, and I won’t watch you beat yourself up over it. Trust me, son, it could’ve been a lot worse.” A flash of concern, worry, and an uneasy smile. “But it wasn’t, and you are to thank for that.”

“No. I mean, I know,” Ari said, coming back to himself. Hearing praise always made him uncomfortable, even when it came from his uncle. But it wasn’t about the skirmish. That ate at him, but there was more to it. His uncle wasn’t seeing it. Why didn’t anyone else see it?

“It’s just—that wasn’t the first time,” Ari said. “Only the worst. It’s worrying. They’re too organized. There’s been more and more reports like that lately. You’ve seen them too, haven’t you?” He hesitated. He might as well just say it. “If I didn’t know better, I’d say they’re feeling out our defenses, gathering information. I fear the worst.”

Raolin’s expression went flat. “You think there’s a Warlord,” he said. “Son, if they had a Warlord leading them, they wouldn’t bother with anything but a straight assault with overwhelming force. You know that.”

Ari gazed back over the grounds. Lieutenant Averil had said much the same—that it was nothing new, nothing unexpected. She’d even ordered him to spend a fortnight reading history before bothering her again, much to his embarrassment. Still, it didn’t add up. The Blin were too organized, even if he couldn’t explain why. He understood their perspective—a couple dozen more deaths than usual over the past year was hardly cause for alarm. But they weren’t there like he was!

He could feel his uncle watching him. The silence stretched on, heavy and uncomfortable. Uncle Raolin knew him better than anyone—knew when to give him space and when to push. But the weight of that scrutiny pressed down on Ari, smothering his thoughts before they could take shape.

Ari turned back to the guard patrols. He pointed off to the side, desperate to change the subject. “See that? There’s a timing gap.”

“A what?” Raolin looked to where Ari was pointing.

“A gap,” Ari said. “Every five or six minutes, there’s about a twelve second gap in the guard patrols… there, that isn’t covered by any manned towers. At least at this hour.”

Raolin grunted, seeming unconcerned.

“I mean, I could probably slip through there if I wanted,” Ari offered. “It would be nothing for a trained assassin.”

“Arlinkar…” Raolin said, gaping at him in disbelief. “We’re in Castletown!”

Ari winced at the use of his full name. He made to respond, but turned away, flustered. There was nothing he could do to salvage it now. Stupid… He knew better than to let thoughts like that slip. People tended to get the wrong idea about his mental state when he talked like that, and he was already under enough scrutiny as it was. He couldn’t help but let his guard down around his uncle, though.

Raolin stammered, running his hand through his hair. “Ari, listen to yourself! Who would do such a thing? We haven’t been at war with the Akhari for over a thousand years. Why in the name of the Holy Mother Above would we be worried about assassins?”

“The King—”

“The Veilwyrd are looking into the King,” Raolin said, cutting him off. “And if they suspected anything, I would be the first to know.” A pause. “As it happens, they say it to be a natural illness.” Pause. “Besides, why would someone send assassins after the fact? That would be absurd.”

Something about his uncle’s words didn’t sit right with him. A natural illness—that’s what everyone kept saying. But Ari had done his research, consulted with the oldest and most experienced healers he could find. No records of anything like it. None of them had ever seen anything similar. And now that he thought about it—would his uncle even tell him if the Veilwyrd had found something? Would the Veilwyrd tell anyone at all?

“Ari.”

Ari sat still. He didn’t like that tone. He didn’t like where this was going.

“Ari, look at me.”

He did so. Raolin placed a hand on his shoulder, eyes red-rimmed. It pained Ari to see him like that.

“Son, you’re far too young to be carrying the weight of the world on your shoulders. I can’t stand seeing you like this. They told me it was bad, but I didn’t realize it was this bad.”

Ari’s eyes narrowed. “I’m fine, Uncle.” He stood and stepped away, turning to hide his frustration. “I just need some time to clear my head, is all. I know I’ve been through a lot, but I can handle it.”

Raolin muttered something under his breath that Ari didn’t catch. “I won’t mince words,” he said. “You deserve that much. Your guard detail assignment for the envoy was a pretense. They didn’t think you’d take kindly to being discharged, and didn’t want to risk what you might do on hearing the news. So they sent you back to me instead.”

Ari’s suspicions were confirmed. “So that’s it, then?” he snapped, his heart pounding in his ears. He was not one to question orders, but everything about the envoy mission had been wrong from the start. He’d thought they were simply sending him away again to “unwind,” but this? How could they?

Raolin stood and hobbled over, leaning heavily on his cane as he came to stand shoulder-to-shoulder with Ari. Ari glanced at him and saw all the years of age written plainly on his uncle’s face. Try as he might, he found it hard to hold on to his anger.

Raolin took a slow, measured breath. “Son, I know how important this is to you. Your heart is in the right place. But you can’t burn yourself out like this. You’ve been on duty for seven years without reprieve! You’re jumping at shadows. No one serves in the field that long, and for good reason. You know that. Most only go a year at a time, and spend twice as long back home between rotations. What you’re doing is insane. Talus has stood for hundreds of years before you, and it will continue to stand after you. There are many, many others to shoulder this burden.”

Ari had nothing to say to that. His uncle wasn’t wrong, and he knew the concern was genuine. Still, it gnawed at him. There had to be more he could do—more he should do. To protect those who couldn’t protect themselves, like the Lightsworn of old. That was his calling! What did sacrifice matter if it meant fulfilling his purpose? He bore the burden so others wouldn’t have to. How could they ask him to abandon what Avalia herself had ordained?

His uncle stirred next to him, scratching at his chest.

“Ah! Before I forget, I wanted to give you something,” Raolin said, fishing a necklace out from under his coat. He unclasped it and held it in his palm. He looked distant, as if reminiscing on an old memory. “It’s an old family heirloom,” he said. He turned the necklace over in his palm before passing it to Ari. “Your grandfather gave it to me, and his father to him. It should be yours now.”

Ari hesitantly took the necklace and examined it. A simple thing: a jagged golden shard on a thin chain. There was something about it, though—something he couldn’t quite place. It felt right in his hands, in a way he couldn’t explain. “Thank you, Uncle,” he said, his gaze transfixed on the object.

“They say it brings fortune and good luck to whoever wears it,” Raolin said, tapping his cane against his leg. “I’m not sure I believe all that, but I’m still standing despite everything, so maybe there’s some truth to it.”

Ari nodded and clasped the chain behind his neck. For a moment, his thoughts seemed to scatter—what had he been thinking about just now?

“Hey,” Raolin said with a pat to the back. “Cheer up—there’s good news. I pulled some strings, and got you a position in the Royal Guard.”

Ari looked to his uncle, askance.

Raolin held up a hand. “Now before you say no, hear me out. This is a prestigious position—it will open doors for you. Young men your age would kill for the chance. They’re highly selective.” He managed to look smug despite himself. “But given your service history, it was an easy enough sell.”

“The Royal Guard,” Ari said testily. “You want me to be a glorified doormat?”

Raolin gave a dramatic sigh and slouched. Ari looked away. He didn’t like disappointing his uncle, but he could also tell when he was being manipulated.

“Oh, come now. It’s not as bad as that,” Raolin said. “You’ll have plenty of opportunities to get out. Princess Xayla is rather fond of excursions around the countryside—increasingly so, as of late.” He paused, a glint in his eye. “Now that I think about it, she’s always going on about her ’research’ and ’experiments,’ convinced her ideas will revolutionize the world. Fix everything.” A knowing smile crossed his face. “I think you two would get along splendidly. And who knows?” He jabbed Ari’s side playfully, winking and wagging his eyebrows.

“Uncle…” Ari protested, feeling heat rise to his face. He looked away, but not before Raolin saw. With a chuckle, his uncle threw an arm around his shoulders and ruffled his hair. Ari ducked free of the grapple, smoothing down his hair and rolling his eyes. Now wasn’t the time for such foolishness.

“Promise me you’ll at least think about it, yeah?”

Ari nodded before he could catch himself.

“Great!” Raolin bellowed, tapping his cane against the floor. “I’ll let Vian know right away.”

“Wait, I didn’t—”

“I’ll schedule a meeting for you in a few hours,” Raolin called over his shoulder. To Ari’s horror, he had already turned to leave and was heading back down the steps. “Oh, and one more thing. You’re to take the day off. That means actually try and relax. No training, no patrolling, and for the love of the Mother: no brooding. And come by for dinner tonight! Those are orders.”

“Uncle!” Ari stammered, calling after him. “I thought I wasn’t under your command!”

His uncle laughed, turning back just as he disappeared below the hatch. “You are now, son!”

Ari stood bewildered as the hatch swung shut with a clack. His shoulders slumped in defeat and he took a long, measured breath. He had a lot to think about, and honestly had no idea what to do next.

It couldn’t be too bad, he told himself. Uncle Raolin had always looked out for him.
