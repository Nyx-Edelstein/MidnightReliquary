Drained. Empty. Like she was piloting a soulless shell.

This is how Zelda felt at times such as these.

She’d lost count of the number of times she had to slip away to her study, or elsewhere as the situation permitted. To weep silently, or to cry and scream and break things, venting her anger and rage at the unjustness in the world. She knew she was not alone. The faces of everyone around her spoke the same truth.

Everything seemed to be going so well! But that’s how it always went. Some momentum here, and a swift swift smack to the face immediately thereafter. This was no different. The war with the Blin was coming to a close—and sure, there were all these other issues to contend with on the side (with more and more cropping up over time)—but damnation if they weren’t on the verge of returning to something approaching normalcy! They were making undeniable progress! The second Blin warlord had been slain, and the main army, lead by the Lord General himself, were on the cusp of retaking Talus and stemming the Blight for good.

Only for the impossible to happen, and for her people to be facing an unstoppable onslaught all over again.

Except this time it was infinitely worse. Not even the fifteen meter tall, five meter thick fortifications that were the walls of Castletown could protect against weapons that could cleave through metal as easily as through air.

They needed a miracle. Again.

Zelda breathed a deep breath. Patience. Focus.

Despite it all, she knew she could not be seen to waver in front of her people. She couldn’t be seen to be weak. The face she would present, even to those closest to her, would be one of resolution and determination.

I am Queen Zelda Numenia Proventia Hyrule, and I will lead my people.

The words stirred something deep within, filling her with warmth and determination.

Zelda wiped her face clean, inspected herself in the mirror, and climbed the ladder to the top of the guard tower.

She watched from Castletown’s central guard tower as mages trained at her orders, led by the Order of Sages and various monks who directed them according to Zelda’s instructions. Mentally, she likenened herself to a perched loftwing; an ever-present symbol to remind her people that she was watching, and that she was with them still. As much as it was possible to convey that image, she tried. The entire world was on fire—at least according to each and every one of her subjects and advisors who constantly sought her attention—and of course each of them had a different idea about what was the most pressing emergency.

Despite all that, here she stood. Resolute and impassive. Doing her best to be a beacon of hope, despite everything they had to endure.

Zelda was immensely thankful to have worked out enough of her issues with High Priestess Vestele that she could rely on her and High Bishop Rharu to coordinate the research attempts. They were not on the best of terms due to differing ideologies, but the priestess did not allow those differences to get in the way here, even though she very easily could have. Zelda knew full well it was a foolhardy endeavor at best, and she needed all the help she could get to see it through. Mentally, she remembered to thank Vestele personally for this should she get the chance.

Notwithstanding it’s relative depopulation, Castletown continued to be a bustling hub of activity. The entire market district had been appropriated for this task. Not that there was much to be done in the way of “appropriation”; the city had been all but emptied of residents, merchants, vagrants, and everyone else—all of whom long since fled to Kakariko or other relatively safe havens in the western countryside.

All around her, soldiers worked hurriedly to make preparations for the coming siege, and to accommodate those yet to arrive: the rest of the mages and then the southern forces—scheduled merely a day and a half ahead of the main army, with the Blin hot on their heels. Only a few dozen mages had arrived so far, but they were expecting many more in the days to come. A couple thousand, at least—though most of those wouldn’t be here until a day or two before Wymar and his forces, thus putting a hard limit on the different avenues of research that could be explored.

Zelda’s lips drew to a line.

It was not ideal. Not ideal at all. She would give anything for another week to prepare. But they did not have that time. Six days is all they had to learn everything they could from the manuscripts Link found at the Obsidian Spire—and not even that much! Care needed to be taken because of regeneration, and the ever-present problem of magical scarcity.

It was a delicate balancing act. Using magic was wholly necessary for exploratory research… and for logistics and siege preparations… but no matter what, the mages absolutely had to be in fighting shape for when the Blin arrived. And each mage had their own talents and capacities at that.

It would be a nightmare to coordinate everything in anything approaching an optimal fashion. As it was, they simply assigned each incoming mage to one of three groups (after sorting based on color), depending on their skill, affinity, and capacity. The first group—those with the highest skill—were directed to do freeform exploratory research after being debriefed according to their color’s expectations. The second group—those with the highest affinities regardless of skill—were directed to assist the first group in whatever manner they needed, but only to attempt the most promising avenues and techniques after the first group was spent. The last group—everyone else—was to serve in an advisory capacity only, and to function as magical batteries for the first and second groups.

Essentially, the first group charted the course, the second explored the most promising options given they could do so most efficiently, and the third expanded the range of the other two. Mages with multiple affinities were asked to exercise their best discretion, and to shift between color camps as they saw fit.

Once again, it was not in any way approaching ideal, but it was the best Zelda and her advisors could come up with on short notice, striking a balance between getting things done and not wasting so much effort on trying to solve the coordination problem that they ate into progress.

(Zelda herself was in the second group of the blue camp. As such, she would pursue the most promising avenues there, unless something in the Red or Green camps proved that much more enticing.)

The tower hatch swung open suddenly, startling Zelda out of her reverie and nearly off the parapet.

Chancellor Taetus. (He was a Chancellor now, she had to remind herself.)

“Ah, my apologies,” he said at once. “I did not mean to startle you, Your Grace.”

Ever perceptive, that man. Zelda waved it aside. “No worries. Deep in thought, is all. Is there something that requires my attention?”

Taetus proffered an open hand. “I have good news, for a change.” He smirked. “I am to understand that Golvara has made it safely through enemy territory, and is expected to arrive at Hylia’s Watch three days hence.”

Zelda paused, then nodded. She had to remind herself that Taetus and the others had not yet heard this news. She had only just heard it from Shesan the evening prior.

She supposed she should loop in Taetus at some point. She questioned however if now was the best time.

“Unfortunately,” he continued somberly, “two of his escort and one of our Sheikah lost their lives during the exfiltration mission. Goddesses rest their souls.” He hung his head as he said the words.

Zelda nodded grimly. She would see to it that the deaths of these brave souls, and all others who gave their lives in service of her kingdom—of the entire world—would be honored, in time.

Assuming they survived the coming siege, at any rate.

She sighed. There still remained the question of what to do with this news. So much had transpired since then, it had nearly slipped her mind. Golvara bore a fragment of Wisdom. A priceless artifact, though not one that would be immediately useful, aside from keeping it out of the hands of the enemy. Not from Zelda’s understanding at least—five fragments total would be necessary to clarify her dreams, which she felt was by far the most useful of her powers. Her abilities to communicate or scry at a distance hadn’t even worked since Impa’s passing—and there were none in her life who could fill Impa’s shoes.

Zelda turned her eye eastward, to the horizon. In the direction of the approaching onslaught. The blight would be upon them before anyone could make it from Hylia’s Watch to Castletown—and even then, would Castletown be the safest place for another fragment?

No. Not by a long shot.

Taetus cleared his throat, bringing Zelda’s attention back to the present. “Shall I send word to Hylia’s Watch, Your Grace?”

In her periphery, Father’s ring on Taetus’ finger caught her eye as he thumbed it idly, waiting for her response. Zelda rubbed a finger over her own ring—another of Father’s—the signet ring empty of its jewel.

She shook her head. “Let them know the message has been received, and that we are contemplating the best course of action. See to it that Golvara and his escort are given a hero’s welcome—as much as that is possible given the circumstances—and for all involved to await further orders.” She met Taetus’ eyes, then glanced back over the grounds. “There is much on my mind, and I will need to meet with you and my other advisors before I am sure of how to proceed.”

Taetus saluted.

“—And thank you, Chancellor Taetus, for everything. Despite our differences, I have come to rely on your expertise, and to count on you as a true friend. Goddesses guide your steps.”

Taetus paused briefly, before giving a humble bow. “It is an honor to serve our kingdom, Your Grace. I shall see your will done.”

He left, leaving Zelda to her thoughts.

Zelda’s Research Notes
Efforts to Recreate Lost Knowledge from the Obsidian Spire

10th of First Flows:

We are just getting started, so the lack of progress is to be expected. Several of the Blue mages expressed interest in Coldstepping, though none present have been able to come close to replicating the technique. I of course tried it myself with the assistance of Fi overcharging my reserves. Unfortunately, I haven’t the athleticism to make use of it, despite sometimes being able to conjure stable footholds in midair.

Perhaps Izu will be better able to make use of the technique? It seems to require something like the entirety of my reserves and half that again, so we shall have to see. I note once again that I possess by far the greatest blue affinity in the kingdom. Perhaps with a month or two to train, some of our more skilled mages could…

But I digress. I’ve directed the Blue mages to look elsewhere in their research, lest we waste all our magic on a technique clearly outside of any of our grasp for the time being.

We had the red mages begin practicing katas with weapons. There have been several injuries—some quite severe—though no progress. Holding weapons while under the effects of Invigorate is, as I was forewarned, a recipe for disaster. I wonder how Link managed to master this technique overnight? Clearly it was related to his status as a Lightsworn—a gift from the Goddesses themselves. If only we could find a way to create additional Lightsworn…

I put a halt on these tests for now. It’s simply too dangerous to proceed.

Still a few days remain. I hold out hope that we may yet find our ace in the hole.

…

It pains me, but I cannot in my mind separate all this ancient knowledge from the memory of those who purchased said knowledge with their lives.

Arlinkar, Grenja, Koja, Ryn. The Crown and all of Hyrule thanks you for your service. You will forever be missed.

11th of First Flows:

No meaningful progress, and what’s worse, I have a million other things pressing for my attention. Let us hope tomorrow is better for results.

Just for good measure, I’ve directed about ten percent of the mages of each color to work on trying to recreate elemental rods. We’ll see how the progress goes.

I spoke briefly with Shesan. She has some interesting theories on the nature of magic, but nothing immediately applicable. She maintains that she does not know specifics of “our” magic any more than we do, though claims that it is but one expression of a more fundamental phenomenon inherent to many worlds. She terms it “innate multi-shard end-positive intent-focused investiture.” I have no idea what any of that means, and the examples she shared are, frankly, confusing and alien.

If ever I have more time, I would love to pick her brain on this subject.

12th of First Flows:

More on Red magic: no mage has been able to achieve a partial Transformation. I am told by every single expert on the subject that they believe the tales in the recovered manuscripts to be just that—tales. As it is, it takes many months, if not years to master Transformation. Those capable of such are scarcely willing to try a partial Transformation, and of those none have had any success.

It does appear that substantial Healing can in fact reverse Transformation. This is useful knowledge to be sure, though not immediately applicable. Common practice of such may reduce the widespread fear and mysticism regarding the technique in time. But again, time.

Absolutely zero progress has been made with respect to Stasis. Even the most learned red mages have no clue how to proceed here. Vestele assures me this has been a subject of intense scrutiny for decades, if not centuries. Even given our relative excess of magic and willingness to spend it, our most skilled red mages are still left clueless. We will need more information if any progress is to be made here.

With regard to Blue magic, it’s difficult not to dismiss some parts of the transcripts as more fanciful tales. The ability to turn foes’ blood to acid? Seriously?? I wish I could do that!

The use of Telepathy in conjunction with attacks is a well-known tactic, and common military doctrine besides. It seems little is to be gained here.

As with Invigorate-with-weapons, using Scry during a fight seems to be similarly reckless. I can hardly believe this was once considered not only a viable technique, but the defining characteristic of Blue-enhanced fighters. It is simply baffling how far we have fallen in terms of magical knowledge and ability.

More fanciful tales, or lost knowledge? The ever-present question.

What is to be said of Green magic? The experts among them assure me that there is nothing new to be had with the accounts of Healing magic. Additionally, I am told that Soothe has precisely zero combat potential, despite any assertions to the contrary. It simply requires too much time and too much magic to be used in an offensive capacity. It is best relegated to more subtle applications on unwary foes.

As well, we have had a substantial number of Green mages attempting to use Luck in unconventional ways as laid out by Master Zobor’s accounts. I can’t say there’s been much of any progress here, either. Master Zobor’s Opportunity may as well be a completely different type of magic.

—Which makes some sort of sense, given the difference in name. I have to wonder how this came to be? His descriptions of its use are also at odds with common understanding of Luck. Specifically, he seems much more blaise about actually relying on it, while subtly implying that he’s not relying on it at all and that instead it’s all down to his own skill and prowess. Sort of—a mindset of knowing it’s there but assuming ahead of time that victory is all but assured? I wonder if that’s an important component?

Regardless, it’s difficult to measure “progress” of any sort in this area. How does one measure precisely how much more “lucky” one green mage is from another? How does one measure the difference between a single green mage, given the ill-defined nature of “changing mindsets” and the vague direction they’ve been given? In absolute terms, the difference in martial outcomes we’ve seen does not appear to be worth speaking of.

What else? The interplay between Foretelling and Luck is well known, but not well understood. I’ve directed the first group of both the Blue and Green camps to explore this in more detail with their remaining time. If we learn something groundbreaking there, that may actually give us a leg up over the Blin, who have their own mages making use of both techniques.

13th of First Flows:

I am at a loss. No results worth mentioning, and the main army is due the day after tomorrow!

Hylia help us. I cannot bring myself to admit this entire venture has been in vain.

Guard Captain Izu.

Laylith immediately set down her spyglass and made her way down to meet him at the northeastern gate.

Izu was right, though; Laylith’s place was at the Queen’s side.

From Grandmistress Lyr’s accounting, Blue mages of antiquity such as Farin the Fearless were a marvel to behold in action. The mere thought of anyone mastering the technique of Coldstepping to the degree described in those manuscripts sent chills down Izu’s spine.

He was a practical man, however, and he knew full well that neither he nor anyone else in the kingdom would ever approach that level of mastery—not even if the youngest, most talented mage spent the better part of their natural lifetime training. Gone were the days of legendary warriors—barring more literal divine intervention at any rate, and banking on that would not exactly be the wisest move, to put it delicately.

But again, Izu was nothing if not a practical man. He did not need to master the technique. He merely needed to pull off one good trick, to help him win one duel. As for divine intervention? He had the next best thing: the assistance of Fi.

So then the question became: how to use Coldstepping most effectively? An aggressive, frontal assault would be worthless—even attacking from above would not offer him much advantage other than a momentary surprise. No; he’d have to get in, underneath his enemy’s guard, and then surprise the Blin Warlord with a trick he could not possibly expect.

That was the idea, at least. Theory always proved messy in practice. This was no exception.

To preserve magic, Izu set up static rigging to give him the footholds needed to perform the maneuver. Run. Foretelling. Avoid and duck. Get inside guard. Leap. Spring off his right foot into a crouch, and then leap into a somersault that would carry him in an arc above his target.

Izu practiced the movement a dozen times, adjusted the rigging here and there, and then practiced a dozen times more with soldiers swinging at him, until he felt confident he could pull off the trick in a real fight. Only then did he practice using magic, and not even his own at first. There were plenty of other mages with stronger affinities around, and so he had them focus on creating the requisite two footholds in precisely the right locations at precisely the right times.

Even then, it took six such mages working in concert. Four to supply the reserves, and one for each foothold. Unlike himself, they could not be augmented by Fi—not without severe losses in efficiency from her unique magical conversion.

Result: two attempts, two successes. With the live-magic practice down, and having proved the efficacy of the technique to himself, only then did he move to using his own reserves.

Focus. Breathe.

Izu fell into stance, then darted forward. His opponent, armed with a halberd. went for an overhead strike that left him precious little room to maneuver.

No matter; he saw the path he needed to take four steps in advance. The first step held short. The second dug in. The third redirected, and the fourth brought him in a heartbeat after the swing cleared.

Mentally, he prepared himself for the vertigo he knew he was about to experience. He focused his magic, summoning a foothold.

Leap. Mind becomes blank; empty.

Breathe. Bank. Float.

A sudden infusion of magic cascaded throughout his being, and Izu summoned the second foothold immediately thereafter, curling up and springing from it with all his might.

The trajectory carried him in an arc over his opponent. A tap to the head with his training sword concluded the test.

The men and women around him erupted into cheers as Izu landed and recovered. Several took the occasion to swarm him, crying out wildly at his success. Aside from an easy smile and a thumbs-up, he paid them no mind, staying focused on the task at hand.

“That’s not all, folks,” he said loudly over the throng. “Back in position! I want to try it a couple more times before we call it quits.”

Begrudgingly, they obeyed his commands. A new sparring partner lined up across from him. This man was a few inches taller than the last, and had greater reach. His weapon of choice: the waraxe.

Izu took his own place and nodded. The man returned the gesture, signalling he was ready to begin.

Three deep, calm breaths to center himself. Izu lined up the attack and charged. His opponent took his best swing, but Izu’s magically augmented instincts carried him under the axehead with a slight crouch and a roll of his left shoulder. Shoving into his opponent for extra momentum, he launched himself forward and summoned the first of the two footholds to spring off of.

Leap, bank, second hold, crouch, jump! He adjusted the angle slightly, this time turning to clear the back of his opponent, who was left wide-open for a coup de grâce.

Perfection. Once again, all those around let out cries of encouragement. Izu acknowledged them with a wave, but wasted no time setting up for a third and final attempt.

His last sparring partner was a squat and bulky man, wielding a greatsword. Just as before, Izu lined up and nodded. His partner thrust his sword into the air, then adopted a high guard.

Deep breath. Despite himself, Izu’s mind felt clouded. He was beginning to feel the edges of exhaustion pressing in.

No matter. This was the last attempt. He had to make it count.

He dropped into stance and bolted forward. This time his opponent opted to observe Izu and wait to attack until the last second.

Hah. That was a fatal mistake. Every step carried Izu closer to his target, pruning off possibilities of attack until precious few remained. Izu got just within range, feinted a half-step, then closed before the man could react. Izu pressed his own sword into the man to create distance in passing (nevermind the damage such a drawing attack could inflict), and set himself up.

Summon, leap—

Vertigo. Even with Fi overcharging his reserves, the expenditure left him feeling drained and disoriented. Then came a sudden surge of energy midair as she infused him with even more magic!

Second foothold. Izu coiled his legs and sprung, but struck at the wrong angle. The result? He didn’t “somersault,” so much as lunge backward and crash into his training partner, knocking them both to the ground.

Frustration. If that were the Blin Warlord, he would currently be having his skull caved in.

Izu sighed, then dusted himself off and helped his partner to his feet. Result: two successes, one failure. And he desperately needed to rest.

He supposed he could call upon Fi to restore his energy as he had seen her do several times on the road with Zelda and Arlinkar, but even if he did so… this was it. All the magic they had budgetted for. Every bit that could be spared for this training session had been spent. Any more, and they risked cutting into siege preparation or not having enough for the actual fight.

Izu was not entirely happy with the results, but it was something. A trick to keep in his back pocket, should he need it. Goddesses willing, the BMOTS would be enough. But if it wasn’t?

The question remained: how much of a margin did he need to leave himself? If he could pull off the Coldstep maneuver mid-fight, it would all-but guarantee his victory.

But so would the BMOTS. That was, if the timing worked out in his favor and everything went according to plan. But that was in fact a risk, and a practical man did not leave anything to chance when the fate of the entire kingdom hung in the balance.

Should he attempt Coldstepping in the duel, he might succeed. Or he could royally screw it up, getting himself killed in the process and leaving the city to burn and tens of thousands to die.

Izu looked to the sky, entirely ignoring those around him. Dawn was approaching, and a single day remained. He would think on it. For now, he decided to rest.

“The plan will work, Wymar! You don’t have to worry about me!”

“Please, Zelda, you must see reason! This is not a matter of appearances—it is my solemn duty to ensure your survival. No matter what transpires, our kingdom will still need its Queen to lead it.”

“As if there will be a kingdom to lead if Castletown falls!”

As soon as the words left her mouth, Zelda regretted having spoken them. Even if only top military brass were present, she could stand to be more discerning. Normally she would be more given to consideration, but in this moment she felt hot and flustered, her blood boiling with anger and frustration. No matter what Wymar said, she couldn’t give in. Couldn’t he see—

“Your Grace?” a messenger boy interrupted their row, approaching her and the Lord General sheepishly.

Oh damnation, Zelda thought. Where did he come from?

His proportions varied substantially from Hylians, with massive forearms and upper body in comparison to his relatively small legs—even if said legs were as thick as Zelda’s waist.

She placed a hand on her chest. Marvelous. Beautiful. Breathtaking. Here, a Goron stood before her—true to life, straight out of the history books!

As in ages past, we Gorons will stand with the Hylians, so long as you—Zelda, Queen—are willing to uphold the blood-oath your ancestors swore to mine.”

Right on cue, or thereabouts.

Zelda said a silent prayer in thanks to the Goddesses. Link was alive! This was good news.

…News she would have to process later.

Izu glanced over the faces of those present. The Queen, the Lord General, several Sheikah, and every other leader and military strategist on hand, offering their perspective and advice on how to proceed.

All waiting on him to make his decision.

The options were simple enough. When and where to strike?

Plan A. There were three secret exits Izu and a small team could take to leave Castletown and get in position behind enemy lines once the Blin settled. Assuming they stopped to rest overnight before attacking—which was a very reasonable assumption, though by no means assured—and further, if their intelligence could spot the Warlord in time…

That would allow for a surgical strike, and if they succeeded, it would result in the fewest casualties.

However, the plan was anything but assured. It had several points of failure, not the least of which was actually getting to the Warlord alive and in time to challenge him to Gugwayah before the attack. Despite the plan’s relative simplicity, Izu and his team might not get the chance.

(It would also be nearly impossible for him and the others to escape after the fact, even if they succeeded. Everyone fully acknowledged Plan A as a suicide mission. But if his life was the cost of ensuring Hyrule’s victory, Izu would gladly pay that price.)

Plan B. Wait for the Blin to attack, and have Izu and a squadron in position to meet the Warlord wherever he be—whether on the field, at the gates, or back at camp. This would definitely be more reliable in terms of actually getting to the Warlord, but came at a terrible cost. Many thousands of Hylians would die in the defense, and Castletown itself would suffer unimaginable damage.

What’s more, they had no idea how the remaining Blin forces would react, especially in the midst of battle. If they collapsed into anarchy as expected, the defenders would likely win and eventually route their foes. —But even then, the cost would be immense.

And if the Blin persisted in fighting? Well, there was always Plan Z: Retreat to the castle and burn the city to the ground. Unpleasant to consider, but it would work. The city had in fact been built with this tactic in mind, reserved for a situation exactly as dire as this.

Plan C. The third and final option would be for Izu and a contingent to wait within the walls, but strike out against the Blin Warlord preemptively as soon as his location was spotted within the sieging horde; a sort of hybrid approach between the first two.

Izu had to admit this was more favorable in absolute terms than trusting him and a small team to work a miracle, but it still relied on intel finding the Warlord before the fighting began. Taking this option also meant they could fall back on Plan B if they could not find and engage the Warlord in time, and further, if they were to succeed, the Blin would still be outside the walls. Which meant it would be that much easier to rout them should they lose coordination than should the same happen in the midst of battle.

Less people would have to die with Plan C than with Plan B, but essentially none of those sent out in the offensive contingent would be expected to return, regardless of the outcome of the actual duel. It would also leave considerably less forces available for carrying out fallback plans (such as burning the Blin alive within the city) should Izu fail.

The Lord General and his staff had signed off on all three plans, leaving the choice to Izu and Izu alone. Whichever he personally felt most likely to succeed.

It was his mission, after all.

“So, then?” Wymar asked, bringing Izu back to the moment. “What shall it be?”

Izu weighed his options carefully, knowing full well that the fate of the entire kingdom rested on his shoulders.
