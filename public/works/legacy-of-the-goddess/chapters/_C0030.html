24th of First Flows:

Can it be? Am I finally getting back to research? It feels like it’s been ages.

We’re headed for a military outpost / supply depot near Lake Hylia. Some weeks ago I arranged for a handful of Blin to be captured and held there, intending to observe them and learn more of their culture and language. …And perhaps use them as test subjects for Fi to perform more dangerous experiments regarding Old Magic.

I had also hoped to study Soulsteel directly, but capturing and holding any Blin capable of summoning forth those vile weapons has proved far too dangerous for anyone involved. I have been informed that in the various attempts, there have been fourteen deaths and six maimings.

…

My heart weeps for the brave souls who gave their lives in hopes of learning more about our enemy. As I have no new ideas for how to improve upon previous attempts by Wymar and his men, I cannot in good conscience order anyone else to try. Taking the creatures captive is easy enough, as they cannot summon their weapons while unconscious. However, no matter their restraint—short of hacking off their limbs—those that possess the power to summon Soulsteel can do so at will. They draw the weapons forth from their own bodies, and can seemingly do so from any point. This makes holding them against their will for any length of time an exercise in futility. And once they break loose…

What we do know:

By and large, the shape and form of Soulsteel weapons would not be effective in the traditional sense. That is, if they were made from normal materials, their various notches, protrusions, odd angles, spikes, etc., would significantly hamper their function and severely reduce their offensive potential. Of course, this does not matter in the slightest when any part of the blade can effortlessly cleave through imbued steel. It’s almost like they were designed first and foremost to look as menacing as possible—and on that count, they readily succeed.

According to some reports, the weapons seem to drink in the light around them, like casting reverse shadows. I haven’t seen any myself, so I can only speculate. They disappear into puffs of smoke moments after they are dropped, or if their wielder is killed or incapacitated. And if any Hylian attempts to hold one before it dissipates, they immediately seize up with painful convulsions. The experience isn’t fatal as far as we know, just agonizing and harrowing. Those unlucky few who have gone through the spell describe it as feeling like their soul is burning up from the inside, very similar to accounts of Bloodrage. I do wonder if there are any long-term effects from this (or anything Fi could detect?), but all indications point towards ‘no,’ at least for a single, short exposure.

As for the captive Blin, they were taken well before the Soulsteel ritual, and have remained isolated in the intervening time. No Soulsteel, no Bloodrage—just some relatively harmless Bokoblins. I plan to set Fi to observe them as soon as we arrive.

Addendum:

After sharing with Fi, she posits an interesting hypothesis: that Blin communicate with one another by building up a “semantic web” using word pairs. That is, they have a root word that represents an object, and they state it repeatedly alongside qualities that object possesses. Once they’ve described two objects and their relevant details, they then describe how one object relates to another.

If true, that would actually make a lot of sense! Even if the less intelligent of their kind can barely hold two thoughts in their head at the same time, the more intelligent ones can still communicate complex ideas to them if they try long and hard enough. How fascinating! I had never considered that language could be so varied.

Even if we are unlikely to make more progress right away, I can’t imagine Fi will have any difficulty learning and translating their language should we need her to. She concurs with this assessment, reminding me that communication is one of her primary functions.


Having the better part of a day to kill (Fi is busy observing Link), there’s an idea I’ve been meaning to try for a while, using Foretelling in conjunction with Scrying. Essentially, one splits up an area to search using a grid on a map. One then uses Foretelling to try and discern whether or not Scrying into any given area would be successful before actually taking the time to Scry, thereby saving considerable time and effort.

My initial tests were a resounding success! I had a soldier take a painted red rock, roll a die, and then go in one of the cardinal directions about a five hundred meters out, placing the rock in a visible location. In four trials, I was able to locate the rock on the first try. And just to make sure there weren’t any confounding factors with the shards of Wisdom or my connection to Fi or anything like that, I had Laylith do the same. She succeeded twice in a row.

Unfortunately, that’s where we ran into problems.

We tried altering the parameters of the experiment by having the soldier choose for himself which direction to go and place the stone. In this trial… it’s difficult to explain, but Foretelling just didn’t give the same feeling, and neither of us were able to do better than chance. We also tried having the four soldiers roll a dice to see who gets the stone before each of them walked off to their designated locations. Same result: nothing. And predictably, doing the same but having them choose amongst themselves also failed.

I am forced to conclude that anything involving people and decision making is just too complex for Foretelling to give useful results.

Oh well. It was worth a shot. I’m out of ideas, so I think I’ll just relax for the rest-

26th of First Flows:

Just as we were preparing to turn in last night, that’s when it hit me. Literally!

I was sitting under a tree with Sera and Laylith, idly chatting and enjoying the sunset, when a warblenut fell from the tree and landed in my cup, knocking it out of my hand and spilling tea everywhere.

It was in that moment I had a brilliant idea for what I am calling the “Technique of Impending Doom!”

(Muahaha.)

Despite the name, it’s not all that complicated or dangerous. Setup: the same experiments as above with Foretelling and Scrying. However, the practitioner sits under a pot of water. The pot has a support mechanism that is rigged to fail after a set amount of time. In this case—having limited materials on hand—I used a constant flow of water into a basin on a scale, offset by a known weight. When the basin fills to the correct height, the scale tips and tugs on some twine, which causes a stick supporting the pot to fall, thus dumping cold water all over me.

Foretelling while sitting under the pot with the mechanism active (with the intention of staying there the entire time) produces a constant, unsettling feeling. Obviously, the timer must be long enough to complete the task, but the longer the timer, the fainter the feeling. I conjecture that a more pressing danger would produce a stronger effect, but I’m also not about to hang a sword over my head or anything. I found that with practice and familiarity, it’s not too difficult to pick out.

The key is this: after using Scry to locate that which one desires to locate, one then has reason to move out of danger. This ties the feedback from Foretelling not to the decisions of others, but to events happening entirely in the immediate vicinity and within one’s own mind. In other words, well within the boundaries that Foretelling is known to work in.

I am delighted to report that with a bit of practice I was able to execute the technique reliably, and used it to succeed on all the prior tests! Admittedly, this isn’t all that useful for searching between four fixed locations—simply Scrying and moving the focus is much faster and less effortful. But this is where the technique shines! We scaled up the experiment, subdividing the outpost and the surrounding fishing village into sixteen regions, each about a hundred meters apart. Sixteen soldiers then chose amongst themselves which of them were to wear a colorful bandana, then each went somewhere within their designated subarea.

It took a while to calibrate and find a suitable cadence, striking a balance between leaving enough time for the trial but still having strong enough feedback. I really must stress the importance of this: after a while, it becomes very easy to imagine the feeling of unease and delude oneself into thinking it is feedback from Foretelling. In testing, I found that five-second intervals (plus a comfortable buffer) was enough to reliably perform the ritual on each subarea and clear the mind before moving on to the next. I also had to use a metronome to ensure I kept the correct pacing, and it was quite difficult to focus on everything all at once.

Once the kinks were worked out, as before I was able to locate the target correctly on the first try: number ten. Confident in my success, we then scaled up and did it again, this time with thirty-two subareas (though we had to rope in some villagers to help). Again, resounding success: number twenty-six!

…And here’s where we run into the limitations. That is to say, the same limitation as always: availability of magic. The first batch of tests exhausted my own reserves and all those of the local mages via Siphon (including Cove and Laylith). On top of that, I required Fi’s assistance during the sixteen and thirty-two trials.

The sixteen trial, I’m pretty sure I could do on my own if I started from full reserves, as could most skilled practitioners, particularly those with higher affinities. However, even I needed Fi to have any hope of completing the thirty-two trial, thus making it completely infeasible for anyone else to attempt, let alone succeed.

(There is the possibility of using Siphon in the middle of the technique, but given the already difficult nature and the required focus of the practitioner, I can’t imagine that would work well.)

There also doesn’t seem to be any way to cheat the scheme with clever tricks, such as progressively splitting the search area into halves and only considering each half at a time. If it worked that way, one would only need five iterations to search an entire size thirty-two grid. Alas, after drenching myself half a dozen times, I’m calling it quits for now.

It seems that the act of actually locating the target via Scrying is what provides the sense of relief (or rather, Foretelling picking up on the imminent possibility of doing so just before you actually do it). Therefore, if the subarea to be searched is half the entire area, there isn’t much advantage to using the technique versus just Scrying the whole area to begin with. One is therefore limited to fixed areas and regular intervals. That is to say, it doesn’t matter which order you consider them in, just that you actually consider each of them, and give yourself enough time to do so.

To summarize: if one has excess magic to burn, then I have fairly conclusively proven that the Technique of Impending Doom can considerably cut down on the time needed to exhaustively Scry a large area by a factor proportional to the number of subareas. However, it is rarely if ever the case that anyone has excess magic, but not enough time. Thus—as is the case with so many of my clever ideas—it solves the wrong problem.

I swear, I am cursed.
