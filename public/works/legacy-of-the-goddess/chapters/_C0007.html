Zelda sat atop a guard tower at the edge of the castle grounds, looking out over her city. The crackling firepit nearby took the bite out of the cool, Brisk air. She held her furs tight.

She was alone now. Well, as alone as she ever was—Link and Cove were nearby, as were her guard detail. But she was unattended for the moment, and she desperately needed the solitude. So much was required of her in day-to-day life. So many were demanding of her time and attention. Petitioners, guards, counselors, attendants, and other castle staff. It seemed scarcely an hour went by without someone looking for her. Without someone needing something. Not an hour without some urgent and important decision needing to be made that very instant, lest everything fall apart and burst into flames and be ruined forever. How could anyone be expected to make headway in that kind of environment?

She let out a sigh, toying with the empty ring on her finger. What would Father do?

He’d be proactive, she knew. He’d take initiative in a bold and daring way that solved a dozen problems at once, and everyone would heap love and adulation on him for his swift and decisive action.

As she looked out over the city, she noticed the refuse was gone. That was a relief. Even so, it frustrated her to admit she couldn’t have done better. That she wouldn’t have been able to solve the problem as quickly or as completely herself, even had it been her sole focus. Despite her misgivings, Taetus was undeniably making progress. She didn’t like him, nor his methods, but perhaps she shouldn’t be so quick to judge? True; he was greedy, self-serving, conniving, duplicitous… but also nothing if not bold, daring, and decisive when he needed to be. She couldn’t help but see the best parts of Father in him.

Perhaps she should put more work towards bolstering morale now that the situation was improving? But truth be told, the fear of another assassination attempt unnerved her. And it wasn’t just her own life she had to worry about. Others had already died in her defense, and to capriciously ignore that threat would be unwise. She could assign more Sheikah to her personal guard, but she also knew that they were tasked with mitigating against the marauding Blin and bandits. Pulling more of them away only meant more people would die. It meant more damage to villages, farms, and infrastructure. A deeper hole her people would have to dig their way out of.

No. That wasn’t worth it.

Link hadn’t been happy with the number of people at the Remembrance Day ceremony at the castle grounds—and she hadn’t been either—but both Lensa and Vestele insisted. Could she find reason to do more things like that? Despite how stressful the event was, they assured her it went a long way towards showing her subjects they were cared for. And that meant a lot to her. She did care about them. More than anything, she just wanted her kingdom to prosper. For her people to live long, healthy, happy lives. She certainly didn’t want them to live in fear. She wanted so badly to meet with each and every one of them. To tell them it would all be okay—that everything would be okay, and that they would pull through. To comfort them, like she had done with that child and her mother.

Would they be okay, though? She honestly wasn’t sure. Famine still loomed on the horizon. And after hearing all those names… Despite her convictions, some part of her felt like they had already lost. There was just so much sorrow in the world. And so much uncertainty, too. In the face of all that, and in the face of what her life demanded of her, it was too easy to want to curl up into herself and hide away. To do otherwise hurt in a way she didn’t know how to deal with. To do otherwise meant she had to face that darkness head on, and acknowledge that it might not actually be okay.

The thought terrified her. How long had it been since the Old Kingdom? How many had been claimed by the Blights already? How many had died trying to stop them, and how much had been lost in perpetually keeping them at bay? She couldn’t help but feel like her people were destined to lose. Like their world was slowly crumbling away over the eons as a force beyond her reckoning tore at its seams.

Her thoughts drifted to her conversation with Vestele. What had defeated Hylia, all those ages ago? And how could anyone stand against something that could kill a goddess?

Zelda remembered the moment she shared with Link last night, looking out over the pyres spread throughout the city. She remembered the resolve she felt then. That urge to action. She felt it even now, when she called it up. She knew on some level that they had to persist—that they had no other option. That despite it all, she had to keep moving. That she had to do what she could—had to play her part. They had to end the Blights, at any cost.

But what did that really mean, in practical terms? It was easy to feel that resolve, but it was another thing entirely to channel it into action. Even more still to do so consistently, in the face of unending hardship.

A definite step forward would be to ensure Link also bore Hylia’s mark. Another “Champion” building on their Divine Power reserves could only help. There were so many things she wanted to try, and Fi never had enough reserves. Fi seemed confident however that she had almost enough power to do it. Just a few more days…

Zelda let her mind wander, and found herself fiddling with with her bracers. What else could she do right now? She was reminded of Impa’s words: Nayru’s wisdom will guide you.

Did her recent nightmares mean anything? Impa would have thought so. Probably. But if they did, Zelda didn’t know what. The details varied, but… souls of the dead… plunging into the River Hylia?

She shuddered at a passing breeze and inched closer to the fire.

There was the war effort. Could she go to the front? Her presence there might inspire the troops. And if her goal was to end the Blights, there certainly wasn’t anything more direct than actually fighting against the Blin. But then again, Wymar never once proposed the idea to her. If he had thought doing so was a viable strategy, surely he would have suggested it? Besides, any real action she took there would likely just result in her getting in the way. And the last thing Wymar needed was to worry after her safety while fighting back the Blin.

She cursed under her breath. It felt like that was all she was ever doing—getting in the way. Her greatest accomplishment thus far was effectively standing aside and delegating the rule of her city to Taetus. Maybe Lensa was right? Maybe her part in all of this was just being a glorified figurehead and doing her best to distract everyone else while the actually competent leaders made progress.

But the thought of that just made her sad. No; she had to be able to do more. She wouldn’t be able to live with herself otherwise.

Idly, she wondered if the Lynx had gotten her message yet. She should probably follow up with Lensa. It was a stretch, but maybe—just maybe—their salvation laid in forging an alliance with an outside force. Especially one that might be able to shed light on the assassins, the Exiled King, and the matter of the cooperating Warlords. And if they could reestablish trade with whatever stable Gerudo blocs still existed, that might go a ways towards forestalling famine.

The more she thought about it, the more important that seemed—especially in light of recent revelations. Perhaps she should go to Last Bastion herself for a face-to-face meeting with the Lynx? Or would that be too drastic? Wouldn’t that put her at greater risk? And what would that do for morale here in the city? She wasn’t sure. Something to consider, at least.

…Later.

Right now, she just wanted to be alone.

Zelda’s Research Notes - The Triforce

The Triforce is undoubtedly the most powerful artifact in existence, though surprisingly little is known about its true nature. Vestele tells me it was created by the Golden Goddesses themselves, and left to Hylia after their work was complete. The Order of Sages claims it to be the ultimate source of all magic. It is theorized to take the following forms:

1. As a unified whole which—supposedly—would grant the wielder the full power of the Golden Three, and thus the ability to recreate the world according to their heart’s desire.

2. As three “Pieces”: “Wisdom”, “Power”, and “Courage.”

3. As “Fragments” or “Shards”: Each piece can be further divided into nine fragments. Fragments are known to magnify the power of their wielder, or allow them access to lesser abilities related to the powers the three whole pieces bestow.

Supposedly each piece or fragment can only be wielded by individuals attuned to their essence. They also have arcane rules governing their possession, and have been known to "seek out" their owners. I do not know precisely what any of this means nor what such entails (and Vestele assures me I am not alone in that), though it does explain certain peculiarities both myself and Link have witnessed.

Wisdom:

The embodiment of the powers of Nayru. Grants cosmic powers of divination. Legends say it allows its wielder to see plainly into the future. It was the first piece to be fragmented and lost to time—the event having occurred before the era of the Old Kingdom. When fragmented, each shard grants lesser abilities related to divination.

1. Wielder gains the ability to scry the location of closely bonded individuals.

2. The wielder may sometimes have prophetic dreams. The dreams are often highly symbolic or metaphorical, and are open to interpretation.

3. Wielder gains the ability to communicate telepathically with closely bonded individuals.

4. Wielder may scry at any recently visited location.

5. Prophetic dreams are more clearly defined.

6-9: ???

Power:

The embodiment of the powers of Din. Grants tremendous strength and endurance, and significantly heightens magical affinity. It was also fragmented well before the current historical record, with countless wars being fought over possession of its fragments. Each fragment considerably boosts the physical and magical prowess of its wielder.

With some number of fragments, supposedly the wielder gains access to an ability similar to “Stasis” (i.e. - The Basilisk’s Gaze). Nothing further is known at present. It is hypothesized that several fragments (or perhaps the full piece) may confer abilities related to Siphon and Transformation.

Courage:

The embodiment of the powers of Farore. This one is the least known, even though supposedly a whole piece was wielded as recently as the time of the Hero’s Stand. The fragments are said to grant "luck," which the wielder experiences as preternatural reflexes and instincts that border on premonition.

Other Notes:

Link possesses one fragment of Courage, I possess three of Wisdom, and Vesyrn (previously Father) possesses one of Power. Nineve of the Zora possesses one of Wisdom. The Gorons possess one of Power. As mentioned elsewhere in my writings, knowledge of the other fragments’ locations have been lost to time.

With eager anticipation, Zelda followed the High Priestess as they descended down a flight of stairs and into a dark, narrow corridor. A dozen robed monks with tattooed faces trailed behind. They walked in pairs, though perhaps four could stand abreast in the hallway.

A short jaunt down the hall, and they came to a halt before a massive iron vault. What secrets lie hidden inside?

Vestele turned to Zelda, handing her a torch. Zelda took it, and Vestele approached the vault. She inserted a long, oddly shaped key into a locking mechanism, then stepped back. Two burly men stepped forward and, with a great deal of effort, spun the contraption a quarter turn counter-clockwise. A loud click reverberated through the hall, and the door swung outward with a protesting creak.

“After you,” Vestele said, motioning.

Zelda stepped through.

Instantly, her breath caught with a gasp. Inside was the most beautiful creature she had ever laid eyes upon. A young woman hung suspended in the air above a stone dais. Frozen; hovering in a beam of light. Her skin radiated a soft white glow.

“Her name is Niesis," Vestele said, stepping up beside Zelda. Behind them, the monks fanned out to surround the dais.

Zelda couldn’t take her eyes away from the woman. Was this what a Fae looked like? “How…?” she asked.

At her side, Vestele offered up a simple scepter. Zelda tore her eyes away from Niesis, taking the item and handing back the torch. It felt cold to the touch; a three-foot long metal rod with a prismatic opal encased in silver at the end.

“This is the artifact the Seekers originally used to seal away the Fae,” Vestele said. “Niesis created it and instructed them in its use, though it’s power has long since faded. She aided them in locating the other Fae, then submitted herself willingly to imprisonment.”

Zelda studied the item, pushing a tiny amount of her own magic into it. Just as Vestele said, she did not sense any energies stored in the scepter. “Can it be recharged?” she asked.

As most knew, ordinary items could be made substantially better by being imbued with magic. A sword would become sharper and take longer to dull, a pen would take longer to run dry, and a torch would burn brighter and longer with a steadier flame. Although Zelda expected the actual truth of the scepter to be more complicated, perhaps the simplest hypothesis was just that it once held a great deal of magic, and that magic had faded over time.

Vestele shook her head. "I don’t believe so. It may be possible, but all knowledge of that sort was destroyed long ago. That isn’t to say we haven’t tried. But simply Imbuing it doesn’t work, nor does leaving it in proximity to fragments. Nor does having a wielder of the fragments imbue it. Believe it or not, we have tried all the obvious ideas.”

Zelda frowned, turning her attention back to the beautiful young woman suspended in the beam of light. Something didn’t add up. “You said she submitted herself willingly to imprisonment,” she said. “Why would she do that?”

“Your guess is as good as mine,” Vestele replied. “Perhaps she knew the threat she and others of her kind posed? As I said, not all Fae were as horrible as Cyrae.”

“But that doesn’t make sense either,” Zelda said. “I get sealing away the malicious ones. But why would Niesis want herself to be imprisoned too?”

“Who’s to say?” Vestele responded. “I’ve pondered it myself. It might not have been her choice? That’s the only explanation I’ve come up with. Perhaps there was some other danger the Fae posed? Or perhaps our predecessors simply viewed them as a threat to the long-term stability of our kingdom.”

Zelda held the scepter out in front of her, examining the opal again. On further inspection, she couldn’t help but notice the strikingly unique array of colors.

“Everyone,” she called, looking out over the room. “I’m going to summon the Emissary of the Holy Mother. Do not be alarmed.” She watched with slight amusement as the monks exchanged glances and murmured amongst themselves. She looked to Vestele. The priestess shrugged uncertainly.

“When you’re ready, Fi.”

“Hello, Mistress,” Fi said, materializing from the shadows before her not a second later.

Vestele spoke up, looking concerned. “Your Grace—if I may ask, what is it you intend?”

Zelda hesitated. She was hoping she wouldn’t encounter resistance here. She looked to Niesis, then to Fi, and then back to Vestele. “If my idea works… I intend to temporarily release the Fae, and see if she can answer some questions.”

“I don’t believe that’s wise,” Vestele said quickly. “Perhaps—”

“—Vestele,” Zelda said firmly, putting all the authority she could muster into her voice. “You said she willingly submitted to imprisonment before. What are you worried about? We’ll just seal her again when we’re done.”

Vestele frowned, looking anxious. “I… don’t know, to be honest,” she said. “I don’t have any definitive concerns. It’s just… Fae should not be underestimated, Your Grace. They were sealed away and imprisoned for a reason.”

Zelda met the priestess’ eyes and took a moment to compose her thoughts.

“And what if that reason was a poor one?” she asked. “What if our ancestors were only acting out of irrational fear? What if Niesis can provide us with invaluable historical and magical knowledge? Knowledge that could prove decisive in combating the Blin? Unearthing hidden secrets? Ending the Blights? Vestele—think about it! There are twelve powerful, trained mages in the room—thirteen if you count myself. Fi can also assist in restoring our magic if need be. I think it’s worth the risk.”

“It’s working!” Zelda exclaimed. She couldn’t believe it. It was actually working!

“Again,” Zelda commanded. “Use three quarters of the remainder. We’ll take a break before moving on to Last Bastion if necessary.”

“As you wish, Mistress,” Fi said. She drew tall again, and the energies swirled. Zelda held the scepter outstretched. She waited anxiously as chromatic vapors flooded into the orb. Its glow returned; intensified. Almost a minute passed before Fi stopped again.

Belatedly, Zelda realized she didn’t actually know how to use the scepter. Fick, fick, fick! Thinking quickly, she rushed over to Niesis, rod extended. Instantly, the opal shattered and a shockwave of energy threw her back. She hit her head and blacked out.

…

From nowhere, a voice spoke.

-o-o-o-

The Void.

Perfect stillness. Absolute emptiness. Unchanging. Forever. Eternal.

Infinite.

Before there was time, before there was space, before there Was, there was the Void.

The Void was all, and the Void was nothing. But how could Nothing be?

The first thought, spoken eons before life—eons before history and outside of time—was one of Division.

And so it was that from Nothing came Creation. And with it…

Destruction.

Two halves of Nothing. Equal and opposite; a paradox and its own resolution; able to exist only because their sum total was still, precisely, Nothing.

-o-

My mind awoke.

Adrift in a sea of stars, I had no focus and no bearing. Before me lay an expanse that I somehow recognized as the whole of the cosmos, stretching from infinity at one end and receding into infinity at the other.

In that expanse, the three layers of reality stood juxtaposed; Divinity, Concept, and Substance. Each a reflection of the others, each flowing, twisting and turning in complex fractal shapes at the borders. I gazed upon the center where all things met, and with a start, I realized something there gazed back at me.

Ageless eons passed in a single ephemeral moment, and I saw how all things were connected, how all things were, and how all manner of things could come to be. In that moment, I knew eternity. I was understanding, and I was perception. I became knowledge. I became Wisdom.

Above me, two brilliant red flames came alight. Entranced, I watched the fires intensify. They grew rapidly, at a frightening pace. Swirling, they came at me. Faster and faster, hotter and brighter, until their radiance outshone all else. I tried to brace myself against the coming shockwave, but before I could react, I was torn apart and engulfed by a heat hotter than any sun.

The whole of my being burst into a raging inferno, wreathed in black flames; a white-hot sphere of condensed fury at its center. Eternal and all-consuming, I burned with raw, crackling energy; will concentrated to the densest substance. I became one with that fire, and it was one with me. It energized me, and drove me to seek perfection. This fire was strength, and it was unity. It was passion and desire; ardor and determination. Raw. Overwhelming. Power.

The flames abated, and an off-white crystalline structure materialized around me. Bones and sinew interwove intricately, a biological scaffolding of bewildering complexity. Muscle fibers stitched together, organs and regulatory systems grew and connected inside, and layers of delicate tan skin folded around it all. My vision retreated to two conical sections emanating from a central locus. The ethereal plane of the divine fell out of my perception, just as the plane of concept became sharper and more focused. I felt a pulsing in my chest and in my ears, and knew that I was alive.

This was a sensation unlike any other. Overwhelmed with emotion, I laughed, and then cried out in shock. With this new form came a mind, and in that mind vast streams of knowledge bent and eddied with the current of my thoughts. But I had not the time to reflect, as the world around me continued to shift. Euphoria and wonder swelled inside my chest as a beautiful green-blue sphere rose from below, expanding to encompass my view.

It was only then that I realized I was falling, and felt a pang of terror. But just as quickly the fear subsided, replaced by a serenity bequeathed by assurances whispered on the winds. Of course, I could not be harmed. This realm and its physical laws were little more than humble suggestions next to my own divine will. Emboldened by Courage, I threw myself at the sky, and flew.

The wind screamed in my ears as I streaked through the clouds. Below, rivers crisscrossed into lakes and estuaries before meeting with a vast ocean expanding in all directions. I watched as mountains jutted out, defiantly presenting their peaks to the sky. Verdant hills came into focus, rising and undulating across the landscape. And with a deafening crack, I slammed into the ground, screaming my elation to the heavens for all the world to hear.

Rising from the small crater left by my descent, my feet brushed lightly against the dew-wet grass. I delighted to feel the softness of its blades between my toes and the coarse earth beneath my feet. With a raised hand I met the sun’s resplendent caress, just as a cool breeze whipped about, tossing my hair and bringing with it scents of charred earth and stone.

Belatedly, the realization struck: I was not alone. I turned sharply to see three majestic feminine silhouettes towering above me. An instant of recognition, and I fell to my knees in reverence of their divine glory. I knew they had been with me this whole time. They’d moulded me; shaped me from the Void itself. They were my mothers, my creators, my progenitors.

Nayru, Goddess of Wisdom. Din, Goddess of Power. And Farore, Goddess of Courage.

-o-o-o-

Zelda came to in the arms of one of the monks, with Vestele kneeling at her side. The priestess was saying something, but she couldn’t make out the words. Zelda held a hand to her forehead, trying to gather her bearings.

She looked over to the dais, but Niesis was nowhere to be found.
