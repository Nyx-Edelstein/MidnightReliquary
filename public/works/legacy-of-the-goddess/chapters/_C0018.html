The Gauntlet of Winds—as Link affectionately referred to the absurdly arduous ordeal—was not nearly as long as he remembered. Unencumbered by gale-force winds blowing in his face, the trek through the length of now stationary obstacles was short and uneventful.

He was glad to have that trial behind him.

Making his way through the open doorway, he found himself in the West Mural Room. There was a nearly identical East Mural Room nearby, at the far end of a short hall. Link had been here before, but tired and weary as he was at the time, he had not stopped to properly admire the artwork. After a surprisingly good night’s rest, he figured he might as well take a moment to do so before pressing on with his quest to unlock the secrets of this place.

The walls of both mural rooms were lined with statues of the Goddesses, figurines, fountains, and other varied tokens of religious imagery. Link felt somewhat guilty for not carting at least a few of the items back to be examined by the historians and scholars of the world—and Zelda particularly. But realistically, they couldn’t be expected to carry the entire complex back with them, could they? They had not planned to haul any amount of cargo on the return trip, and it was going to be difficult enough to manage several books and the fragments of the worm-thing’s carapace.

With luck, there would be ample time for that later when countless lives were not at stake. With luck, they could figure out how to make more Lightsworn, and then the scholars could come here themselves and catalogue every speck of dust while he went on to do more important things.

Link took a look around the room with fresh eyes. He really wished Zelda could see this. The sheer detail that went into these murals was mesmerizing. He was certainly no artist, and attempting to sketch a facsimile that did any of it justice would be an exercise in futility. Every face and every figure had to have been painstakingly crafted over the course of days by a master painter.

The mural on the northern wall was his favorite. It depicted the Goddess Hylia blessing a single kneeling warrior with a vast army gathered at his back. Tall and majestic, Hylia shone with an otherworldly radiance befitting none other than the Mother of Creation. The kneeling warrior wielded a gleaming sword with a blue hilt—a sword that glowed with a holy light, surpassed only by the radiance of the Mother Goddess herself.

There was no way that was anything but the Sword of Evil’s Bane! Its inclusion in the mural here proved beyond a shadow of a doubt that it was real and not just a story. Link couldn’t wait to throw this back in Shesan’s face.

But enough dawdling; he could worry about that later. Right now there was work to do.

Halfway down the short hall towards the eastern room was a green-runed door that barred the way to the next part of the complex. Link supplied a green gem, and the runes on the door pulsed. The gem spun and the door slid upward… to reveal another green-runed door.

Thankfully Link had taken the time to “solve” the puzzle in the sandpit room. He supplied a second green gemstone and the second door receded. Beyond the door was a flight of stairs.

Only three levels to go.

Judging by the view from the outside, the worst of it was behind him; the structure narrowed considerably towards the higher tiers. Link estimated he had traversed well over half the complex by now. What more could it throw at him?

He ascended the stairs. A towering statue of Hylia stood at the center of the room, and there was another mural on the far wall. In the mural, two warriors stood back-to-back, facing away towards either side of a branching path. One wore robes and their hands glowed with magic. The other wore armor and brandished a sword. Barring the way of both were stylized figures of three monstrous foes.

Link looked to his left and to his right. To either side of the room were massive runed doors. Each glowed with a faint white light.

A sense of dread rose up from within.

Two warriors; two paths. Three foes to either side. This was the final challenge, wasn’t it? He was to choose a path, then face off with a series of powerful monsters to prove his worth as a Lightsworn.

Was he ready?

Link couldn’t help but imagine the corpse of Geira’s avatar behind the massive ornate door, except less dead.

Surely the threats ahead wouldn’t be that dire. How bad could they be? Nothing else in this place had proved to be all that dangerous…

He thought back to the three Stalfos that had ambushed him earlier. He had burned the entirety of his reserves in that fight, and even then suffered a nasty cut that required immediate attention. Had that played out differently—had he chosen a different approach to the second wind tunnel and had just a little less magic to rely on…

He glanced down at his forearm and flexed his hand. The sutured and mended wound still ached and itched slightly.

Three foes. Each of them more, or less dire than three Stalfos?

Probably more. That gave him pause.

There was also the Stalnox to judge by. That was a harrowing fight. Could he expect much worse than it? Alone he stood no chance against the monstrosity, but the raw power of the transfabrulator proved decisive—once Link figured out how to use it.

—Surely the creators of this place had taken that into account? There was no way he would be able to just use a magic gizmo supplied to him earlier on in order to brute-force his way through the trials ahead. Anyone could do that. If that was the case, what would be the point of having the trials in the first place?

…Or maybe that was the point? One of the murals in the previous room featured a warrior in a green tunic holding the transfabrulator, surrounded by the mysterious blocks it produced. A series of images and runes around the mural formed the basis of Fi’s understanding of the device. This was a training ground after all—probably. It would make sense if the trials ahead were just an exercise to further train prospective Lightsworn in use of the ancient weapon.

But if that were the case, there was also the mural with the Master Sword to consider, displayed just as prominently in the opposite room. Link’s longsword was among the finest quality in the kingdom, but it was certainly not a mythical blade capable of banishing evil to the depths of the Void. Would the Sword of Evil’s Bane be required to proceed?

Surely not. Surely…

He was probably overthinking it. Then again, he had to admit—he wasn’t even sure there was anything else to be found here. He had already found the transfabrulator, and Geira’s avatar was dead. Sure, the ancient Lightsworn structure was a trove of forgotten knowledge and all—but was there anything actually useful beyond these trials? Anything that would aid them in the fight against Ganondorf? Against Demise?

It seemed unlikely. It was possible, yes, but did it make sense to bet his life on it?

Link found his resolve wavering. He had only persisted this far because…

He glanced up at the statue of Hylia at the center of the room. Something stirred at the back of his mind.

Was this fate? Was this destiny?

He closed his eyes, clenching his fragment of the Triforce of Courage in his hand. It felt silly, but—

—No. He had to have faith. Faith had gotten him this far, hadn’t it?

He approached the statue and knelt.

“Dear Hylia,” he whispered. “Holy Mother. Goddess Above. I know you’re not even around anymore. Everyone tells me you died long ago. Even if that’s true, I still have to wonder—what’s all of this for? Why am I here now? It’s too much to be a coincidence… everything that’s happened… you had to have planned it…”

Link looked around the empty room as he fumbled for words. He was expecting to feel the same welling of intensity that he had felt so many times before. He was hoping to feel the same conviction that had driven him to stand before the Warlord half a lifetime ago. But as he spoke the words now, they felt cold and empty.

Who was he kidding? Before now, he would have sworn to anyone that he felt Hylia’s guiding hand urging him on. But now?

He looked up again at that motionless, dead statue in this long-forgotten place.

He looked over to his right, at the looming door.

“I only ever believed because I had to,” he confessed. “I have to be your champion… Because otherwise, I’m just a man.”

It hurt to admit, but it was true. He could really die here, and he couldn’t even say for sure that it was worth the risk.

But he couldn’t turn back either, could he? Not now. What would he tell the others? What would he tell Zelda?

He had to keep going. He had no choice. There wasn’t anyone else who could. For all he knew, there could be a whole cache of ancient Lightsworn artifacts at the top level of the structure, and if he gave up now, then he bore sole responsibility for everything they could have helped avert.

He felt sick. Why did it all have to be so absurd? Was a sign too much to ask for?

“Just in case you are still listening,” he muttered. “In case you do have a hand in this still… I could really use some help right about now.”

Fi appeared at his side. “I would be happy to help, Master. What is it you need assistance with?”

Link blinked. “Fi, I thought you couldn’t manifest an avatar here?”

Her colors shifted. “Apologies; the interference field does not appear to be active in this location. I was uncertain as to whether I should bring this detail to your attention immediately, but ultimately decided to delay, as I did not judge it to be immediately relevant, and further expected it would distract you from your introspection. Was my judgement in error?”

Link snorted, then burst out laughing.

“Master, I am confused; I made no attempt at humor with my previous statement. My models suggest—”

“—No, Fi, it’s fine,” Link said, wiping his eyes. “It’s just…” He waved a hand. “It’s not important. You can restore my magic and stamina now, yeah? I mean, if I need you to?”

“Yes, Master. Of course.”

Link grinned. Why had he ever doubted?

Hmm. No wind.

No bugs either.

Was there some kind of puzzle he needed to solve first? Wary, Link crept over to the nearest boulder and tapped it with the transfabrulator.

Nothing.

He had chosen to be the hero.

Do you have what it takes to be the hero, Link?

“Goddess Above,” he whispered, feeling once more the conviction burning within. “I will be your champion.”

There was nowhere to hide, and his enemy was likely to come up from below. Link took his sword in one hand and the transfabrulator in the other. At the first sign of movement, he’d…

The hair on his skin stood up. What was that sound?

If he wasn’t looking for it, he might have missed it. On the far side of the room, two mounds of dirt shifted.

Link wasted no time, manifesting a cube and climbing on top. The mounds shifted again as he moved, faster this time, picking up speed as they went. One snaked around to his left while the other went to the center of the room.

They were moving in for a pincer attack. Not good. Link’s heart thrummed in his chest. He tensed, expecting an attack, but the movement ceased.

They were maintaining distance. Lying in wait.

Seconds crawled by in what felt like an eternity. He was all too aware of the passing of time. Every second spent waiting was one second closer to the transfabrulator running out of energy and leaving him exposed. But what could he do? His enemies were burrowed safely underground.

…How did they know where he was?

Thinking quickly, he took an arrow from his quiver and tossed it several feet away. No sooner than it landed, the freakishly grotesque head of some alien monstrosity erupted from the mound at the center of the room. A pair of mandibles parted with a loud shriek and its three eyes swiveled to fixate on him. Energy crackled between the two claw-like antennae on its head.

Link only just managed to keep his nerves in check. He summoned a sphere with the transfabrulator, but before he could take aim, the creature launched a ball of lightning his way. Link did the only sensible thing he could, jumping down to hide behind the cube. Not a moment too soon, as the ball hit and exploded in a shower of sparks.

Link spun. Caught up in the moment, he had all but forgotten to keep eyes on the other mound. He barely had time to throw himself out of the way as the second creature breached the surface and flew through the space he previously occupied. He didn’t get a good look at it, but it was at least as wide as he was, and twice as long. It skidded along the surface of the ground as it landed, then burrowed back underground.

Link scrambled back to the cube and peered around the corner. The first creature was still above the surface, its head wobbling about. Good. He took aim and let the sphere fly.

Crunch! Thud! The sphere slammed into the dirt and threw up a plume as the alien monstrosity’s head exploded into wormgoop.

One down, one to—aaugh!

The second creature came at him again, and this time he wasn’t fast enough to get out of the way. It clipped him in passing, knocking him to the ground and tearing a long gash in his side.

Link winced in pain as he staggered to his feet. Its razor-sharp mandibles had cut clean through his armor, but thankfully the wound underneath wasn’t deep. To his right, the cube fuzzed and vanished in a shower of green light. Link strapped the transfabrulator to his belt and pulled his shield to guard. The mound of dirt ahead sped away from him, then stopped and turned back, building up speed.

He had to end this now. He waited until just the right moment and—

Link drew sharply on his red affinity. The world came to a near halt. He broke into a dead sprint, kicking up dirt in his wake. The worm-thing’s grotesque head broke through the surface, and its body followed up in an arc. Its alien eyes fixed on him and it opened its maw wide.

At the last second, Link slid to the ground, then sprang up with all his might, driving his shield into the creature from below. It shrieked as it landed, crashing into the dirt and thrashing wildly a few meters away.

This was it! He might not get another chance.

With a fierce cry, he tore forward and leapt through the air, bringing his sword to bear. His blade sunk deep into his foe. It writhed and bucked underneath him, but he held on tight, stabbing it again and again, over and over, merciless and unrelenting in his assault. The worm-creature jerked and twitched violently, but its thrashing grew weaker with every attack until at last it fell still.

Panting, Link pulled his sword free and plunged it into the thing’s head once more for good measure, then fell away, completely exhausted.

-o-

After taking time to restore, patch himself up as best he could, and scarf down the last of his rations, Link made it up the stairs to the fourth floor. It was dark here, so he needed his lantern.

He was really hoping there wasn’t much more he had to do before he could head back to base camp. He could really use a nap.

The fourth floor, as best he could tell, consisted of a single medium-sized room with an altar at its center. There were sconces along the walls, though they held no torches. A stone tablet sat in front of the altar, with a familiar script etched across its surface. The way ahead was open.

“Fi? What’s it say?”

“It says: ‘Congratulations, initiate, on successfully completing your trials. Please place the transfabrulator upon the altar so that it may be used to test the next candidate, then proceed ahead to receive your blessing.’”

Link did a double take. “What? No—why would I do that?”

“Master, the architects of this facility likely did not take into account the eventualities which lead to the present situation. As the transfabrulator confers exceptional tactical utility, I must insist that you ignore the instructions and proceed ahead.”

“Yep,” Link said with a chuckle. “My thoughts exactly, Fi.”

He ventured on through the open passage, down an empty spiralling hallway and up a final set of stairs that let out into the open air. He shielded his eyes as they adjusted to the light. Though it felt like days since he started the final trial, it was still morning. it had only been three hours at most since he set out.

He found himself on an open platform under the obsidian obelisk. It rose up above, apparently unsupported by any part of the structure. A large basin stood at the center of the platform, filled with shimmering, sky blue water. There was nothing else in sight.

Was this the “blessing”?

He approached and dipped a finger into the pool. It was cool and refreshing. Link unclasped his empty waterskin and dunked it into the basin.

…Huh? He lifted it in and out of the water. It wouldn’t fill. Not only was the waterskin empty, but it was still bone dry.

What the heck? So much for that.

He bent forward and cupped his hands, taking in a mouthful. It was ice cold, but in a nice way. It had to be the best, most refreshing water he’d ever tasted. As he swallowed, he felt a relaxing chill spread through his body. He felt lighter, as if he were previously tethered down and was now free.

His head swam and his vision blurred.

Link grunted. “Uhhhh—hrk. Maybe that wasn’t the best idea.”

The world washed away in a burst of blinding light.

-o-

Link awoke, submerged in water. He swam up and climbed atop the surface of a lake of pure, radiant light. Above, a rift in the sky opened to an endless golden expanse. Around him, the lake flowed outward from a single island at its center, cascading over the edges of a great structure in five tiers of luminous waterfalls.

A lone woman stood on the island, clad in a simple robe of the purest white.

The murals did not do her justice.

She beckoned him forward, and Link approached.

“Holy Mother,” he whispered as he neared. “Is it really you?”

“I must commend you on making it this far, my child,” the Goddess Hylia spoke. Link stared wide-eyed, listening intently. He dared not interrupt.

“Although your trials have only begun, you show great promise. It is the solemn duty of all who walk this path to protect the realm, and to seek out and vanquish evil wherever it is found. The powers you are to wield must only ever serve this purpose. Prove yourself worthy of them, and you will have earned my favor.”

Link’s breath caught in his throat. Something about this wasn’t right. If Hylia was still here, then…

He knelt before her. “Holy Mother—please, you must listen! The world needs you! The realm is beset by a great evil already—there isn’t time! A man named Ganondorf… he serves… the dark god Demise…”

“You doubtless have many questions for me,” Hylia said, speaking over him as if he weren’t there. “Would that I could answer them. Rest assured that I am with you still, guiding your steps from beyond time. Have faith, and you will be rewarded. For now, you must continue your quest. I charge you with this task: release the shade of Ibboso from his eternal prison, and retrieve the fragment of Courage his tribe defends.”

She reached out and touched him, and the world fell away. The light of the lake faded, and tendrils of shadow latched onto him, threatening to pull him below the surface.

“No… no!” Link cried, fighting against the tendrils. He futility grasped at her hand as he sunk deeper and deeper into the darkness. “Please! Not yet! Please…”

“Be brave, my child,” Hylia said sadly, her fingers brushing over his. “Go forth, and defend the realm.”

The darkness swallowed him up.

-o-

Link opened his eyes to find himself in the middle of a pitched battle. He grabbed his—

Where was his sword?

Where was he?

Disembodied, he took in the battlefield around him. Gerudo armies charged and clashed, chaotic half-formations meeting with each other, shifting and parting like piles of sand in a twisting wind. A cavalry charge swept through where he was standing, but the horses passed through as if they were made of mist.

Just ahead, two combatants were locked in a heated struggle. Link’s attention was drawn to the larger of the two—a Gerudo king in resplendent armor, wielding a greatsword with a fragment of gold embedded in its hilt.

The landscape changed, the mess of bodies and the chaos of battle fading away to reveal an abandoned graveyard. Shadowy silhouettes shambled back and forth around a decrepit mausoleum. The ghostly form of the same Gerudo king sat atop it.

His eyes fell on Link. He stood and pointed his sword, letting out a wail that sent shivers down his spine. The restless dead around him echoed it, their voices mingling together in an unholy cacophony of anguish and torment.

-o-

Link felt a rushing sensation, and his vision swam once more. He shook his head as his mind cleared. He floated down from the air to land gently on the ground.

He looked up to see the faces of three rather surprised Sheikah.

Koja dropped her playing cards.

“Oh, hi guys,” he said lamely.

“Welcome back,” Ryn said fondly. “Been busy, I take it?”

“Link?” Grenja said warily. “Why are you glowing?”

“I—what?” He looked down at his hands. Sure enough… though just as he noticed it, the glow faded.

“Ah, that’s probably related to the vision I just had,” he said. “I completed the last challenge by defeating three—well, technically four—giant, nasty monsters, one after the other. I then drank some magical water that I think unlocked my potential or something? I’m not sure yet. Then I was transported to the Sacred Realm, and I saw Hylia. Well, not Hylia exactly, but close enough. Anyway, she told me to be brave and to continue on my journey and to protect the realm, and charged me to go free the souls of the damned from their eternal prison. Not sure what to do about that yet, but I think I found another fragment of the Triforce.”

The trio stared at him in stunned silence.

“Yeah, okay,” Koja said, standing up and straightening herself out. “I was getting tired of this place anyway.”

“Wait, where exactly are we going?” Ryn asked.

Link frowned. “I’m not sure, but I got a vague feeling it was… thataway?” He pointed somewhere off to the east.

“Aww yeah, new mission!” Grenja shouted, pumping his hand in the air. “Time to kill us some ghosts!”

“Err, not sure if we’re supposed to kill them exactly—”

“You dolt,” Koja said. “You can’t kill ghosts.”

“Oh, come on! Since when are there ghosts?!” Ryn demanded.

Link smiled to himself. He glad to be back in warm company. “Don’t worry, we’ll talk it over later,” he said. He stretched and let out a big yawn. “Right now, I need a good, long nap.”
