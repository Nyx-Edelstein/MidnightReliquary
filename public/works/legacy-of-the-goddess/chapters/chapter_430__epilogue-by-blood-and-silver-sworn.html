The Obsidian Spire rose from the desert like a black blade thrust into the heart of the Realm. Five massive tiers of volcanic glass, each narrower than the last, crowned by an obelisk that clawed at the sky. Azahndor reined his horse to a halt as the structure came into view, sunlight catching its edges in thin, bright lines that made his eyes water.

Twenty-six years since he’d last stood before these walls. He’d been little more than a boy then—seventeen summers and burning with youthful certainty.

His horse balked as he urged it forward, nostrils flaring at some scent carried on the wind. He pressed his heels in firmly. The animal obeyed, though its ears remained flat against its skull. Smart beast. Even after all this time, the Spire still exuded a cursed air.

He dismounted near the entrance, boots sinking into golden sand. Behind him, three lieutenants drew their mounts to a halt. Desert-worn armor, faces wrapped against the wind. Good men. Loyal men who had followed him into exile.

One dismounted and took Azahndor’s reins, murmuring to calm the restless animals. The other two positioned themselves on either side of the entrance, hands resting easy on their sword hilts. They knew their duty. They would stand watch until he emerged, whether that be an hour or until they dropped dead from exhaustion.

He turned back to the Spire with different eyes than those he’d brought as a desperate boy seeking to prove his worth. A king’s eyes now. Eyes that had seen death itself conquered within these walls.

She waits, he thought. Still she waits.

He lowered his hood and strode forward without a word.

The corridor stretched ahead into shadow—exactly as he remembered. Impossibly precise stonework, not a crack or hint of settling despite the millennia. His boots rang against smooth stone. Fine dust covered every surface, sand drifting in delicate patterns that swirled with each step. Eternal stone beneath ephemeral sand. The work of ages yielding to the patient invasion of the desert.

Twenty-six years was a long time to wait. But the waiting was over.

He paused at the threshold to the vestibule, letting his eyes adjust to the dimmer light. Sand and dirt covered every surface—thin layers on the walkways, larger drifts in corners where wind had pushed it into piles. Half-melted candles littered the space, dozens of them on small braziers scattered along the walkways and benches. Pilgrims, then. Or worshippers. The blood-stained altar before the upper door suggested purposes darker than prayer.

Fools.

His gaze lifted to the ornate door at the far end of the upper walkway. Both halves bent inward at the bottom, metal curled inward like petals from the violent impact of that ancient Lightsworn device. His doing, all that time ago.

He climbed the steps, boots leaving clean prints in the dust. The wrecked door opened onto a narrow hallway that widened as he followed it deeper. At its end lay the arena itself.

The dome stretched above him—vast and circular, supported by four massive pillars that rose from the sand. Light filtered down from nowhere, softer and stranger than true sunlight.

This arena had witnessed countless trials in ages past. Criminals and prisoners offered as sacrifice to what lurked below. Young boys staking their lives against death itself. Among the Akhari, merely surviving and escaping this place earned a warrior’s name.

Until he’d ended the practice.

And there, half-buried in sand—

“Hello, old friend,” he whispered.

The carapace of Mor Kharath’s avatar, still intact after all these years. All that remained of something once terrible beyond imagining. Azahndor approached slowly and rested his gauntlet against the chitinous surface.

They had hailed him King Among Kings for killing her.

“But I didn’t kill you, did I?” His voice echoed in the empty chamber. “Not truly.”

He’d only postponed the confrontation.

Azahndor flexed his gauntleted hand, feeling power hum through his veins. Seven fragments now. Seven pieces of the Sanctern of Will, the most recent additions liberated by the Khamsyr from Kiat the Warmonger—some ancient Baastani pack leader out of the Novali Badlands who’d held them for gods knew how long.

The spiral teardrop shape was nearly complete. He could see it plainly, etched in silver light across the back of his hand. Only two more shards remained.

The raw might of Vaka, nearly at his command.

His fingers curled into a fist. The fragments pulsed brighter, responding to his will. Power thrummed against his bones like a second heartbeat—vast, hungry, and eager.

He’d needed time, then. Time to grow strong. Time to prepare.

But the time had come.

“We have unfinished business, you and I.”

Twenty-six years earlier…

The attendants fell like wheat before the scythe.

Azahndor strode through the carnage, boots splashing through spreading pools of blood. Six robed figures had stood between him and the arena. Six corpses now, their bodies slumped against ancient stone, the last one’s screams still echoing faintly in the enclosed space.

He snapped his blade to the side, flinging droplets across the stone. The Sword of Kings—claimed by rite of blood. It thrummed in his grip, eager for more.

Ornate doors stood before him—massive things of worked metal that had been sealed for centuries. Without the cooperation of the attendants, this was the only way in. And he had no chance of opening them. Not on his own, anyway.

In his hand, the strange device hummed. A metal half-dome mounted on an ornate frame, covered in symbols he couldn’t read. Ancient Lightsworn work, the Great Elders had told him. A weapon beyond mortal comprehension.

He didn’t need to understand it. He only needed to use it.

A high-pitched shriek followed by a thundering crack. The metal didn’t bend or shatter—it simply ceased. The doors unraveled like burning parchment, curling inward from the point of impact. And there, in the center—

Images bloomed between them—warriors charging forward one by one, each falling in turn. A giant of a man in golden plate armor, strangled and crushed by writhing tendrils of shadow. A sorcerer in immaculate robes, consumed by black flame that left no ash. A hunter whose arrows split the very air, reduced to pulp by an alien maw.

Azahndor’s grip tightened on his sword. His pulse quickened and his blood ran cold—but he stood his ground. Let her try to intimidate me. His patron had already assured him of victory. A deity whose power rivaled Avalia. This creature, for all her posturing, was nothing in comparison.

He forced his shoulders square, bringing his sword up to guard position. His heart hammered in his chest, but he kept his voice steady. “Tell me, O great Mor Kharath—did any of those champions have the favor of a god?!” His smile sharpened. “Or were they all just pretenders playing at prophecy?”

Her expression went still. The amusement bled away—the mockery died on her lips. Those golden eyes hardened into something cold and ancient and furious.

“You dare?”

The sand erupted.

A dozen skeletal forms rose from beneath the surface—withered corpses with gleaming bones and hollow eyes. Remains of those who’d come before him. Those who fell to this demon. They shrieked as they charged, fleshless hands reaching, jaws gaping wide.

He pivoted, the Sword of Kings sweeping wide in a defensive arc as his left palm snapped up again. The motion flowed from hip to shoulder—ancient forms made reflex through endless kata. Arcing amethyst crackled between his fingers. He released. Two more skeletal forms exploded into fragments.

Laughter.

It started as a woman’s chuckle—soft at first, almost fond. Then it deepened, growing into something vast and monstrous, too large for any mortal throat. The sound swelled, reverberating from places between places that had no names.

The creature twisted with impossible speed for something so huge—flowing beneath the sand before erupting again, segments rippling with alien grace.

He met it head-on, driving the Sword of Kings into the chitinous surface. The blade skittered across the armor—barely a scratch. The creature reared back, and the impact sent him tumbling across the sand until he skidded to a halt.

It didn’t slow. If anything, it moved faster, enraged. The massive body coiled, and suddenly he was surrounded—rings of segmented flesh closing in from all sides, cutting off escape.

He scrambled upright, sword raised, words already forming on his lips—

The arena twisted. Reality folded inward upon itself, and suddenly he was falling through spaces that had no names—dimensions that existed only in the fever-dreams of gods.

He was in her maw. Not consumed. Becoming. His flesh merged with chitinous plates. His bones hollowed and stretched. His thoughts fractured across segments that reached toward infinity.

“I am the Void that devours all things,” her voice whispered through his dissolving mind. “All will feed me. All will become one with me.”

Color went first. Not fading to grey—simply ceasing. Sunsets over Kamorh. Blood on steel. His mother’s eyes. The concept itself unmade, leaving only hollow absence.

Sound followed. Not silence—he knew silence, from long treks across silent deserts. This was the erasure of sound’s possibility. His father’s laugh. His brother’s war cry. Fires crackling in the night. All of it unwritten from reality.

Then his body scattered. Each moment of his existence became a grain of sand, pulled in directions that had no shape. His first hunt. His coronation. A thousand fragments of self, dispersing across scattering winds.

He tried to remember why he’d come here, but the thought dissolved before it could form. Worse—part of him wanted this. Craved it the way drowning lungs eventually stop fighting and welcome the water.

He wasn’t being devoured. He was being revealed—a temporary eddy in an infinite current. A brief pattern that had mistaken itself for permanence.

No.

The Void whispered with his own voice, gentle as a lover’s promise: “Why struggle? You were always meant to feed me.”

Three years earlier…

A shove sent Azahndor tumbling from the saddle. He hit the ground hard, breath driven from his lungs on impact. The sack over his head reeked of sweat and oxgoat dung. Rough hands seized him—one at his collar, another at his belt—lifting and hurling him into softer sand.

Something landed in the sand near his head with a soft thump.

He rolled onto his back, arms still bound. Voices muttered above—low, gruff, speaking too quickly to catch. Someone pressed a knife into his hands.

“Good luck, boy.” Laughter, then footsteps receding. Wood creaked, leather groaned, an oxgoat snorted. Hooves crunched away across packed earth until the sounds faded to nothing.

He lay still until silence settled completely. Then he started working at the rope.

He twisted his wrists and sawed at the bindings, ignoring the burn against his skin. It took longer than it should have. The blade was barely serviceable—not quite dull, but close.

The rope finally parted. He worked his hands free and yanked the sack from his head, sucking in a breath of clean air as his eyes adjusted.

Desert sands stretched in every direction. The sun hung low to the west, casting long shadows across crumbling stone structures about fifty paces away. Ancient ruins—half-buried columns and broken walls jutting from the sand like bones.

He pushed himself upright, muscles protesting. His shoulder throbbed where he’d hit the ground. He rolled it experimentally—painful, but the joint moved. Probably just bruised. He’d need to be careful with it.

He glanced toward the knapsack a few paces away and crawled over to check its contents. Jerky wrapped in cloth, a satchel of dried glistenberries, flint and steel, a leather sleeping mat. A waterskin sloshed when he shook it—maybe two days’ worth if he was careful.

He cut the knot free and coiled the remaining rope into his pack. Might be useful later.

He stood and glanced about again, brushing sand from his clothes. It would be dark soon.

The ruins were the only landmark for miles. Not coincidence. He’d known they would bring him here—it’s why he’d started that fight during the trade run.

His pulse quickened as he considered what lay ahead.

The gateway to the underworld. That’s what they called it. A place where the veil between worlds grew thin, guarded by a demon that had feasted on warriors’ bones for generations.

Most boys dumped at trial sites were expected to fend for themselves. Find water, hunt game, come back with scars and trophies worth bragging about—or don’t come back at all. Few survived.

But Azahndor hadn’t been dumped here by accident. He’d planned this—years spent hiding in tavern basements after dark, listening to old warriors spin their tales. Scraping together wages to pay for tips from those who’d survived trials like this. Sneaking into restricted archives when the Elders weren’t looking, stealing moments with forbidden texts that outlined the true nature of these places.

He’d been caught once, sneaking through the archives. The beating had left him unable to sit for a week. It was worth it, though. He’d learned what he needed—these weren’t just any ruins. They were the ruins. The place where demons walked.

And he was going to kill one.

He shouldered the knapsack, tucked the knife into his belt, and set off.

The Baptism of Fire. That’s what they called it. Blood and pain and survival—the trial that turned boys into men. Only afterward could they return to claim the final Baptism of Spirit and take their place in Akhari society.

Air, Water, Earth, Fire, Spirit. He’d survived being born—Baptism in Air. He’d knelt in the sacred pool at seven summers—Baptism in Water. He’d endured two years of backbreaking labor, hauling stone until his hands bled and his shoulders nearly gave out—Baptism in Earth.

Now came Fire.

Most boys chose their own trials. Hunts. Pilgrimages. Challenges issued to rival clans. A man’s place in Akhari society depended on how much he risked—and those who staked too little found no welcome among warriors, hunters, or clansmen.

But Azahndor’s family had enemies. Whispers in council chambers. Debts owed to the wrong people. They’d volunteered him for indentured servitude before he could make his own choice—carted him off in the dead of night like they were doing him a favor. He’d be worked until he was useless, then discarded.

But he’d spent years preparing for exactly this. When rumors reached him—debts being called in, his name whispered in the wrong conversations—he’d known his time was running out. So he’d positioned himself carefully. Started that fight the moment they reached Karlev. Made himself just inconvenient enough that dumping him at the nearest trial site seemed like the obvious solution.

They thought they’d stripped him of choice. Doomed him. Instead, they’d given him exactly what he wanted.

The first ruin was a collapsed archway half-buried in sand. Symbols covered the stonework, worn almost smooth by wind and time. He traced one with his finger—Old Akhari script. Warnings, mostly. Turn back. This is not a place of honor. Death waits below.

Perfect.

He moved deeper into the ruins, boots crunching on sand and silt. The structures formed a rough circle around a central courtyard. The sun hung low now, barely a fist above the horizon, stretching shadows across the sand.

He set down his pack and began gathering dry brush. He’d need a fire—warmth against the desert cold, light to work by. Time to prepare for what came next.

He wasn’t going back empty-handed.

He was going back a king.

He’d gathered flat stones from the collapsed structures and stacked them into a rough three-sided enclosure around the fire pit. Clay had been harder to come by—he’d had to scrape it from a dried streambed, digging with a scooped stone until he hit the pale, dense earth beneath. He rehydrated it with urine—unpleasant but effective—and packed the mixture into the gaps between stones.

On his third strike, the fire crackled to life. Sparks caught in the dry brush and spread into hungry flames. He fed it slowly, adding twigs first, then larger branches. A roaring blaze would attract unwanted attention—a small, steady fire would serve him better.

The makeshift stove worked better than expected. Heat radiated from the clay-sealed stones, flowing back toward him rather than bleeding out into the desert night.

He positioned himself in the corner formed by two crumbling walls, the stove between him and the open desert. The ruins offered protection on two sides, while the heat would discourage anything larger than a jackalbadger from approaching. Smaller predators—those were easier to deal with.

From his pack, he pulled the kippongu-hide sleeping mat and unrolled it across the sand. He swept the ground first, clearing away loose stones and debris that would dig into his back come morning.

One more thing.

He fished a small cloth pouch from his pack—stoneveil blossoms inside. Orange petals spotted with dark purple, wrinkled from being pressed together. He’d found them growing in a scraggly cluster beneath an overhang while searching for clay. An old hunter had taught him this trick years ago. The man had been half-mad from sun exposure, but his advice was sound.

He scattered a handful of petals onto the hot stones at the edge of his fire. They sizzled immediately, curling and blackening as thin wisps of acrid smoke rose into the air. He coughed once, then turned his face away as the smell intensified—bitter and sharp, like burnt pepper mixed with tar. The smoke would ward off insects and snakes—anything with enough sense to avoid the scent.

Satisfied, he settled onto his mat with his back against the ruins. The stone still held warmth from the day’s sun. The fire crackled softly. Smoke drifted across the sand in lazy coils.

The sun climbed behind him as he circled the ruins again—his fourth time traveling around that morning. More cautious now, examining each collapsed structure with fresh eyes.

He found it on the eastern edge, tucked behind a cluster of broken columns. The opening was barely visible—just a darker shadow where sand had drifted away from the wall’s base. But the stones surrounding it were different. Precise. Deliberate. Not collapsed rubble, but placed.

Someone had sealed this.

He knelt and began moving rocks. The smaller pieces came away easily enough, settled loose on top. Beneath those lay larger stones, wedged tight. He worked methodically, testing each one before committing his weight. His injured shoulder protested with every heavy lift, a dull throb sharpening when he twisted wrong.

The sun climbed higher. Sweat soaked through his shirt.

By midday, he’d cleared enough to reveal the entrance—a rectangular opening barely wider than his shoulders, steps descending into darkness.

He sat back on his heels, breathing hard. His hands were raw, palms scraped and bleeding. He wiped them on his pants and returned to his camp.

The torch took time to prepare. He’d scavenged a long root that morning—twisted and gnarled, pried from a dead scrub tree. He wrapped one end in cloth strips torn from his shirt, then doused them in oil he’d pressed from emberreeds growing near the clay deposits.

The reeds had been tricky—crushed between flat stones, the viscous amber liquid collected drop by precious drop. But emberreed oil burned clean and long. After imbuing it with magic, the crude torch would last maybe six or seven hours.

He fashioned a sling next—rope from his knapsack, leather from his bedroll, knotted at both ends with a wider pouch in the middle. Simple, but effective. He’d spent countless hours as a boy whipping stones at targets. Not the best weapon, but it would do.

He gathered pebbles from around the ruins—smooth ones, roughly uniform. Not ideal either, but they’d shatter bone if thrown hard enough. A few well-placed stones would kill anything. He dropped them into the satchel that previously held the glistenberries and tied it to his belt.

The sling took careful work to imbue. He held it stretched between his hands, focusing his will through the leather. The material warmed, drinking in the magic until it glowed faintly. When he released his concentration, it felt different. Stronger. More resilient.

Ready.

He gathered his supplies and lit the torch. The oil-soaked cloth caught immediately, flames spreading in a ring of steady light.

He paused, staring at the flickering Red-tinged flames. The color of his affinity—the magic that had marked him from birth. A secret he’d guarded more carefully than any other.

Mages were valuable. Too valuable. The Great Elders would sacrifice a weak one without hesitation if it meant securing favor with the gods. So he’d learned to hide it—never practiced openly, cultivated a reputation for mediocrity instead. Adequate at everything, exceptional at nothing.

Better to be overlooked than a sacrifice.

He turned back to the entrance and descended.

The stairs were worn smooth, each step slightly concave from centuries of use. He counted twenty-nine before the passage leveled into a corridor. The walls pressed close, the ceiling low enough to force him into a slight crouch. Stone blocks fitted together with precision that should not have been possible without modern tools.

The corridor branched. Then branched again.

He marked his turns with small piles of sand scraped into corners—easy to miss unless you were looking, easy to find on the way back. The structure sprawled far larger than the ruins above suggested. Rooms opened off the main passages, some collapsed, others intact but empty. Carved reliefs flickered past in his torchlight. Figures in procession. Symbols that might have been prayers or warnings.

Time had reclaimed much of it. Rubble choked some passages. Others bore crusted salt deposits from ancient floods.

All passages funneled inward.

He followed them deeper, tracking the subtle downward slope. The air grew cooler. His torch flickered in drafts that came from nowhere.

The passages converged into a circular chamber—maybe thirty paces across, the ceiling lost in shadow above. The floor sloped toward the center, where a large circular opening yawned. He approached slowly, torch held high, and knelt at the rim.

No—not quite darkness. Something moved down there. Not shadow, but fog—thick and dark, hanging motionless in the still air. Black motes drifted in defiance of natural law, refusing to rise or settle. And beneath it all, a faint reddish glow pulsed like dying embers in a banked forge.

The gateway to the underworld.

His pulse hammered. Every instinct screamed at him to turn back—that he’d gone far enough, that he should retreat to the surface and claim some lesser trial, make a life for himself in another village.

He rose to his feet and tested his injured shoulder with a careful roll. The pain had dulled to a steady ache—nothing he couldn’t handle. A quick check confirmed his knife sat secure at his belt, while his torch continued burning steadily, casting restless shadows across the chamber walls.

The chamber below stretched before him—vast enough that his torchlight couldn’t find the far walls. The black fog hung thicker here, swirling lazily around his boots. Each breath tasted of copper and ash.

A god. Not some petty spirit or demon, as the stories claimed. Something vast and terrible—something that had walked this world before mortals had names for power.

Perhaps this was the true gateway to the underworld.

Sacrifice.

“Who’s there?”
