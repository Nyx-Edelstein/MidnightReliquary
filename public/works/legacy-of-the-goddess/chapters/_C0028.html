A merchant’s wagon at the back of a caravan heading from Hateno to Kakariko rolled along at a leisurely pace, driven by two palomino horses. The noonday sun shone overhead.

“Papa, can I drive now?” a boy asked.

“Not now,” the boy’s father said. “Maybe later, son. We need to keep pace with the caravan.”

Link pouted. “Why not? I’m bored back here and I already finished all my work, just like you said. Please, papa?”

“Fine. If you promise to behave,” Karindal said mirthlessly. “But just for a few minutes.”

Link gleefully hopped up front, took the reins in his hands, and—

He paused. Something about this felt wrong. Very wrong. A sense of darkness and foreboding hung over him, like a great shadow looming above his head. His mouth felt dry. He swallowed, hard.

“Uh… actually, I don’t think I’m ready for this,” he said, offering the reins back.

Karindal refused them, giving Link that disapproving glare of his before glancing away with an exasperated sigh. Link wilted and sunk into his seat.

“Look, Ar… Link. You do have to learn eventually—I suppose now is as good a time as any. It’s not hard. Watch.” He placed one hand over both of Link’s and gestured with the other just ahead. “See that bend there? The horses will follow the road on their own, but we want to make sure we steer clear of that rock so they don’t stumble around it and we don’t damage the carriage by running over it. Got it?”

Link nodded in understanding. When they came up to the bend, he tugged the reins to the left, and—

“Woah now! Easy does it,” Karindal said, taking the reins back as the wagon rocked back and forth uncomfortably for a second before settling again. “Gentle, now. Just a light touch is all you need.”

Link watched as his father set the wagon back on track with a gentle tug, and then another to correct course, before handing the reins back. “Here, try again. Hold steady for now.”

Hesitantly, Link took the reins, nodding. The wagon made it around the bend and they traveled for another forty yards or so. Karindal then pointed ahead to a slight incline in the road ahead. “See that dip, just before the knoll? We want to slow down a touch before we get there. And how do we do that?”

“I pull back on the reins?”

Karindal nodded. “Gently. And keep a loose grip.”

“…Okay,” Link said uneasily, scrunching his face up as he focused on the task at hand. He’d get it right this time for sure. As they neared, he waited until just the right moment, and—

He tugged back gently on the reins. Just a light pull, causing the horses to slow their gait. He felt slack build in the reins as the wagon slowed, until a second or two later when the rope pulled taut again.

“Excellent,” Karindal said with a smile. “Just like that, son. See? Easy.”

Link smiled, feeling a warm glow of satisfaction at Father’s approval.

But just as quickly, the smile faded. There was that sense of foreboding again. Something was wrong—he just couldn’t place it. It was like…

It was then that he saw it. A great shadow—small at first, but growing larger and darker as it sped towards them. A shrill cry rang out, and the wagon rocked violently as the great winged beast slammed into—

Link startled awake, squinting bleary-eyed at the painful brightness that met him back in the waking world.

What in damnation was that dream about? He had nightmares fairly often, but nothing quite like that. He was… he was…

He rubbed his eyes. It seemed so clear just then in his mind. But as he regained clarity and pushed through the haze, the memory of the dream left him just as quickly. He sat still, thinking about it for a long moment. It was about that day, back when… back when… except he hadn’t… but they still…

A shrill cry rang out in the distance, barely audible. If he wasn’t primed to hear it, he probably wouldn’t have noticed. Link immediately sprang into action, reaching for his bow, and—

Pain.

He fumbled, barely managing to extend the fingers on his right hand before fire and agony shot up his wounded arm.

“KARGAROK!” he heard someone yell, followed shortly by a blaring horn. The fiendish cry rang out again, louder this time.

No. No!

Pulse pounding, Link slid out the back of the wagon and turned his eyes to the sky, shielding them with his one good hand. There! A mass of black feathers, talons extended, bearing down from above with frightening speed. The Dread Shadow. The Sky Reaper.

Kargarok.

Damnation, but he was too late! The beast crashed into the side of a wagon up ahead, sending splinters and debris flying. It perched atop the wreckage and raised a wing to buffet away a volley of thrown spears and arrows, shrugging away the attacks as if they were nothing. The fearsome beast then turned its attention upon its attackers, screeching with that horrible, shrill cry—

VORP!

—And then exploded into a shower of black feathers and gore as a luminous, green-runed block hurdled through the space it once occupied.

“YEAH! Take that, you ficking oversized parrot!” Ryn yelled.

“You show those fiends, girl!” Koja called after her exuberantly.

Dozens of others in the caravan took up the cry, cheering and applauding. A couple soldiers rushed her and hoisted her up on their shoulders as a small crowd gathered. She caught Link’s eye and winked with a thumbs up, the transfabrulator glinting in her hand.

Link felt dizzy. He stood there, stunned, before he remembered to breathe. He took in a deep breath and shook his head slowly.

“Hey, don’t worry, Link!” Ryn called to him. “We’re on top of it!”

Link could do nothing but gawk. He half-stumbled as someone brushed past him, running over to assist others who were checking the destroyed wagon. He came back to himself in that instant with a start. Someone could be in there still! He—

“All clear!” someone at the wagon yelled out. “Just cargo!”

Link stumbled to a halt. He felt so utterly, completely…

Useless.

Worthless. Powerless to do anything but stand on the sidelines and watch.

Ryn caught his eye again, frowning. She hopped down and checked with Koja before heading over.

“Ugh,” Link muttered to himself. Now she was worried about him again, and she’d want talk about it, and…

Just ugh. He pivoted in place and headed back to his wagon. He knew what she’d say, but it didn’t matter. They were too slow. They got lucky. Someone could’ve been in that wagon, and if they were—

“Link, wait!”

Frustration nearly boiling over, he came to a halt and spun. “What?!”

Ryn stopped and gasped, raising a hand to her chest. She gave him a hurt look, then one of intense anger before her expression softened. Link felt his cheeks begin to burn. On top of everything, here he was sticking his foot in his mouth. He felt like a complete and utter fool.

“I’m sorry, Ryn,” he said, sullen. “Listen, I didn’t mean to snap at you. I just—”

“—No, it’s okay. I get it,” she said, stepping over. “Really, you don’t have to worry—we’ve got this! We’ve got your back.” She placed one soft hand on his wounded shoulder, cocking her head slightly.

Link leaned into her, hanging his head with a sigh. She kissed his forehead gently, and he felt some of the tension leave him.

“Want to talk about it?” Ryn asked.

Link grunted. “Not really. I was napping, and I heard its shriek in the distance just before the alarm sounded,” he said. “Went for my bow. If my arm weren’t… I could’ve …”

She narrowed her eyes at that, mouth drawn to a line. “No one got hurt. And you know that’s not what I meant.”

Link had to resist the urge to groan. This was going exactly as he expected, and he didn’t have any outs.

“You saw what it did to that wagon, right?” He glanced over her shoulder, and she shut her eyes hard, turning her head to the side. “I know you did. Someone could’ve been in there!”

“Someone can always get hurt, Link,” Ryn predictably responded. “And do you honestly think an arrow or two would’ve stopped it? In case you hadn’t noticed, we had half a dozen soldiers shooting and lobbing spears at it. Didn’t even scratch the thing.”

Ugh, why didn’t anyone ever listen? “Yes, I have in fact taken out a Kargarok at two-hundred fifty meters with a single arrow before, and I can do it again!”

“How many times?”

Link stammered. “I mean… well, yes, you can argue that one time I got lucky, but—”

“—You got lucky. Once,” Ryn said, not letting him finish. “And now you’re using that as an excuse to beat yourself up for feeling like you can’t be the hero you think everyone expects you to be.”

He winced. She wasn’t exactly wrong, but did she have to say it like that? “I mean…” he started, only to feel the wind leave his sails as Ryn glowered at him. He sighed, deflating. “Yeah… I suppose you’re right.”

Her being right didn’t make it hurt any less.

Koja strolled up a moment later. “Hey! What’d I miss?”

Link shrugged noncommittally.

Koja glanced to Ryn. “Oh,” she said with a frown. She paused, cocked her head, then perked up as she drew tall and crossed her arms. “Hey, captain! Don’t sweat it, yeah? It’s okay to take a breather. We’ve got this—The Party to Totally Lay Waste to all Evil, on the job!” She clapped him on the shoulder. The one that wasn’t injured.

Link sighed. “Yeah,” he said, glum. “That’s what Ryn said, more or less.” Koja turned back to Ryn and half-smiled, half-frowned. “—But yeah, thanks for the support,” he hastily added.

“Look, I’m sure you’ll be back to normal before too long,” Koja said. “See? You’re already recovering.” She pointed at his fingers.

Link flexed them. Slowly. They still hurt and felt numb and unresponsive, but he could move them a little more now. “Yeah, I guess,” he said. “Not fast enough though, if you ask me.”

“Okay, that’s it!” Ryn said, throwing her hands up. “I’ve had enough of this.” She turned to Koja. “All good here?”

Koja mock saluted. “Yes, ma’am! Wagon’s lost, but Darial said we should be back on track within the hour, after plucking what’s left of the cucco and salvaging what can be salvaged.”

“Right then,” Ryn said matter-of-factly. “You.” She jabbed a finger at Link’s chest. “You’re coming with me. We’re going to go for a ride, out scouting. Just the two of us. A nice, long ride, all by ourselves. And we’re going to talk.”

“Oh, is that right?” Koja snickered.

Ryn eyed her, then broke out into an impish grin. “Well, I’m sure we’ll get to talking eventually,” she said with a wink, meeting Link’s eyes as she ran her tongue along her teeth.

Link gulped and took a surreptitious look around, face ablaze. No one, excepting Koja, seemed to take note. Koja for her part only gave an exaggerated wink-smile and a thumbs up as she turned and sauntered off. “Take care, you two! Don’t get into too much trouble out there.”

“Uhh… okay,” Link said sheepishly.

“Well? You coming?” Ryn asked from a few paces away, unfastening the bridle of her horse. She hopped atop it and wheeled around.

Link furrowed his brow. “I mean, how am I supposed to say ‘no’ when you put it like that?”

“That’s the neat part,” Ryn said gleefully, drawing up beside him and offering a hand. “You don’t.”

Link managed an uneasy smile as he took her hand and hopped up behind her in the saddle. They set off at a canter.

No. Absolutely not. Just the thought of pushing through all the kodongo dung Lensa would pile up just to get her to seriously consider the Sunseekers—not to mention Taetus and whatever other trifling political nonsense…

Breathe. Let it go. Don’t let it affect you, Zelda told herself.

She was still angry at the countess, and she’d have to deal with that eventually. She was not looking forward to that conversation.

Urgh. Who else could she rely on? Laylith she trusted well enough, but even she seemed to dislike the Gerudo as much as anyone. That wouldn’t—

“You know what—no,” Zelda said aloud to no-one in particular, standing up from her chair abruptly. She was so fed up with this garbage. The Sunseekers were Hylian citizens now, the same as everyone. It was time the rest of the kingdom came to understand that fact.

Regret. Immediate and intense. Zelda was so far out of her element as to be a fish out of water.

She would be fine. Probably. She just needed to push through it.

Zelda stifled a laugh, and Naibi smirked in response. Okay, yeah. She could see the appeal. There was a certain mutuality to this… uhm. Whatever this was.

She shook her head, trying to keep a hold on her thoughts. It was too easy to get lost in the moment. She… uh…

The baroness shifted, setting one leg over the other and placing a hand on the table. “I had meant to tell you at the soonest opportunity,” she replied. “I suppose that moment would be now. We found out and executed two of Ganondorf’s spies among our number just prior to the siege. Zelda gave a slow, purposeful nod. Of course there were spies. That made sense. How could there not be spies? How could she possibly have expected anything else, inviting a large group of foreign peoples into her nation? A couple spies, among thousands? That was not surprising in the least! In fact, she should have anticipated it sooner. She tilted her head back and let out a long sigh, then unthinkingly committed to downing the rest of her pint as she tried not to spit it out.

Yuck.

Thankfully, she had tools purpose built for this exact situation. The Sheikah could handle this.

Zelda nodded once more to herself and wiped her mouth clean. “No worries, Naibi. And thank you for bringing this to my attention. I have just the man for the job. Malg—a Sheikah agent specializing in intelligence and interrogation. I will get him on it immediately. Work with him, and I will assign to him all other resources we have available. Naibi bowed her head low. “Again, it brings me great sorrow to deliver this news, my Queen. And yes, thank you—I welcome the assistance, and will of course make every accommodation.”

Zelda nodded again. She wasn’t entirely satisfied, but she could worry on her own time. Malg was nothing if not a consummate professional. He would do what needed to be done. The reason for this meeting, however…

She strummed her fingers on the table. Hrm. Perhaps she should ease into it.

Wow. Her ears felt hot, and the room spun a bit.

“So, uhm—”

Naibi laughed and cocked her head. “You want to ask something of me, yes? By all means, just say it. I am ever your humble servant.”

Zelda mentally stumbled. “Oh? Uhm, hmm…” she muttered, gathering her thoughts again. She had planned to ask about Naibi’s experiences thus far as a means to segway into the topic, but—

“Or if it is easier for you, we can do it your way,” the baroness said with a shrug. “But really—whatever it is. I am in your debt.”

Zelda regarded her. It really was that simple, wasn’t it?

“Naibi, I would like you to go on a diplomatic mission for me,” she said simply. “To Goron lands, back with their envoy, Kenak. There is information about a paragon-hermit I would like you to find out and return to me. At any rate… While you are there, they may regard you with suspicion and mistrust, and a wrong step could invite disaster. But what else is new?”

She met Naibi’s eyes and held her gaze. Naibi broke first, glancing away and raising a hand to her chin as she pondered for a long moment. “As I said, if my queen requires this of me, I will do it. I believe I get your reasoning, but I must ask: why me? Surely you have another more suited to this task?”

Zelda took time to consider her next words carefully. She found there was a certain calmness to her thoughts, and they came easily. The noise in the room even seemed to die down as she spoke.

“My aim with the Gorons, as with the Sunseekers, is to heal a divide between cultures spanning decades. Naibi: There is no other for this task. You and you alone are the perfect choice, and sending you sends exactly the right message. I believe you see that? Not just to the Gorons, but to those in our kingdom who would question you as well.” She tapped her chin as another thought came to her. “And besides, given your prime location, it would be most advantageous for the kingdom to have a reputable liaison. Why not begin that work now? And just imagine—if we manage to reopen trade? Well, the Sunseekers stand to benefit a great deal, no? Not to mention, your barony is on the way—not even a day’s digression. I am certain you have people back home you would love to see?”

Naibi let out a jovial, merry laugh, waving her hands defensively before wiping away a tear. “Please, Your Grace! I cannot bear another moment of your shrewd and harsh negotiation tactics!” She hung her head low, but it was not enough to hide her smile. “You drive a hard bargain, but I fear I am honor bound to oblige your request.” She glanced up and winked.

Zelda smiled warmly in kind. “Thank you, Naibi,” she said. “I am very glad that we are friends.”

She started then, and glanced around the tavern. She hadn’t imagined it—the room had gone quiet, all eyes on her.

Oh dear.

Everyone just as quickly found something else to occupy their attention.

Queen Zelda?
