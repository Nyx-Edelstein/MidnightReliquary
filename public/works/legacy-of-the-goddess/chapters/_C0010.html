“Thank you, Captain,” Zelda said, trying to calm her nerves. She held her hands clasped in front of her and nodded to Roesar, then to the guardsmen at the door. “Let us proceed.”

They opened the door. Link and Laylith entered first, followed by herself and Roesar. Koja and Cove waited outside. Zelda scanned the room. It was spacious and grand; fit for guests of the highest station. It featured a balcony, a small study, several paintings and decorative pieces, two beds with quilts embroidered in gold, and a great chandelier that hung low over a massive, ornate oak table.

Naibi, First of the Sunseekers, sat at the far side of the table, reclined with her feet propped atop it.

“At long last, Queen Zelda Numenia Proventia of Hyrule—the Champion of Hylia herself!—deigns to grace me with her presence,” the woman said in mock reverence. “Truly, I am honored beyond words, Your Grace.”

Mistress, came Fi’s voice, I do not believe her to be—

“Not now, Fi!” Zelda muttered under her breath, masking the words by turning her head and clearing her throat.

“Ahem. The honor is mine,” Zelda said with a nod of her head. Whatever slights this woman had suffered, Zelda would need to defuse the tension quickly. She approached the table, taking a seat. Roesar sat beside her, and Link and Laylith fanned out to either side. “Let me first say that I sincerely apologize for the delay, and for any other inconveniences or inhospitalities you may have—”

“—Pfah. Spare me, please,” Naibi said in a thick accent. “I did not come all this way to be coddled by a soft Hylian—”

“Watch your tone, woman!” Roesar snapped. “This is the Queen of Hyrule you are speaking to!”

“Captain,” Zelda said, letting some bite into her words. This was going to be difficult enough already. “I am capable of speaking for myself, thank you,” she said. “In the meantime, I believe you said you had other important duties to attend to?”

He was caught off guard by that and was clearly dissatisfied at the dismissal, but did not seek to challenge her. “You are quite right, Your Grace,” he said, standing and sliding in his chair. He bowed low, first to her, and then to their guest. “If you will excuse me, I will go and attend to them now.”

Zelda nodded to him, waiting until she heard the door open and close behind her before continuing. A short breath. She steepled her hands. “As I was saying—”

“Is this truly necessary?” Naibi interrupted, gesturing to Link and Laylith. “Is the Queen of Hyrule so fearful for her safety that she cannot speak with me alone, in her own fortress? First I am made a prisoner in all but name, and now am treated as little more than a common thug. Is this how you treat with all ambassadors, Your Grace?”

Zelda began to mouth a response, but stopped herself. Surely she couldn’t expect—

Absolutely not, came Laylith’s voice. Don’t even think about it.

Zelda turned, eyeing the older Sheikah woman. Laylith shook her head fractionally.

“Well?” Naibi demanded.

She was indeed a difficult one. Zelda thought back to what Lensa had told her—that supposedly she had been making unreasonable demands since she arrived.

—The thought occurred to her that perhaps this was merely a cultural misunderstanding.

“Naibi,” Zelda asked plainly, “do you feel disrespected by me?”

The Gerudo woman let her feet down, swinging around to rest her head on the backs of her hands with elbows propped against the table. She stared at Zelda, but said nothing.

Zelda had a feeling—an intuition—and chose to pursue it. She was really hoping this wasn’t a mistake…

“Allow me to be frank,” Zelda said. “Much rests on the outcome of this meeting. I am here because my kingdom finds itself in the most dire of circumstances. We are at war, and our resources are strained. Many have died, and many more will die before this is over. We have repelled the Blin for now, but it will take decades to reverse and repair the damage this Blight has caused—if we manage it all.” She let her voice rise towards the end to punctuate the gravity of her words.

“Any true ally we could find right now,” she continued, softer, “I would consider to be a blessing from the Goddesses themselves. And so I wonder, Naibi: what is it you want? Why are you here? We both know the history between our peoples, and that the tenuous truce we once held was shattered by the raids on our lands that forced my father’s hand almost a decade ago.

“And yet—” she gestured to Naibi, then leaned in, lowering her voice. “And yet, here you are. Despite—or perhaps, because of the unrest and instability within the Gerudo nation. Despite the mistrust and disrespect you have been shown by Roesar and his men. Despite the mutual mistrust between our peoples. Despite that, you are still here.” She paused, letting the words settle.

“Now, the Lynx had little to say to me in her missive,” she said. “She offered aid—which, absolutely, we are in desperate need of. One could read this the obvious way; of one party seeking to ingratiate themselves to the other at an opportune time, in hopes of political gain.” She studied Naibi, but the woman had scarcely moved an inch. Zelda decided to continue.

“I, however, choose not to believe that.” Zelda turned slightly, placing one hand on the table. She gazed ahead, recalling the events as she spoke. “Naibi, I watched as the Lynx’s messenger fought and died to protect me, after failing to warn me in time of an attempt on my life. An attempt, she claimed, was made by the Exiled King.”

Naibi shifted in her seat at his mention. Interesting. Zelda glanced over to Link. He nodded.

“Now, a cynical person might believe this all to be a ruse, or a coincidence, or perhaps just opportunism. But I have reason to believe this business with this ‘Exiled King’ runs deeper than just an opportunistic assassination attempt. He’s been rather persistent. Twenty days ago, his agents tried again. But this time? This time they used bombs. Bombs that we have evidence to believe were taken from the stores of Talus, subsequent to it being overrun.”

“There is perhaps more to you than I was lead to believe,” Naibi said, speaking up at last. She reclined back in her seat, crossing her legs. “I acknowledge you, Zelda. As to the disrespect I have been shown by your subordinates, I am willing to look past this. As you say, there are far more important matters at hand. Let us start anew, shall we?”

Yes! She knew she was on to something! Zelda smiled, but managed to keep herself in check.

“Tell me, Queen of Hyrule, how much have you learned of the coming conflict?”

The… the what now?

“Dry sands take me,” Naibi muttered, rubbing her face. “You were chosen as the Champion of Hylia, and yet you are just as clueless—”

“I know that Hylia is dead,” Zelda said quickly, not considering the implications. She was pretty sure she heard Laylith choke behind her. Naibi cocked her head. She looked to be waiting for more.

Nothing for it, she supposed. Worst case, Zelda could always have the woman done away with if necessary.

But what did she know? She tried to put together the pieces.

“I know that Hylia died to save Hyrule from the Blin,” she said, staring straight ahead as her mind worked, trying to figure out what it was the Gerudo woman was looking for. “She used the last of her power to act through me to save my kingdom when all hope was lost. To be honest, I never really considered what an action like that meant before now. I always assumed that helping us to repel the Blin was cause enough? —But no, that never sat right with me either. Before… whatever happened, that forced Hylia into that state? There is no way a mere Blin army—no matter how large, no matter with how many Warlords or Exiled Kings leading them—there is no way any mortal force could possibly stand before the Mother of Creation.”

Ahead of her, Naibi grinned. “You do not know the half of it.”

Zelda shuddered. Why did she always have to be right? “I confess, Naibi,” she said. “I am terrified. Here we are discussing a force that defeated a Goddess. In the face of that?” She looked up, shaking her head. “But if there’s anything that we can do—any way at all to get ahead of what might be coming next, I’m willing to do whatever it takes. And if the Lynx has any information that might help?”

She watched as Naibi rose from her seat and walked, slowly, carefully, deliberately around to her side of the table. She stopped and sat at the corner, a few feet away, just as Link rustled at her side.

“Zelda, Queen of Hyrule,” she said. “Make no mistake: the fate of the world hangs in the balance. We must move quickly. The game has been set in motion, and it may yet be too late. You must come with me at once to speak with the Lynx. Only she can provide you with further direction.”
