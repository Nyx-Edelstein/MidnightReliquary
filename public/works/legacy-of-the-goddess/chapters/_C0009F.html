Carefully, and with all due consideration of the threat from fiends, Link made his way through the thick brush around camp. After a day’s worth of travel from the temple, they’d stopped in between the banks of Lake Hylia and the surrounding woods. Link had taken a small scouting party to comb the area while the others set up camp.

If he was being honest with himself, this level of precaution wasn’t strictly necessary. They had six Sheikah around to guard the Queen, and twenty-six perfectly capable guardsmen besides. A fiend (or twelve) was hardly worth the concern. Truth be told, he was feeling somewhat superfluous with that kind of muscle around. He understood perfectly well Zelda’s paranoia—she had after all just been the target of an assassination attempt not two weeks prior—but even he had to admit this was overkill.

Still, he couldn’t be satisfied that he had fully discharged his duty until the surrounding area had been canvassed to regulatory standards. After all, it was always better to be proactive than to have something large and deadly blunder into camp overnight.

The sound of a branch snapping nearby grabbed his attention. Link whirled about, ready to meet the threat with an arrow drawn from his quiver. Out of the corner of his eye, he saw Cove pivot and leap forward, he too ready to face whatever threat had—

“Ack! Sorry, sorry!” Burdah said sheepishly, holding up his hands. “Don’t shoot! Sorry, just bad footing. It’s been hours, and to be frank I’m getting a bit tired.”

Link sighed, returning the arrow to his quiver. By his reckoning, they were about half an hour away from the main camp at a brisk walking pace. Scouting to that distance on foot was a bit excessive, even he had to admit. There didn’t seem to be much out here, and stumbling around through the woods in the dark of night for much longer wouldn’t do them any good if they were starting to tire out.

“Alright, I think we’ve done enough,” Link said. “Let’s head back.”

“’Bout time!” Cove said jovially, jogging over to give him a pat on the back. Link rolled his eyes. He appreciated Cove’s enthusiasm, his skill, and at times his company, but more often than not he could be a bit much to handle.

“Hey buddy. Since we’re no longer on duty looking out for all manner of terrible nasties, mind if I ask you a question?”

“Go ahead,” Link said, already regretting it.

“What’s going on between you and the Queen?” he said, wagging his eyebrows.

“Nothing’s going on between me and the Queen,” Link grunted. “It’s not like that.”

“I mean, I’m just sayin’. You two spend an awful lot of time together. Alone. In private. With no one else around.” He gave Link a wink and a nudge on the shoulder. “C’mon! What’s it like? Just between us. And for the record, ‘Nothing’s going on,’ is exactly what you would say if there were—in fact—something going on. You can’t deny that.”

“Seriously,” Link said, stepping over a pile of leaves and brushing away a low-hanging branch. “It’s nothing. Knock it off, okay?” He knew Cove meant well, but by the Mother if he wasn’t a strain on the nerves.

“Fiiiine,” Cove said, his expression dropping to an exaggerated frown with shoulders hunched. Link knew better than to be taken in. “Sorry, if I offended,” he said. “Just trying to pass the time.”

They were approaching the treeline now. Just a couple more minutes and they’d be back in sight of camp.

“So you’re a Lightsworn now, I hear,” Cove said eventually, when Link refused to take his bait. “What’s that like?”

“I don’t know. Not yet. I’m not one yet” Link replied, slowing to a halt. Something was off. He could feel it. But what?

“Oh, come on. How can you be so sure? You don’t feel any different? Don’t you feel the power of the Holy Mother surging through you with each and every beat of your heart? How sure are you that you can’t turn evildoers into stone at the slightest glance?”

“Heh, that’d be something,” Link said, musing. He turned his head, trying to make out what it was that got his attention.

…Nothing.

“Look, let’s just get back to camp, okay?”

“Aye, I’m ready to be done with this shite,” Burdah said, joining in. “Can’t wait to get some decent food. And some shut-eye before dawn would be nice, too. If it’s all the same to you, that is. No offense, sir.”

His tone was amicable enough, but Link recognized the rebuke, gentle as it was. The soldier had a point.

“Fine, fine,” Cove said, throwing up his hands in exasperation. “I’ll lay off it. Let’s get on with it.”

The trio ventured forth out of the trees and headed towards camp along the banks of the lake. The shore was mostly empty, save for some crags in the distance and a boulder a ways in front. Link could just barely make out the flickering lights of campfire in the distance.

Everything was fine. That was, until the boulder took a few steps away from them. Link held his hand up, and the other two stopped in their tracks.

“That,” Burdah whispered, “looks to be a kodongo.” He carefully placed his waterskin on the ground and readied his spear.

“No sudden movements,” Link said. “If we’re lucky, it’ll go on its way and won’t bother us.”

The beast in question was definitely a younger member of the species, only being about the size of two grown men. A quadrupedal, lizard-like creature, with a scaly hide that tapered off to a stubby tail at it’s rear end. Link noted it was chewing on some plant or another, knife-length fangs glistening in the moonlight as the rest of its jaw masticated away. He noted the length of the horn on its snout; definitely an adolescent.

“It’s not moving,” Cove whispered after about a minute. “I don’t think we have a choice.”

Link grunted and drew his sword; arrows would do little to nothing against the creature’s thick hide. He motioned for Cove to sweep left and Burdah to go right, as he cautiously stalked up to the creature head-on. Three paces. Eight. Twelve. The other two followed suit. It didn’t yet seem to notice them. Steady, they continued. Link’s hair stood on end. He was ready to fly into action at the slightest provocation. He knew kodongos could charge from a dead stop faster than a loosed arrow, on land or in water.

At last, they were in place. Link nodded to the other two, and with a shout, he ran forward, thrusting his sword into the creature’s neck. At the same instant from his left, Cove came barrelling toward their target. Sparks of red energy flew from his fist, and he buried his arm deep in the creature’s chest. Simultaneously, Burdah’s spear caught true, piercing straight through the kodongo’s temple and out the other side.

It barely had time to let out a pathetic whine before it jerked violently and collapsed to the ground.

Link pulled his sword free, wiping it clean.

“Gross,” Cove muttered, shaking blood and gore from his arm.

“Ah, dammit,” Burdah cursed. “Tip broke off my best spear.” He muttered something else unintelligible. “You two go on ahead,” he said. “Left my waterskin back on the shore. I’ll catch up.”

Link grunted his acknowledgement.

“So. What do you want to do with the carcass?” Cove asked as he sniffed his hand and turned his nose. “I reckon we should skin it. The leather’s hella useful, and the meat’ll make fine jerky.”

“AAAAAHHHHH!”

-o-

He was moving almost before it happened, shouting for the soldier to get back. Burdah managed to dodge the first charge, but wound up diving face-first into the dirt. The beast—far bigger, meaner, and nastier than its younger kin—skidded to a stop, throwing up a massive plume of dirt. It brandished its large horn and bared its dagger-like fangs with a challenging roar. The thing towered over Burdah as the middle-aged soldier clambered to his feet. Unarmed, Link put his odds of surviving a head-on attack at somewhere between nil and not good.

Link’s legs and chest burned from the exertion as he frantically tried to close the distance. He knew he wouldn’t make it. He knew he wouldn’t be able to get there in time, and that someone else was going to get hurt on his watch again. That someone was going to die on his watch, again.

…

No!

That wasn’t going to happen. Not this time. He would be fast enough this time.

The moment stretched out to eternity. Burdah raised his arms over his head to protect himself. The beast tensed, muscles rippling under its scaly hide, ready to snap forward and crush the man underfoot like a twig. Link held his sword high, gritting his teeth and preparing to strike.

He had to be faster.

Like the key of a lock falling into place, something inside him clicked. A response to a need, spoken in the right language for the world itself to understand. His will called out, and his magic answered with power. His feet pounded divots in the ground, and he tore forward at blistering speed. The great lizard turned its head in challenge to the new threat…

…But it was too late.

“HYAAAHHH!” Link shouted, pouring every fibre of his being into the attack. His sword severed flesh and bone with a single precision strike, and another dead carcass hit the ground, decapitated cleanly at the neck. Link slid to a stop a few feet away.

Panting. Dizzy.

Link wobbled. His insides felt like they were on fire. Exhausted, he let his blood-soaked sword slip from his grip as he fell to one knee. When he caught his breath and regained his bearings, Burdah and Cove were staring at him in awe.

“Not a Lightsworn, huh?” Cove said, eyes wide. “One more question before I shut up for the night, sir. Where do I sign up?”

-o-o-o-

“You should’ve seen it! He was like woosh! and the kodongo was like RAAAWR! and then he was all HYAAH! and HE KILLED IT IN ONE STRIKE!”

Zelda watched with fascination as Cove recounted the tale to the delight of the dozen or so soldiers gathered around the campfire. Link, for his part, seemed to be doing his best to disappear as the men around him raved and hollered with the occasional clap on the shoulder or slap on the back. Poor guy.

“Gentlemen! I believe Arlinkar has had enough for now,” Zelda said, raising her voice to be heard. “Give him some space, please!”

Link raised his head an inch or two, gratitude showing plainly on his face.

“Fine, fine. You heard the Queen! Let’s let ’em be, lads!”

“Three cheers for the Hero of Castletown!” one of the soldiers exclaimed, raising a flask in toast.

“To the Queen’s Champion!”

“Hurrah! Hurrah! Hurrah!”

“Here here!” / “’eyy! Haha!” / “Now there’s a good lad!”

Zelda couldn’t help but smile. It wasn’t every day her champion awoke as a Lightsworn, and she was glad to see her retinue in good spirits. Most of the soldiers dispersed shortly thereafter, and Link waved to her as he left for his tent.

“Hey, Link! Just a moment,” she said, raising a hand as she called after him. Her curiosity was getting the better of her. She could always ask her questions later, but she knew it would be more difficult to sleep with her mind busy. Link halted and spun around.

“Something wrong?” he asked, looking concerned.

“No—I just had some questions, if you don’t mind?” she said. “I know you’re probably tired. It can wait until morning if you’d rather sleep.”

“No, of course,” he said, walking back and taking a seat nearby so they could converse at a more comfortable volume. “I doubt I’ll get to sleep right away anyway. Happy to answer any questions. But if it’s about the magic stuff, I don’t think I know any more than you do.”

Curious…

Zelda waved away an approaching guard nearby. Whatever it was, it could wait. Someone else offered her a drink and she turned it down. “Have you had training in other magics?” she asked. “I thought you only practiced green affinity. Though I suppose you’ve seen plenty of red mages in combat…”

“No. I mean, yeah. Er, only green,” he said, visibly struggling to get his thoughts in order. “Honestly, I don’t know what I did or how I did it. It kind of just happened.”

“So what, you just used Invigorate out of nowhere?”

He shrugged.

“Fascinating.” It went without saying that Zelda was envious. It took her years of training to be able to use red magic at all, and here he was displaying mastery on his first attempt. Using it in combat like that—in conjunction with a weapon, no less—was no small feat. People like Impa and Cove trained their whole lives to be able to do the things they did. If he was one of the best swordsmen in the kingdom before, he now had no rival.

Someone got her attention out of the corner of her eye. Grenja looked to be starting up his knife antics again, and Zelda signaled to Koja to keep him from getting too carried away. This lot; it was almost more trouble than it was worth having so many Sheikah around. “So can you use other magics too? she asked, half distracted. “Have you tried Telepathy? Transformation?”

“I haven’t tried, no,” he said, scratching his head. “And if I can, I don’t even know where I’d start. Like I said, I don’t know how I did it. I just needed to get to Burdah faster, and I felt… I felt like my magic just answered. It gave me exactly what I needed in the moment.”

“Huh.” So not only did he have other affinities, but he could use magic he had never practiced intuitively. Zelda’s thoughts went immediately to the forgotten arts. “Do you think if we set up the right circumstances, you might be able to use other abilities? Like Stasis?”

“I’m not sure?” he said. “But I’d be up for trying it. —I don’t want to put anyone in danger, though,” he hurried to amend.

“Oh, no. Of course not,” Zelda huffed. She supposed the situation wasn’t that dire. Yet. Besides, if they had to, she was sure she could concoct a scenario where Link only believed someone to be in danger.

…Hmm. But would that even work? Would she need to fool Link, or Link’s magic?

“Seriously,” Link said, glaring at her. “I don’t want anyone to get hurt.”

Zelda raised her palms in a placating gesture. “No one has to get hurt, Link,” she said, smiling. “We’ll think of something.”

“Okay,” he said. “We can try.” He nodded in affirmation, though somewhat hesitantly.

They probably needed to get to bed soon. It was still four? Five days to Last Bastion, and keeping up a good pace was rough going. She had so many questions though! And she was eager to see Link get proper training.

“We’re set to arrive in Kakariko tomorrow, right?”

“Just before sundown, yeah. Earlier if we continue to make good time.”

Zelda nodded. “I know we’re on the move right now and we don’t have much time to spare, but I want to see you properly trained at the earliest opportunity,” she said. She held up a finger, preempting his objection. “We have more than enough hands to handle the grunt work around camp—you can use that time to train instead. I can teach you the basics of blue magic myself. Grenja should be able to handle red, and Koja can help you shore up your green training where it might be lacking. And once we’re back at Castletown, I’ll see to it you have access to the best instructors available. I’m sure I don’t have to tell you of all people this, but having a combat-capable mage as skilled as you with all affinities is kind of a big deal.”

“Heh, yeah” he said, blushing. “And I really appreciate that. I’ll put everything I have into training, I promise. Though I don’t want to get my hopes up. I mean, it would be weird if I didn’t, considering the circumstances. Have all the affinities, I mean. Still…”

Humble to a fault, as always. Zelda admired that about him. So many other men would be full of themselves, were they in his shoes right now. She wasn’t sure he fully understood his place in the world, and how much he potential he had to change the future of their kingdom for the better. …Or maybe he did, and that was in part why he was so level-headed.

…He was just so dedicated. Selfless. Compassionate. Though he always pushed himself too hard, and often forgot to take care of himself, to the point he had to be reminded—or commanded—to relax and recuperate.

It wasn’t as if Zelda couldn’t relate to that. Goddesses knew she struggled with the fact that she wasn’t more competent. That she was so small, and that even in her role as Queen there were hard limits on what she could do and what she could accomplish. Though looking at it from that angle, she supposed she probably was too harsh on herself most of the time…

She glanced over at Grenja with his massive ego and bullish bravado, currently in the act of juggling knives to impress the others. Koja with her impish whimsy and carefree, lackadaisical nature. Cove with his faux innocence and playful exuberance that he leaned so heavily into. They all had their ways of coping with the harshness of reality, she knew.

But Link? He never let his limitations get in the way. Nothing ever bothered him, and everything he did was for the greater good. She tried to see the world through his eyes. Here was a man who was willing to go to damnation and back in the name of duty. Literally. He was willing to endure excruciating agony at the drop of a hat, and even die if need be, in service of his kingdom. This was the same man who led sixty soldiers in a suicide charge to save an entire regiment during a hopeless battle—and succeeded. For him, no risk was too great, as long as it made sense on balance.

But what was it that motivated him? He cared, she knew. But so did she. Most did—excluding literal psychopaths, but they were the exception. Most people, under the hardened exterior they had developed to survive in the world, were good at heart. But however noble, they were also selfish—at least a little bit, from time to time. Herself included.

But him? No; his dedication to service bordered on fanaticism. …And the more she thought about it, the clearer it was. No one was that selfless by nature. No one got that way without reason. What had he been through? What had he done? What was he trying to prove? Not to the world, for he never cared about perception or the opinions of others. What was it he wanted so desperately to prove to himself?

“Usually I’m the quiet one,” he said softly, looking Zelda over with those pensive eyes of his. She met his gaze, but didn’t respond right away.

“What did I do this time? I know you’re worried about me. I can tell.”

Zelda started. She had to remind herself how perceptive he was. Precious little ever escaped his notice. …Was that why he was so awkward, socially? What would it be like, to be so attuned to every minute detail of your environment, and never able to turn it off?

In a word? Overwhelming.

“Zelda?”

“Yeah. Just thinking.”

“About what? If you don’t mind me asking.”

“About you.”

“Oh,” he said, looking away. His face turned a bright red.

“Hey. Stop that.”

“Stop what?”

Zelda cocked her head. “Link, I’m not daft. We had this conversation already. I know you’re attracted to me. You don’t have to be embarrassed every time you think about me that way. It’s okay, really. I find it flattering, even.” She sighed, running a hand through her hair. “But I can’t have you going red-faced and stiff as a board every time I give you the slightest bit of attention. It’s awkward, and it makes it difficult to have genuine interaction with you.”

“Ah,” he said, straightening up. “Okay. I’ll, uh… I’ll try to be better about that.”

“Haven’t you been with a girl before?”

He sputtered, burying his face in his palms. Zelda grinned. She took sordid pleasure in teasing him. It wasn’t even remotely fair, but he wasn’t ever going to make progress if he wasn’t pushed.

He took a slow, deep breath, then nodded. He even managed to look her in the eye. “A couple times, yeah.”

“Only a couple? I find that hard to believe,” she said, giggling. “Lesser men would be fighting them off with a stick.”

He chuckled nervously. “I mean, yeah. Sometimes. But relationships aren’t… haven’t ever really been a priority for me,” he said. “Don’t get me wrong. I like women as much as the next guy, but most of them aren’t all that interesting. And I’m usually pretty busy.”

“But you think I’m interesting?” Zelda said, driving in on the implication.

He nodded.

“And what is it you find interesting about me?”

“Er… you’re, uh…”

“Link.”

“I’m trying! Really. It’s just hard.”

“Yeah, I can see that,” she said, eyeing him.

“Gah!” he said, burying his face again.

Zelda tried not to laugh. She was having too much fun at his expense. “You were saying? I’m…?”

“…Well, you’re smart. And capable. And you have this… sort of air about you. Like you just expect people to do what you want, and everyone knows it and goes along anyway without fuss.” He shrugged. “Goes with being royalty, I guess. Kind of silly now that I think about it.”

“I don’t think that’s silly at all.”

“…You don’t?”

Zelda shook her head. “No, I don’t. I wasn’t born like that, you know. I was trained—every day of my life, or close enough. Groomed, one might say.” She let out a huff.

“Heh, yeah. That makes sense,” he said, picking up on her tone. “I can relate. About being forced into a role you didn’t choose, I mean. I know what that’s like.”

Zelda sensed an opening to probe him about his life before enlisting at Talus. She figured he would just clam up again if she asked directly, but with some work…

She adopted a frown, looking down at her feet. “Not that I’m not appreciative of the privileges and stature afforded to me by birth,” she said, pausing for effect, “but it wasn’t always pleasant, growing up in that kind of environment. I never wanted to be the Crown Princess. Far from it. But I was rarely, if ever, afforded the luxury of making choices for myself. I only ever really cared about my studies. History. Magic. Natural sciences. The philosophies. Ever since I read the stories as a child, I dreamed of growing up to be a Queen-scholar. Of leveraging my station, together with all the knowledge and research and bright minds at my disposal, to unearth lost secrets and restore our kingdom to its former glory—hey, don’t laugh! I know how that sounds.” Zelda rolled her eyes, laughing along with him. She knew that he too was idealist, so it only made sense to focus on that commonality.

“Father was often harsh with me,” she continued, letting a touch of gloom back into her voice. “He never approved. Always said I was too idealistic. That I needed to focus more on practicalities. That there were enough problems to solve already without creating more for myself at every turn. Lensa doubly so.” A sigh. “And he was right, in the end. I never really paid much mind to the practical stuff. Governance. Politics. Bookkeeping. Law. Keeping up appearances, fostering relationships and maintaining social ties to my subjects and the nobility. I made every excuse I could to shirk my duties and find more time for myself. And look where that’s gotten me.” She threw her hands up.

“I don’t think that’s true at all,” Link said, perking up. “All that stuff you always say, about lack of experience and not being…” he trailed off. “Sorry,” he said with a slight wince. “Probably not my place.”

Okay, she was actually interested in what he had to say now. But she had to put a stop to that for good.

“Link, don’t give me that horseshit,” she said plainly. “I respect you, and I know anything you have to say is genuine. Despite whatever you might think, advising me is exactly your place. You don’t ever have to hold back on my behalf—and don’t ever let me, or anyone else tell you otherwise. Even if I’m in a foul mood—I’ll come to my senses eventually. And I’ll only respect you more for your honesty, and for the courage to speak up. Understood?”

He nodded emphatically, sitting upright. “Yes, Your Grace. Of course.”

“Damnation, Link!” she grumbled, resisting the urge to yell at him. Why was this such a hard concept? “I’m not…” she took a breath, letting go of some of the tension. “I’m not ordering you in any official capacity. Please understand. I’m telling you: as a friend, I just want you to treat me like you would anyone else. That includes telling me what you really think, without worrying about reprisal. Or any other considerations that come from me wearing the thrice-damned crown. Okay?”

“Okay,” he said meekly. “I’m sorry—I understand, you. I do. It’s just… it’s habit, more than anything. Might take some getting used to, but I can do that for you. I will do that for you. I promise.”

“Good. Now, you were saying?”

He took several minutes to compose his words. Zelda gave him the space to do so, keeping her mind occupied with the flickering flames of the campfire a few feet ahead so as to not lose her sense of presence in the moment. This was important, and it deserved her full attention. However it played out, however he chose to proceed, she knew it would be a defining moment for the both of them.

“Zelda, you aren’t your father,” he said finally. She turned to listen. “Everyone knows that,” he said, “but that’s not a bad thing. It doesn’t have to be.” He hesitated, looking conflicted, then edged closer, lowering his voice. “What others—Lensa? Your father?—what they saw as weakness, I see as strength. You aren’t the person that focuses on the practicalities and keeps everything running smoothly day-to-day. You aren’t the person who takes bold, decisive action at the first opportunity. And you aren’t the person who cares about the trappings of high society.”

Slowly, carefully, he placed his hand on her arm. Zelda was impressed; that was a bold move for him. She looked to him intently. Where was he going with this?

“What I think, is that you knew all along none of that was for you. And despite the incredible pressure to go along with it anyway—to do as you were told, to do what was expected—you fought back. In your own way. In the only way you could. Because you knew, deep down, that you had to do something else. It’s like you told me before. How long has this been happening? How long has the kingdom been in decline? How many kings and queens have made the sensible decisions each and every day, focusing on what needed to be done just to keep everything going? Zelda: you aren’t the person that sees that and looks the other way. You’re the one who sees that and looks to the future. You inspire me, and I want to help you find that future.”

“Oh,” was all she could manage to say. She had to wipe away a tear. How was he capable of such eloquence? No one… no one had ever spoken to her like that. Not that she could recall. No one had ever told her…

Before she knew what was happening, he had her in an embrace. Her breath caught in surprise, but she relaxed, settling her arms on his shoulders.

This was… nice. For once, she felt understood. Appreciated, even. She also felt safe. Protected. She felt…

Oh.

Oh dear.

Link withdrew, and it now was her turn to hide her face. It had to be radiating heat. She fought to keep her breath in check.

Damn this man! What the hell?! Who in damnation was he to—

“Zelda? Everything okay?”

“Yep! Everything’s fine!” Don’t do anything you’re going to regret. Don’t do anything you’re going to regret. Don’t…

“Are you sure? Because it looks like—”

Before she knew what she was doing, she had him by the collar, pressing her lips against his. She ran her fingers through his thick, messy hair, delighting in the feel of it against her skin. She placed her other hand on his chest, and…

Oh dear. Ohhhh dear. She slipped back into her seat on the log. Oh, no. What had she just gotten herself into?

“Wow,” he said. “I, uh. I wasn’t expecting…”

—Oh, to hell with it. “Shut up, and come with me to my tent,” she whispered in his ear.

“…Er, are you sure? Because you said before that—”

“Link. Shut up. And come with me. To my tent.”
