The Gate blazed to life. The portal’s surface transformed from mere window into passage, its depths swirling with currents of temporal energy that pulled at her with unrelenting force. Zelda flew forward, drawn like a magnetic needle aligning within a compass—the Sword of Evil’s Bane unerringly seeking its mark.

Reality fractured around her like a mirror shattering in reverse. Chronology unraveled into prismatic streams that carried her through an ocean of ephemeral instants. She fell through the kaleidoscope of eternity, past and future blurring together until causality existed not as sequence but as symphony, each moment a note resonating with all others across the infinite expanse of what-was and what-might-be.

And then she was there.

Divinity, Concept, and Substance twisted and merged at their boundaries, and through their cascading resonance, Zelda's consciousness cohered. The Golden Power sparked from the sword and surged through her veins, Nayru's Gift illuminating the mathematical perfection of the moment as the Fi-blade calculated trajectories and probabilities with absolute precision, guiding her descent.

The battlefield erupted into existence around her. The air burned with heat and magic, thick with the reek of spilled blood and divine fury. The sky bled with unnatural wrongness, reality itself warping under the weight of powers it struggled to contain.

The Demon King began to turn, some celestial instinct alerting him to the wrongness, the violation of causality itself. But even his transcendent awareness couldn’t react to an attack that arrived before it departed, couldn’t defend against an achronal alpha-strike that defied the laws of time.

The blade struck true—not with a slash or thrust, but with a severing. The ultimate concept of separation didn’t merely cut through the god; it unmade him.

Light and Void and divine fury condensed into a single point, concentrated with such overwhelming force that the very air ignited. The Godcleaver sang a note of perfect annihilation as it passed through the Demon King’s back and emerged from his chest in a fountain of black flame.

Zelda slammed into the earth before him, the momentum of her impossible strike throwing a plume of dust into the air. She came to rest, her weapon still blazing in a white-knuckled grip.

Voidshimmer radiance exploded outward in cascading waves of iridescent darkness—a shockwave that echoed throughout eternity. Reality screamed as the propagating wavefront spread through the magical field, rippling across physical, conceptual, and spiritual planes alike, shattering and reshaping everything around this singular, eternal moment.

Zelda rose slowly, her entire body trembling. Behind her, the obliterated remnants of what had been the Demon King scattered across scorched earth—fragments of armor dissolving into ash, traces of flame guttering and dying, the terrible presence that had weighed upon reality simply fading into nothingness.

She turned, and her breath caught.

Hylia stood before her, radiant and terrible in her divine glory. Tears streamed down the Mother’s face—crystalline droplets that caught the burning light of the ravaged sky before falling to the earth and vanishing.

“I am so sorry for summoning you here, my child,” she said, her voice heavy with grief.

Zelda’s mind went blank. Summoning her? She’d just spent subjective days learning the Repository’s controls, searching through endless archives, meticulously constructing the exact moment through sheer determination and cunning manipulation of—

“Farewell, Shūen,” Hylia whispered.

The words shattered Zelda’s thoughts. She looked again between the goddess’s weeping face and the scattered remains of the Demon King. Why address the Enemy with such—

“…Oh,” Zelda said as the realization struck. “I suppose that would make sense.”

It seemed even Hylia was Hylian.

A long, pained silence settled between them, and with it came understanding that cut deeper than any blade. The Mother’s tears continued to fall—each one a fractal eternity of grief shattering against the scorched earth. Hylia was wounded. Not physically—but spiritually. The pain in her eyes was achingly familiar: the same grief Zelda had felt when duty demanded sacrifice, when love and necessity opposed each other and no path forward existed without surrendering something precious.

“I must ask you to carry an unfathomable burden, Zelda Numenia Proventia Hyrule,” the Mother Goddess said at last, her voice steady.

The full weight of it struck Zelda like a fall through ice into black water.

This had already happened.

She hadn’t accomplished anything by coming here. She had merely fulfilled a role written for her before her birth, before her world’s founding, before even the concept of Hyrule had taken shape in the ever-shifting currents of could-be.

“Go, my child,” Hylia whispered. “Be the Warrior Queen that I could never be.”

The words hung in the air between them—simultaneously command, plea, and benediction. Zelda felt something break open inside her chest, then. Something terrible and building. Her eyes narrowed.

“I am no warrior,” she said coldly, enunciating each syllable with deliberate precision. The venom-laced words tore from her throat before she could stop them, seething with a fury that had been smoldering since the moment she’d learned of her guardian’s fate. “Impa was a warrior!”

The Mother winced, lowering her head as if physically struck by Zelda’s words. “The mightiest of foes fell to her raw might and magical acumen,” she offered, her voice half-whisper.

Zelda summoned the memory of lying in the infirmary like a shard of ice, letting it crystallize in her mind: “She was the mother I never had!”

Hylia gasped and dropped to one knee. “I did not intend offense, my child! I mean only to say that her death was a grave injustice.” Her voice carried with it genuine pain.

Zelda balked, understanding forming in her mind with bitter clarity.

It seemed even divinity was a scarce resource.

She wiped at her own tears—anguish and grief and anger mixing into something too complex to name. Her breath came ragged, but she forced her voice level. “Why Link?”

“Given your bond with Impa, he was the closest reciprocal counterbalance.”

The question erupted from somewhere deep and primal. “That’s not—why not save Impa?!”

“In another world, I did, Zelda!” Hylia grasped her chest, bowing her head with a reverence that could almost pass for shame. “Just not yours.”

Zelda gasped. The confirmation of what she’d already suspected—that infinite variations played out across infinite worlds, that somewhere Impa lived while here she died…

She took a moment to breathe, feeling the storm of emotion threatening to overwhelm her. She let it flow through her—acknowledging its presence without allowing it to consume her entirely.

“So then why not choose Link as your champion?” She asked, already suspecting the answer.

Hylia lowered herself further, palms now pressed to earth. The words went unspoken—but Zelda understood. And she realized too the symbolic weight of this moment: the Mother Goddess prostrating herself before her own Divine Majesty.

Zelda stood frozen, the Godcleaver still thrumming with reality-defying power in her grip. Part of her—the part still aching with Impa’s absence—wanted to hold onto her anger, to make Hylia feel the weight of every choice. But another part, quieter and steadier, recognized the same awful truth she already knew: that even the divine power of the Mother Goddess wasn’t enough to save everyone. Hylia hadn’t chosen to spare Link and sacrifice Impa out of cruelty or indifference—Zelda simply found herself on the one side of fate where her guardian and protector fell while a hero and champion rose.

Understanding flowed through her. She released her hold on her weapon’s enhancement, letting the Void-darkness dissipate, allowing Fi to return to the aether. The sustained conceptual heightening fueled by the Golden Power and her own limitless magic withdrew like a receding tide. The reality-defying weapon returned to something more mundane—merely a legendary sword imbued with the power to banish evil.

She tossed it aside. Steel clattered against stone, its light dimming to barely a glow.

“I will not be a Warrior Queen,” Zelda said with quiet resolve.

Hylia’s composure shattered. Her shoulders shook with violent sobs—wrenching sounds too raw to come from a goddess. Yet threaded through the anguish came something else: breathless laughter, bright and disbelieving, joy and sorrow twining together. She pressed her palms harder against the scorched earth as if anchoring herself against the storm of emotion that consumed her—mourning what she’d lost while appearing to rejoice at Zelda’s proclamation.

Zelda felt something twist in her chest—an unexpected sympathy born from witnessing pain so visceral, so utterly mortal in its raw intensity. She took an involuntary step forward, then halted. “What must I do?”

There was a long silence as Hylia composed herself with visible effort.

“Go with my blessing, Zelda,” the Mother of Creation said, at once pleading and resolute. “Go and bring peace to the Realm.”

Zelda glanced behind her at the scattered remnants that had been Demise—towards ash and dying flame and fragments of a dead god—then back to Hylia.

“I was going to do that anyway.”

Hylia laughed—a sound so meek and broken it barely deserved the name—even as her form began to dissolve into luminous mist that caught the burning light of the ravaged sky. “If only I had summoned you sooner,” she whispered, her words carrying the weight of infinite regret, of a goddess who had sacrificed everything and still felt it was not enough.

Reality twisted.

The battlefield vanished. Time reversed its flow—not gradually, but all at once. Zelda felt herself ripped through layers of causality, backwards through the currents of temporal energy that had carried her here. The Gate’s power seized her, drew her back through the impossible distances she’d crossed.

The grotto materialized around her as she fell onto her back. The Sentinels stood in their eternal vigil. The control panel gleamed with faint ethereal light exactly as she’d left it.

And Zelda was alone once more, suspended in the Repository at the edge of the Void between Worlds, burdened by the weight of impossible knowledge and the echo of a goddess’s dying wish.

Go with my blessing, Zelda. Go, and bring peace to the Realm.
