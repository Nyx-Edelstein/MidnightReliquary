Zelda came to a stop at the doors to her study. Shesan waited for her inside.

Lucia.

Her heart thrummed in her chest as she reached for the door handle. She hesitated, and her fingers flit nervously. She turned to pace back and forth.

She wanted, needed, and had no choice but to speak with the Fae, but also she had no idea how this worked. Was Lucia in control of Shesan now? How did Shesan make contact in the first place? It had to be through Vesyrn, right?

…Was that something Fi could learn to do?

But more pertinently—could Lucia possess someone else? Had Shesan been aware of this possibility? Had she been coerced? What was Zelda risking by having this meeting?

She had to remind herself that Lucia once came to Hyrule’s aid during the Gerudo Wars, in one of the kingdom’s darkest moments. The official recounting was that Hylia herself descended from the heavens and put a stop to fighting, allowing leaders on both sides of the conflict to come to an agreement. However, Vestele insisted that it was not Hylia who came to their aid, but Lucia. The very same entity waiting for her on the other side of these doors.

How powerful was this Fae? Could she help them turn the tide against the enemy?

And if so, what would she demand in return?

Zelda took a deep breath and stood tall. There was only one way to find out, and time was ticking. They’d have little privacy on the journey south. She wasn’t sure how long they had before needing to leave, but it wasn’t enough.

She pushed the doors open and stepped inside, letting them close shut behind her. Inside, Shesan sat at the meeting table by the hearth with her legs crossed, sipping tea and perusing one of Zelda’s journals.

Zelda strolled over, snatched her journal back, and pulled out a chair.

“Hello again, Zelda,” Shesan said, seemingly unaffected.

“Shesan,” Zelda answered warily as she sat down. “It is still Shesan in there, right?”

Shesan snorted and treated Zelda to a smug grin as she set the cup down on the table. “I’m not possessed, if that’s what you’re asking.”

Zelda tensed. That wasn’t a clear answer, and the exchange earlier seemed to indicate otherwise. Fi wasn’t alerting her to a potential lie, but that didn’t mean—

“Ugh. Look, there’s nothing—”

“—How do I know I can trust you?” Zelda said, cutting her off.

Shesan leaned forward, placing both palms on the table. “That’s a good question,” she said. “And you’re right to be suspicious. My answer to that is the same as it’s always been: actions, not words.”

Zelda eyed her skeptically. “Then what was that earlier? That sounded exactly like how Link described Urik’s possession.”

Shesan leaned back and tossed one leg over the other. “Technically speaking, the term would be ‘coinhabitance,’ as it refers to a voluntary compact and not an involuntary one.”

Uh-huh. “Can it become involuntary?”

Shesan waved a hand. “Not really. It’s complicated—not worth worrying about.”

Zelda wasn’t so sure about that, but it answered one question at least. “So you trust her, then?”

A shrug. “As much as I trust anyone, sure.”

That wasn’t saying much. “And what did she do to earn your trust?”

Shesan grinned. “It’s more about what she wants, and what she hasn’t done to get it.”

Zelda cocked her head and narrowed her eyes. She was about to complain about never getting straight answers, but stopped short as she noticed Shesan tense up and glance away.

Perhaps a different tack? She relaxed into her seat. Maybe if—

“This would probably work better if you spoke to her directly,” Shesan said before Zelda had a chance to gather her thoughts. “You and I rarely see eye to eye.”

Hmm. That was a surprising degree of awareness for her. Was she showing… respect? Zelda sat forward and laced her fingers together, setting her chin atop them as she pondered.

“Okay then,” she said after a moment. “How does that work?”

Shesan rolled her eyes with a huff as she extended a hand across the table. Cautiously, Zelda took it, knowing what to expect by now.

The world flashed white as she was transported somewhere else.

She blinked as she took in their surroundings. They stood on nothing, in the middle of a dull-grey, featureless landscape. Shesan held her hand, occupying roughly the same place she had at the table. Zelda let go and turned to her left. There floated a faintly glowing figure with long silver hair, scantily clad in a mesh of vines and leaves. Metallic shackles with shifting chromatic hues bound the Fae’s wrists, but the chains did not connect to anything and dangled freely in the air.

The Fae, Lucia, held her arms wide with palms up, but said nothing.

Zelda looked back at Shesan with concern.

“A symbolic gesture,” Shesan said. “Those have more meaning in this place. You’ll see.”

Zelda nodded slowly as she felt pieces clicking into place. —Wait, no. She shook her head vigorously. “Where is ‘this place?’”

“Good question,” Shesan said. “I believe you would call it the ‘Spiritual Realm,’ but of that I’m not certain.”

Even if so, that didn’t tell her much.

Zelda sighed. Okay. Taking that at face value… somehow, symbolic gestures meant “more” here. Palpably more, yes. She could feel it, even if it didn’t quite make sense and she couldn’t quite explain it.

Hmm. She turned again to Lucia. Still floating impassively, arms outstretched. Welcoming. Inviting.

Zelda let her instincts take over. She stepped forward and extended a hand. The Fae shook it.

“Well met, Zelda,” Lucia said, dropping a foot and a half to stand on the… nothing. They were roughly the same height, Zelda noted. And she looked exactly like Niesis.

“You know who I am?” Zelda asked.

Lucia nodded. “And you know who I am.”

It was not phrased as a question, but Zelda found herself nodding anyway.

She asked the very first question that came to mind. “Earlier, you mentioned you were ‘looking forward to working with me again.’ When was the last time we worked together, and how did it go?”

Lucia opened her mouth to speak, then stopped and smiled. “Pray tell, what year is it?”

Zelda raised an eyebrow. She didn’t know? “Year 548 of the Common Era.”

Lucia continued to smile impassively. “Allow me to rephrase. How long has it been since Basilius Rhomar took up the crown?”

Okay, that piqued her interest. She supposed Hylian calendar time wouldn’t make much sense if Lucia had lived—or was previously more active?—in a different era, and had been locked away all this time.

She shook her head slightly as she refocused. She was having trouble concentrating. “That would have been… Year 414 of the Forsaken Era,” she answered. “So in absolute terms… 765 years ago.”

Lucia laughed with a playful glint in her eye. “Then that is your answer.”

“So the legends are true?” Zelda asked, though she felt she already knew the answer. Lucia had all but confirmed it with her last question.

“That depends,” the Fae said. “Which legends, specifically?”

“The Reaffirmation? The legend of the Frozen Armies, and how…” Wait. She shook her head again as her thoughts caught up to her. There was something about this place—something strange, where things just made more sense than they normally did, and yet somehow didn’t make sense at all. “Hold on,” she said, raising a finger. “Those were my ancestors. Are you saying I’m the reincarnation of Queen Cordatia Hyrule?”

“More or less,” Lucia said with an upturned hand.

“But that doesn’t answer my question,” Zelda said, trying not to show any frustration. She wasn’t frustrated—not really—she just wanted a clear answer. “What does it mean to be… a reincarnation of…”

She stopped mid thought as she felt something tugging within her that expanded to encompass the whole of her attention. Lines flowing outward from her being that wove into a fabric, extending into the past and unwinding again to individual threads that terminated at her ancestors. The structure was there in her mind, and yet it was not. She could feel its abstract, symbolic meaning, even as every bit of it fell apart under rational scrutiny.

“Huh,” she said aloud.

“You are the Blood of Hylia, yes?” Lucia asked.

Zelda nodded.

“So in a sense, you are what remains of the Goddess Hylia in this world.”

She felt something then. A glimpse of something strange. Something wonderful. Something beautiful. But the whole of its meaning evaded her grasp. Zelda sighed as she buried her face in her palms. “I believe you, I do. But I still don’t understand what that means, in practical terms.”

“Give it time, and you may,” Lucia said.

So many questions. And every answer yielded more still. “But what does that make you? A Fae?”

“What is it you think you know of Fae?” Lucia asked with an impish grin.

Zelda paused to collect her thoughts. She decided it was best to be truthful and honest. “Vestele—the current High Priestess of the Hylian Faith—told me they were… that you are… shells, she said. Husks. Fragments of Hylia that broke off from her, traveled through the Void, and made their way here. —To our world, I mean,” she amended as she glanced about. “She said that you sometimes have her memories, but rarely do you care for us.”

Lucia gave a single nod. “I was ‘born’ when Cordatia’s mother, Justitia Venias was murdered, along with her brother, King Domhall Gavrillius, and his wife and their two infant daughters. Those deaths left behind a Void. I am that which filled that Void.”

Zelda was just about to ask, “A void in what?” when she felt the answer instead.

Perfect stillness. Absolute emptiness. Unchanging. Forever. Eternal. Infinite.

She shuddered. She had pondered this concept before, and the memory of that cold emptiness chilled her to the bone. She thought back to her half-remembered fever dreams at the Temple of Time. “Does that mean you have memories from when you were Hylia?”

“In a manner of speaking,” Lucia said.

Zelda considered for a long moment and composed her next query carefully. “Care to enlighten me?”

Lucia laughed again, though this time it was more amusement than joy. “That would be too dangerous for you, I fear. I do not wish to risk it. Perhaps when you have bonded the other fragments of Wisdom, we can speak of this again.”

“Okay,” Zelda said, unsure exactly what to make of that. Another thought came to her, and she decided to voice it. “My father was murdered,” she said solemnly. “Did that also leave behind a Void? …Is there another Fae we should be on the lookout for?”

Lucia turned to Shesan.

“No. It’s unlikely, anyway,” Shesan said. “Best not to worry about it.”

Zelda’s heart sank.

Was she imagining it, or did it darken a shade just now?

“You have more questions for me,” Lucia said. Again, a statement, not a question.

So many questions. Where to begin? Zelda thought back to what Shesan said just earlier. “What is it you want?” She winced. “Sorry, that came out more blunt than I was intending.”

Lucia raised her chin and crossed her arms. A long pause made Zelda feel distinctly uncomfortable under her gaze. “As before, I seek to avert calamity,” the Fae said.

That was promising, if vague. “So… what can you tell me about the enemy?”

Lucia turned again to Shesan. Shesan crossed her arms and shook her head.

“It would not be wise for us to speak of this here and now,” Lucia said.

Zelda eyed Shesan warily. Why not? “Then when? Where?” she asked.

“Soon,” they both said in unison. “When you have bonded the fragments,” Lucia added. “I will reveal everything about this and more to you. Soon.”

Okay. That sounded like a firm commitment. She was okay with that. It was only a couple days away.

“What about Ganondorf? Or Ja’bu? Can you talk about them?”

A strange calm settled over her just then. Peaceful. Tranquil. And something was tugging at her awareness. A light at the center of—

“Best not speak that name here,” Shesan said. “I can’t protect you if you dive too deep.”

Zelda started and blinked. Dive too… deep?

“I do not know more than Shesan does on these topics,” Lucia said, drawing Zelda’s attention away. “This may come as a surprise, but I know little of your world, save for the relatively short window of time from my birth to… the events immediately thereafter.”

“You were imprisoned with the other Fae, then?” Zelda asked.

Lucia did not answer.

“Sorry. Is that something you’d rather not discuss?”

“I cannot speak to this,” Lucia said after a moment. “But the circumstances of my ‘imprisonment’ were… different.”

“I see,” Zelda said, feeling even more confused. Too many threads, all vying for her attention. It was so hard to pick just one. “What about other Void Entities? Can you—or are you willing—to tell me anything about them?”

“They are legion,” Lucia said. “Multitudes beyond counting.”

The landscape darkened as she spoke, until they were surrounded by an inky blackness that wasn’t quite night. Zelda gazed out into that unnatural darkness and noticed that it flowed over itself, pooling in some areas and leaving even darker voids behind. Those voids rhythmically collapsed and reopened… in a manner not unlike… blinking eyes…

Zelda gasped and jumped in fright as she realized she was being observed. The inky blackness faded, and they were again left in the dull-grey featureless landscape.

“They are many, and varied,” Lucia said. “Trust me when I tell you that you do not wish to contact them or in any way draw their attention. Further, should you or your friends ever find the name of one written down someplace: Do not speak it.”

Zelda gulped and wiped a bead of cold sweat from her brow. “Noted. So, uh… without referring to any specifically, are you… aware of any other hostile entities that might have a presence here? —In my world, I mean.”

“I am not,” Lucia said after a brief pause.

Zelda breathed a sigh of relief. One of her worst fears was that yet another entity like Ja’bu or Morudo Geira lay dormant somewhere in the Gorons’ territory. She wasn’t sure Hyrule could survive another cataclysmic reawakening of a long-thought-dead ancient evil.

“What about non-hostile ones? Do those exist?”

The landscape shifted again, this time brightening until they were surrounded by billowing clouds of white light. Three beings of radiant energy swam and flew through the clouds: A fearsome kraken, a mighty dragon, and a powerful serpent. Zelda watched on in awe.

“Lanayru, Eldin, and Faron,” Lucia said, giving a name to the shapes. “Guardian Spirits, tasked to protect the realm from intrusion in Hylia’s absence.”

The lights faded and the clouds retreated until they were once again left in the midst of mundane emptiness. Zelda felt a sense of longing as the light-entities retreated.

“If they have not yet intervened,” Lucia said, “then they are either slumbering and unaware of the threats, are engaged in struggles elsewhere… or are dead or otherwise incapacitated.”

“Wow…” was all Zelda could say. “How do we… is there a way to contact them?”

Lucia met her eyes. There was sadness and mourning in them. She shook her head. “I fear I am of no help here.”

“Okay,” Zelda said. Even if not, that gave her a glimmer of hope. Somewhere out there, there were entities who could help. Maybe.

She remembered just then the shimmering barrier from Shesan’s vision, and the mural Link described from the Obsidian Spire. “Could the Golden Goddesses—or what remains of their influence—still be present in the world in a way that could be used to our advantage?”

“There is the Triforce, and the Great Barrier,” Lucia said. “Beyond this, I cannot give you an answer that you would understand or find satisfying. Not without great danger to you, anyway.”

Zelda frowned. “Another ‘more fragments’ thing?”

“…You may need the entirety of Wisdom for this one.”

Zelda nodded, feeling somewhat unsettled at that. “Okay. And what about this ‘Great Barrier?’”

“You have seen it.”

“Yes, I mean—what is it?” Zelda amended. “And is it connected in any way to those Guardian Spirits?”

“It is not,” Lucia said. “It is however all that protects this world, and many others, from the Void.”

Zelda’s mind flashed back to the vision Shesan showed her in the desert. The writhing mass of dark flames and living shadow. The serpent coiled around the firmament, and the radiant fire it cradled within its scaly mass. The wonderful, terrible, beautiful, awesome spark that threatened to consume her, to burn away everything and all. It was the rising, triumphant thunder of a thousand horns heralding the coming of a hero, and the sad, mournful—

She snapped to as Shesan grabbed her and shook her by the shoulders. The woman raised a pointed eyebrow. “Careful. Not here. Understand?”

“But… No, I don’t understand. What about—”

“What I showed you before was carefully prepared,” Shesan said. “Do not draw its attention here.”

Zelda felt the blood drain away from her face as she nodded. “Yeah. Got it.” She glanced about. Again, that unsettling feeling. It was so easy to get lost in one’s thoughts here. And not just metaphorically.

Was that part of what made Fae so dangerous? It didn’t seem to bother Lucia. Or Shesan, for that matter. Zelda touched a finger to her chin. “Forgive me, but I have to ask. How are you so different from other Fae? Like Cyrae?”

“What do you know of Cyrae?”

Zelda started. That was phrased as a question, but was felt as a command to speak.

“You don’t have to answer that,” Shesan said.

Zelda turned to face her. “Should I not?”

She shrugged. “Up to you. No harm either way. Just letting you know—you are not compelled to answer.”

“Forgive me,” Lucia said, more gently this time. “Old habits.”

“Uhm… okay,” Zelda said meekly. Was she implying something there? “To answer your question: I don’t know much. Just more stories Vestele told me. I suppose Arminel’s Conquest was after your time?”

Lucia frowned, then nodded. “To answer your question: When we Fae use our powers… our power over the hearts and minds and essence of mortal beings… we tend to lose pieces of ourselves. No matter our initial aims… over time, we become something different than what we once were. And so, despite our origins, we are in this manner susceptible to corruption by the forces of evil. To answer your next question: I am yet a young Fae, and I have taken great care not to use my powers any more than absolutely necessary. And to answer the unasked question: Yes, that demonstration was both intentional, and necessary.”

Wow.

Zelda’s mind spun as she grappled with the implications. Is that what Shesan meant earlier, about what Lucia “hadn’t done?” She felt her attention drawn to the dangling chains around the Fae’s wrists, and the full weight of what those superficial shackles symbolized came crashing down on her all at once. She could feel the full force of Lucia’s presence lying dormant just beneath the surface, like the winds of a raging tempest as she stood in the eye of the storm. In that moment she knew for certain she was little more than an insect next to this being, and yet the Fae still chose to speak and treat with her as an equal.

Vertigo.

Zelda raised a hand to her head and stumbled as Shesan reached out to steady her. She felt a sense of awe, wonder, and terror at Lucia’s restraint. Could she do the same, if the roles were reversed? Likely not.

Something else clicked.

“Is this why Niesis imprisoned herself and the other Fae?”

“I cannot say,” Lucia said. “But from a certain perspective, that would not be an unreasonable course of action.”

Was that because she didn’t want to discuss it further, or because it was after her time? And how much of this did Vestele, the Sages, and the Seekers know?

Zelda felt another weight pressing in on her—this one sharper, more threatening. Her mouth felt dry, and she licked her lips. “Are you my ally?”

“Yes,” Lucia said without reservation.

“And does this mean… that one day you will become my enemy?”

“I would sooner die than allow that to come to pass,” the Fae answered. She turned to peer up at the nothing with a hand on her chin. “Though I suppose that process is like death, in a way.”

Zelda swallowed, pushing down her fear and taking a calming breath. The evasion was not lost on her, though she appreciated that Lucia chose to be so transparent. “Vestele seemed to believe that you require Void Power to sustain yourself,” she said, connecting more dots. “—Not that she phrased it in those terms, exactly. Is that true?”

“It is,” Lucia confirmed.

“So if you were to run out—”

“—I would die, yes. And… at least for now… I would prefer that to the alternative. But to answer your next question: One does not expect stories of Fae who quietly wither away into oblivion to survive the ages.”

“I see,” Zelda said. “So if I could provide you with Void Power instead… to sustain you without you needing to use your powers… without needing to feed on anyone’s essence…”

“I doubt we will reach that point,” Lucia said. “But if we do, I will keep your offer in mind.”

“I don’t intend any offense,” Zelda said, “but that wasn’t a confirmation.”

“You didn’t ask a question requiring a confirmation,” Shesan said. “Actually, I’m not sure that even counted as a question.”

Zelda eyed her curiously. Was she seriously implying—

“I know what you’re thinking,” Shesan said, arms crossed. “And I advise against it. She can’t lie to you, but she can evade and misdirect. And if you ask her any question thrice, she’s forced to give you a direct answer. But that does not mean she will allow you to ask, nor does it mean you have the fortitude to weather the storm. So to speak.” She glanced towards Lucia and rubbed her nose, then turned back to Zelda. “I fully expect this to fall on deaf ears, but—you really should not trifle with things you don’t understand.”

“Hah,” Zelda gibed. “Speaking from experience, are we?”

Shesan didn’t respond.

Curious. Zelda raised an eyebrow. “…Are you speaking from experience?”

“I’m not a Fae, Zelda.”

“Okay. But are you speaking from experience?”

“Unbelievable,” Shesan muttered. “You just can’t help yourself, can you? And yes, for what it’s worth, I am speaking from experience.”

A very loud and very paranoid voice in the back of Zelda’s mind refused to let her ignore the fact that Shesan had just given her a direct answer after being asked thrice. “Okay,” she said simply, deciding to file every bit of that mess away for later.

She turned back to Lucia. One thing in particular was bugging her, and she had to ask. “I can’t help but notice some parallels,” she said. “You know about Fi, right?”

“I do,” the Fae answered.

Come to think of it, could Fi manifest here? Zelda considered trying to call her, but didn’t want to risk it without checking with Shesan first, just in case.

“Are you in any way similar to her?” she asked, refocusing.

“That is a very broad question,” Lucia said.

“Fair,” Zelda responded, considering how to better phrase it. “Alright, allow me to rephrase: Do you see Fi as being an entity that is substantially similar to you in any important ways?”

“I do not.”

Zelda frowned. “Still—I can’t help but note that some of the things she can do—compelling sentient beings, for instance—seems very similar to the powers you claim to have. To the powers we’ve observed you exercising, even. That was you, back at the haunted pass, right?”

Lucia nodded. “Yes, of course.”

She paused to muse on a passing thought. “What happened to Urik, by the way?”

Lucia did not answer, and Zelda felt a cold chill creep up her spine. She shook her head, deciding not to press. “Anyway—are you willing to tell me anything about these similarities? Can Fi learn from you?”

“I am not willing to use my powers in any capacity, except when it is absolutely necessary,” Lucia said.

Zelda paused, then turned about, gesturing with upturned palms. “Then what about this?”

“This is… different,” Lucia said. “To give a simple and somewhat misleading, but not entirely wrong answer, I am leveraging Shesan’s powers to speak with you here and now. Not my own.”

“And Ryn?”

“On precisely two occasions I exercised influence over Ryn using my own power, focused through the pendant she wore. She remains unharmed.”

“I see,” Zelda said. “Thank you for that,” she added.

“Of course,” Lucia said with a smile.

Zelda pondered on all the Fae had just said. Yet again, these answers only raised more questions. “So how does that work? And is it related to the tale of Moraga and her Pendant of Amethyst Sands?”

Lucia was silent for a long moment. Just when Zelda was beginning to think it best to drop the subject and move on, she answered. “I can’t say I know to whom or what you refer, but that seems to me like a story based in truth. Gems such as these serve as a focus for our power. A trick we Fae use to preserve ourselves as long as possible and make the most of our magic. And before you ask: they are of no use to you.”

Interesting. So did that mean… the scepter back at the Temple of Time, with the chromatic opal…?

“I believe I have encountered another such item,” Zelda said.

“Shesan has informed me of this,” Lucia said before she could finish. “And yes, that sounds very much like a focus.”

Zelda nodded along. “Should we work on getting you a new focus?”

Lucia smiled warmly. “I appreciate the offer. If you can retrieve the pendant, that would be ideal. Otherwise, it will take me some time to make a new one. And for, I hope, obvious reasons, I would prefer not to do that.”

“Ah—that may prove difficult,” Zelda said with a nervous laugh. She turned to Shesan.

“We’re still working on it,” Shesan said. “We’re going to have to turn it over to the Seekers. It may have been possible to replace it with a fake… but that’s a tall order on such short notice. And it would not behoove us to do anything that might arouse suspicion.”

“Mm,” Zelda said. “And I suppose asking to have the pendant returned to us rather than allowing them to store it in the vaults—”

“—That would arouse suspicion, yes.”

Zelda let out a pained sigh. Why did this have to be so difficult? “Alright,” she said, turning back to Lucia. “We’ll see what we can do.”

“You have my gratitude,” the Fae said with a nod.

What else? Zelda shook her head and blinked hard. She continued having trouble focusing. Every time there was a lull in the conversation, it felt like her thoughts were all competing to see which could force their way out first.

“Oh, before I forget—” she said, not wanting an important one to slip by, “Speaking of Fi. She sometimes communicates in a language we don’t understand. I don’t suppose you know what that is? Or, might you be able to translate for us?”

Shesan immediately cut in. “Zelda, Fi isn’t speaking another language. She’s using words for advanced technology and concepts you have no reference for, yet trying to explain them to you anyway in a manner she—as a construct—is unsuited for. If I tried to explain ꡃꡨꡔ꡷ꡩⰡꡌ ꡢꡘꡖꡙꡱꡔꡙ to you, you similarly wouldn’t understand. Those words might as well be nonsense.”

Zelda scrunched up her face. “New-clear… what?”

“Exactly,” Shesan said.

Zelda let out a groan of frustration. “Why can’t you explain them? I’m not an idiot.”

“Far from it,” Shesan said, to Zelda’s surprise. “Would you believe me if I told you that I’m prohibited by oath?”

“Maybe,” Zelda said guardedly. “Are you?”

“Something like that, yes,” Shesan said.

Huh. Okay. That would explain a few things.

Zelda jumped as she felt just as much as she heard a knocking sound echoing from everywhere at once. The featureless landscape and Lucia with it flew away at incredible speed, and a heartbeat later she landed with an uncomfortable jolt back in her chair in her study.

“Zelda? The retinue’s ready to depart.” Laylith, speaking through the cracked-open door.

Zelda rubbed her eyes, adjusting to the light and taking a moment to get her bearings. That jump was one of the most disorienting things she had ever experienced, and as a well-practiced Blue mage, that was saying something.

“Uh… right,” she said, looking back over her shoulder. “How much time do we have?”

“We’ll need to leave within the hour if we’re going to make it to the docks before dusk.”

Zelda turned back around to look at Shesan.

“Thank you Laylith. We’ll be just a moment longer,” Shesan said.

“Yes. What she said,” Zelda echoed lamely.

Laylith appeared for a moment as if she might object, but then nodded and left. Zelda waited for the door to close before turning back around.

“You have more questions for her,” Shesan said, stating the obvious.

“Uh, yeah. Can you blame me?”

Shesan ran her thumb and forefinger over her brows. “I understand. Listen, Zelda: you should know that we’re working on something big. A plan, and a good one. I can’t say more right now—let’s just say it would be more effective to demonstrate it firsthand.”

Zelda crossed her arms. She didn’t like the sound of that, but she supposed it was a step up from what she was used to. “Okay. And when can I expect this demonstration?”

“Soon,” Shesan said.

“When I have the other fragments?” she ventured.

Shesan hesitated.

Mistress, I believe Shesan is intentionally withholding important information from you.

No shit, Fi. Zelda pursed her lips.

Shesan seemed to pick up on her displeasure. “Lucia expects a significant change to your spiritual essence,” she said, giving unusual weight to the words. “After you’ve bonded with the fifth and sixth fragments.”

“That doesn’t answer my question,” Zelda said.

“No, it doesn’t,” Shesan said. “But please believe me—I am trying to explain.” She sighed as she ran her fingers through her short hair. “It’s… difficult. I don’t think you quite understand the magnitude of the powers you’re dealing with. One does not wield the power of gods without being fundamentally altered by the process. The fact that you bond so strongly to Wisdom suggests you resonate well enough with the ideal that you won’t be destroyed by it—which, given everything I know, appears to be by design. Still, you likely aren’t in for a good time.”

Zelda slowly nodded along. “I see. And what does this have to do with your plan?”

“Our plan,” Shesan corrected. “And really, it’s more Lucia’s than anything.”

She wasn’t yet sure if that made it better or worse.

“The same changes… that will allow you more clarity into the nature of your dreams,” Shesan continued, with long pauses mid-thought, “…will also allow Lucia to communicate… concepts to you… more directly. And safely. Safe enough, anyway. At least, that’s her thinking. Do you understand?”
