Zelda’s mind blanked. Intended for… “Fi, you’re going to have to be more specific than that. What do you mean it was intended for you?”

“It is written in a language that only I understand,” she said, floating up to rest horizontally in the air, front facing the ground and eyes on Zelda. “Although I am unable to translate the instructions into your language, I am now able to make use of the item if desired. I must caution you against its use, however; the procedure poses a substantial danger to the health and wellbeing of the participant.”

“Uh, Fi? You’re doing that thing again where you skip over the important parts,” Link said.

“Apologies, Master. What would you like clarified?”

“How about starting with what it does?” Zelda said.

“That is also impossible to accurately translate.”

“Can you try?” Link asked.

“I will attempt to do so,” she said, returning to her neutral pose. “Using the substance contained inside, I will be able to permanently alter the essence of a single individual. Though the nature of the transformation is not describable in your language, I can say that it was an initiation rite of the Lightsworn. It was intended to be performed before undergoing their trials.”

Zelda blinked.

Chalia let out a low whistle.

“Astounding,” Kakaro said.

Vestele held the silvery egg out in front of her with a newfound appreciation. “Just to think, all this time…"

“Fi, when you say it’s dangerous, what exactly do you mean?” Zelda asked.

“There is a greater than twenty-percent probability of suffering permanent spiritual damage, death, or worse.”

“Worse?”

“I’ll do it,” Link said.

The four of them turned to face him in unison.

“What? I suffered through bloodrage and lived,” he said with a shrug. “There’s no way a Lightsworn ritual can be that bad.”

He couldn’t be serious! …Or rather, he was absolutely serious, and therein laid the problem. Zelda supposed it only made sense, considering that a fragment of Courage had found its way to him. “Uhh—you do remember the part where I spent a good chunk of the magic reserves of a literal goddess healing you until I blacked out, right?”

“Yeah, but Hylia was the one who sent you after me in the first place, right? And she had faith in me then. I have to do the same now. The way I see it, if I’m not fit to be a Lightsworn, I’m not fit to be her Champion either.”

“Unbelievable,” Zelda muttered. She looked to Vestele, who only shrugged with a minute shake of her head, as if to say don’t ask me. “Link, listen—”

“—Didn’t you say something to me earlier about finding the ‘edge we needed?’”

Okay. She stepped right into that one. “I’m not going to be able to talk you out of this, am I?”

Link shook his head. “Nope.”

-o-o-o-

The High Priestess passed the silvery egg off to Link and backed away without fuss. It felt cold to his touch, and was a lot lighter than it looked. Sage Kakaro and Sage Chalia followed her example, giving him a wide berth.

Odd. He expected more of an argument. Did they know his secret, or did they just blithely accept that he was insane?

“You’re sure you want to go through with this?” Zelda asked. “Right now? As in, right this very second?”

“It’s as good a time as any, isn’t it?” Link said. “Better here than on the road.”

“You’re a braver lad than I,” Sage Kakaro said in a hushed tone.

“Don’t you think we should at least consider the possibility that—”

“—I’ll be fine,” Link interrupted. “It’s okay. I’ve got this.”

Zelda studied him again with that piercing gaze of hers, her head cocked to the side and with arms crossed. He had to figure out how she did that. He always felt like she could see right through him.

“You’re sure? What if it doesn’t work?”

It was true—he was banking a lot on this, but even if the fragment didn’t save him, he had a feeling he would be okay. Something about the idea of a Lightsworn ritual that blindly killed a fifth of its inductees didn’t sit right with him. How could Hylia allow such a thing? It wasn’t anything like what he expected of the Holy Mother.

No; he was pretty sure this was a test. He couldn’t be certain, but it made a lot more sense when you looked at it from that perspective. No doubt the trials they faced were harrowing. It would only make sense to filter out the halfhearted and self-serving at the first opportunity, wouldn’t it? And if it did pose an actual danger? Well, then he just had to take the risk. The Lightsworn were a force of myth and legend! Hylia’s own order of chosen knights, sworn to defend the realm and rise to her aid when called…

…

He wondered if the allure of it was clouding his judgment. He didn’t believe himself to be a vain man—but then, who did? Link imagined five men under his command considering the same decision. He certainly wouldn’t make the choice for them. But at the same time, if they decided to put their lives at risk, knowing one among them would die? He wouldn’t stop them either. Four Lightsworn versus five regular soldiers, no matter how talented? It wasn’t even a consideration. If it meant risking his life for the good of the kingdom, he had to do it.

“Link?” Zelda said, looking concerned. “Having second thoughts?”

“No,” he said with conviction. “I’m sure. Fi, let’s do this.” He raised the egg up high, clasping it with both hands.

Zelda rubbed her face with her palms. She looked to object again, but then scurried off to stand with the others.

“Master, I must reiterate my concerns as to your safety,” Fi said. “Are you certain you wish to proceed?”

“I am.”

“As you wish.”

He wasn’t sure what he was expecting. A fancy ritual? More swirling magics, followed by instructions from Fi on how to use whatever the egg contained?

It was nothing of the sort.

Without warning, the egg burst. Fragments of jagged, razor-sharp metal flew from his hands and whirled about, embedding themselves into his flesh one at a time. The pain was overwhelming. Link screamed, falling to his knees. At the same time, he choked as a thick vaporous substance forced its way into his mouth. The taste was fouler than anything he could imagine, though it barely registered next to the searing agony that flared up from every fiber of his being. He retched, then convulsed, and fought with all his strength to remain conscious.

And then it was over. He looked up to see Zelda standing over him, motes of green swirling in her hands. He held up a hand and shook his head. She backed away. Voices… there were voices. He couldn’t make out what they were saying, but he knew they had to be worried about him.

“I’m alright,” he managed between breaths. “It’s okay. That wasn’t… so bad.” He smiled and, with some effort, rose to his feet.

What were they staring at?

Dizzy.

Link wobbled, trying to steady himself. His vision faded, and he blacked out.

-o-

A grand palace nestled in the forests at the edge of a river; at its center, a courtyard with a chasm that stretched deep into the abyss. An obsidian spire hidden away in the mountains; the path to it obscured by shifting sands and blistering winds. A vast basin filled with water and teeming with life; an island rested at its center, perpetually clouded by fog and shadow. A great temple built from living wood; trees and brush shaped to grow into a twisting labyrinth of bewildering complexity. A lone mountain in the middle of the ocean; surrounded by a ring of islands, with fire and brimstone erupting from its peak.

-o-

Link stirred awake in the middle of the night in a bed at the infirmary. Here again? They should probably be more careful with these ancient artifacts. But strangely enough, he felt just fine after the ordeal. Great, even. Was he a Lightsworn now? He didn’t feel any different…

Looking around, he noticed two other bodies resting nearby, though Link wasn’t sure who they were. Quietly, he slipped out of bed trying not to wake them, and found his gear tucked away in the corner of the room. He wasn’t sure what time it was, but a walk would be nice to gather his thoughts and reflect on all that had happened.

“Link?” Zelda whispered. “Are you alright?”

“Oh, hey,” he said, turning to see her sitting on the edge of her bed in a silken nightdress. He tried not to dwell on any improper thoughts. “Yeah, I’m fine. Just going out for a walk. Don’t mind me.”

“Whazzit?” came another voice. The other body rustled awake, and Link recognized her as one of Zelda’s Sheikah guard. Koja, wasn’t it?

“It’s nothing,” Zelda said. “Link’s awake. Nothing to worry about—you can go back to sleep.”

“Mmm, kay.”

“You’re sure you’re alright?” she asked. “You’ve been out for three days. We should probably get the nurse to check on you at least.”

Three days? It definitely didn’t feel that long. “No, I’m alright,” he said firmly. “Really. But I’ll go see a healer if it makes you feel better. You should get back to sleep. We can talk in the morning.”

Zelda sat at the edge of her bed in silence. Link wasn’t sure what she was thinking, and couldn’t make out any features of her face in the darkness. “Alright, if you say so,” she said at last, yawning before tucking herself back into bed. “Goodnight.”

“See you later,” he said. He passed through the door, through the halls, and made his way out into the night.
